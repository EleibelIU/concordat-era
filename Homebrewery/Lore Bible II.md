## Goals

By the end of this book, a player should be able to:

1. Name every major faction and articulate why a reasonable person would support or oppose each one.
2. Explain the Force crisis in the terms their character would use.
3. Build a character with specific loyalties, specific enemies, specific fears, and specific hopes.
4. Understand the military, political, and economic landscape well enough to make informed in-character decisions.
5. Feel the setting's weight without being crushed by it: know that the galaxy is broken and believe that their character might be part of what comes next.

# Section A
## The Rakata, the Star Forge, and the Star Maps

### The Infinite Empire

Thirty thousand years before the Concordat Era, the Rakata ruled a galaxy-spanning empire powered by the Force. Not Force philosophy. Not Force sensitivity. Force engineering. The Rakata built machines that drew on the Force as a power source: hyperdrives that navigated through Force currents, terraformers that reshaped planetary ecosystems through Force manipulation, and at the apex of their civilization, the Star Forge.

The Star Forge orbits a star in the Lehon system, deep in the Unknown Regions. It is a factory the size of a small moon. It draws energy from the star it orbits and from the Force itself, converting both into raw material: ships, weapons, droids, armor, anything its operators can conceive. At its height, the Star Forge produced entire fleets in days. It won the Rakata their empire and then consumed them.

The Rakata's collapse followed a pattern that Kreia recognized when she first encountered references to it in pre-Republic archaeological texts: they used the Force as a resource, and the resource ran out. The Star Forge amplified its operators' aggression and channeled dark-side energy back into them, accelerating a feedback loop of conquest, consumption, and corruption. The Rakata's slave species revolted. A plague (possibly engineered by the slaves, possibly a consequence of the Rakata's genetic homogeneity, possibly both) swept through the Rakata population. Their Force sensitivity deteriorated. Without the Force, their technology stopped working. Without their technology, their empire collapsed.

Within a few centuries, the Rakata went from ruling the galaxy to squatting in the ruins of their own monuments, unable to open the doors their ancestors had built.

### Why the Rakata Matter Now

The Rakatan collapse is the historical precedent for the current Force crisis. A civilization that built everything on the Force, lost the Force, and died.

The parallels are not subtle, and Revan was not the first person to notice them. Kreia studied Rakatan ruins decades before Revan found his first Star Map. The Jedi Archives on Ossus contained fragmentary references to the Infinite Empire before they burned in the Great Hyperspace War. Sith scholars on Korriban maintained their own records of Rakatan ruins found in the Stygian Caldera (the nebula surrounding Sith space) and interpreted them through a Sith theological lens: the Rakata were a warning about what happens when a civilization relies on Force power without the Sith discipline to sustain it.

The difference between the Rakatan collapse and the current crisis is speed. The Rakata declined over centuries. The galaxy's current Force deterioration has accelerated catastrophically in the twelve years since the Great Hyperspace War. Something is different this time. Whether that difference is the Sith rituals, the Ashen Campaigns, some deeper structural failure, or something no one has identified yet is the campaign's central mystery.

### The Star Maps

The Rakata built Star Maps as navigational monuments: self-sustaining holographic systems, powered by the Force, that recorded the location of the Star Forge and other key Infinite Empire installations. Five Star Maps were scattered across the galaxy on worlds significant to the Rakata: Dantooine, Kashyyyk, Manaan, Tatooine, and Korriban.

The maps were designed to be self-repairing. When the Rakata destroyed them during their collapse (to prevent their slave species from seizing the Star Forge), the maps slowly reconstructed themselves over millennia, drawing on residual Force energy embedded in their construction sites.

In this AU, the Star Maps have a more complicated history:

##### Dantooine
Intact but partially degraded. Located in Rakatan ruins near the Jedi Enclave. The enclave's Jedi scholars studied it for decades without understanding what it was. When the Mandalorians established their presence on Dantooine, the map fell into a contested zone. Its current status is uncertain: the enclave may have moved it, the Mandalorians may have seized it, or it may still be sitting in the ruins, guarded by nothing but geography and Force-sensitive fauna.

##### Kashyyyk
Destroyed. Revan's forces located the Star Map in the Shadowlands during Year Five of the Mandalorian Wars. After extracting the navigational data, Revan ordered the map demolished to prevent anyone from following his trail. The demolition was thorough. Wookiee elders who witnessed it reported that the ruins "screamed" in the Force when the map was broken. The dead zone around the former map site has not healed.

##### Manaan
Intact and hidden. The Star Map on Manaan is submerged deep beneath Ahto City, in the ocean trenches near the Hrakert Rift, the deep-sea geological formation where kolto is produced. The Selkath know about the Rakatan ruins. They have known for longer than the Republic has existed. The Order of Shasa (the Selkath Force tradition) maintains the site as a sacred place and does not discuss it with outsiders. The connection between the Star Map, the Hrakert Rift, and kolto's Force-resonant properties is not a coincidence, and the Selkath know more about this relationship than they share.

\column

##### Tatooine
Destroyed. Malak's forces located this map during Year Six, acting on intelligence separate from Revan's research. Malak did not destroy the map for the same reasons Revan destroyed the Kashyyyk map. Malak destroyed it because he recognized what the Star Forge represented: an infinite factory powered by the Force. He concluded that in a galaxy where the Force was dying, an engine that consumed the Force to produce weapons was the last thing anyone needed. His destruction of the Tatooine map was a strategic decision. It was also, possibly, the most morally clear decision Malak has ever made.

##### Korriban
Intact but corrupted. The Star Map in the Valley of the Dark Lords has been there since before the Dark Jedi Exiles arrived to found the Sith Empire. It predates everything the Sith built on Korriban. The tomb builders constructed around it. Sith sorcerers drew on its residual Force energy for millennia without understanding its origin. By the Concordat Era, the Korriban map is saturated with dark-side energy accumulated over thousands of years of Sith ritual practice. Whether it still functions as a navigational tool is unclear. What it has become, a Force artifact soaked in millennia of death, is something else entirely.

### The Star Forge: Does It Still Function?

Unknown. Revan believes he located it. His research notes (the portions Kaeranth has been able to interpret) reference "the station" and "the engine" in ways that suggest he found something in the Lehon system. Whether the Star Forge is operational, dormant, or destroyed is a GM-level decision. What matters for the setting book is that the possibility of the Star Forge drives faction behavior:

{{note
- If the Star Forge exists and can be activated, whoever controls it can build an infinite fleet.
- If the Star Forge exists and requires the Force to operate, then in a galaxy where the Force is dying, it may be the thing that finishes the job.
- If Revan found it and it broke him, that is its own kind of answer.
}}

The setting book presents the Star Forge as a legend with teeth. Players will hear about it. Factions will pursue it. Whether it's real, and what it does if activated, belongs to the campaign's arc.

# Section B
## The Deep Sith — Ancient Survivors

### The Exiles and What Came Before

The Sith Empire that fought the Great Hyperspace War was already a hybrid civilization. Seven thousand years before the GHW, renegade Dark Jedi led by Ajunta Pall (exiled after losing the Hundred-Year Darkness) arrived on Korriban and found a Force-sensitive species already living there. The Sith. Not an empire. A people. Red-skinned, sharp-featured, with a caste society built around the Force: Kissai priests who practiced sorcery, Massassi warriors who channeled the Force through combat, Zuguruk engineers who built with it.

The Dark Jedi Exiles conquered the Sith through a combination of Force superiority and technological advantage. Over the next two thousand years, interbreeding (facilitated by Sith alchemy) blurred the line between the two populations. The word "Sith" came to mean both the original species and the hybrid culture that the Exiles built on their backs.

But the original Sith, the people who lived on Korriban before Ajunta Pall arrived, did not vanish into the hybrid population uniformly. Some Kissai lineages maintained what they called *Jen'jidai tsiss*, "blood before the Exiles." These were Pureblood families who could trace their ancestry to pre-Exile Sith, whose Force traditions predated the Dark Jedi by millennia, and who viewed the hybrid Sith Empire as a colonial imposition on their culture rather than a natural evolution.

### The Keepers of Adas

Among the oldest Sith Pureblood traditions is the cult of King Adas: the ancient Sith ruler who united the Sith species and repelled the Rakata when they attempted to conquer Korriban roughly twenty-seven thousand years before the present. Adas predates the Dark Jedi Exiles by twenty thousand years. His legacy is the oldest continuous cultural thread in Sith Pureblood identity, and it has nothing to do with the Jedi, the Republic, or the Dark Jedi who came later.

The Keepers of Adas are not a formal organization in the Concordat Era. They are a cultural memory maintained by elder Kissai in diaspora communities, transmitted through oral tradition, ritual practice, and a small number of Sith-language texts that predate the Exiles' arrival. The Keepers hold that the "true Sith" are the people, not the philosophy. That the Force traditions of the original Sith (which emphasized the relationship between passion, the natural world, and the Force as an elemental power rather than a moral binary) were corrupted by the Dark Jedi Exiles, who imposed their own light-side/dark-side framework onto a tradition that had never needed it.

\column

### Why This Matters for Characters

A Sith Pureblood character in the Concordat Era can draw on multiple identity threads:

##### Imperial Sith
Descended from the hybrid culture. Raised in the traditions of the Sith Empire. May have served in the Sith military during the GHW. Carries the Empire's defeat as a living wound. Views the Republic with suspicion or hatred. May serve the Triumvirate, a remnant warlord, or have integrated into Concordat society while maintaining private loyalties.

##### Diaspora Sith
Born after the GHW's end, raised in exile. May have grown up on Nar Shaddaa, on a Hutt world, in a Republic resettlement camp, or in a Mandalorian-held territory. Identity is shaped by displacement and prejudice. Knows enough about Sith culture to feel its loss. May speak Sith as a heritage language rather than a first language.

##### Keeper-Tradition Sith
Connected to the pre-Exile Pureblood traditions. Views both the Sith Empire and the Republic as colonial structures imposed on the Sith people. Practices Force traditions that predate the dark-side/light-side binary. May see the current Force crisis as the consequence of millennia of abuse by both Jedi and Sith who never understood the Force the way the original Sith did. This character type challenges every faction's assumptions about what "Sith" means.

##### Ancient Survivors
The tombs on Korriban are not all empty. Sith sorcerers of extraordinary power practiced techniques for extending life: stasis fields, Force-sustained hibernation, the transfer of consciousness into artifacts or constructed bodies. The Valley of the Dark Lords contains dozens of sealed tombs, and the Force crisis has destabilized some of those seals.

{{descriptive
Reports from Korriban in the last two years include: tremors in sealed tomb complexes, activation of security systems that have been dormant for centuries, and (in one unverified report from a Czerka archaeological team) a voice speaking Old Sith from behind a wall that the team's sensors said was solid stone.
}}

Whether ancient Sith have awakened, and what they would make of the current galaxy, is a campaign-level question. But the possibility exists, and it gives Sith Pureblood characters a connection to something older than the Empire, older than the Exiles, and potentially older than anyone alive understands.

# Section C
## Kreia's Long Game

### The Architecture

In KOTOR II, Kreia manipulated the Exile (a single individual) as a proof of concept for severing the Force. In this AU, she is playing a longer, wider game. She is manipulating the galaxy.

Her core insight remains the same: the Force is not a gift. It is a dependency that turns every civilization that relies on it into an addict, and every conflict between Force users into an existential threat. The Rakatan collapse proved it. The Great Hyperspace War proved it again. The Ashen Campaigns proved it a third time. The pattern is clear, and Kreia is done watching it repeat.

But severing the Force from the galaxy cannot be accomplished through a single act. It requires preparation. The galaxy must be brought to a point where severance is survivable, where enough of civilization's infrastructure operates without the Force that cutting the connection doesn't kill everyone who depends on it.

This is Kreia's project. She has been working on it for decades.

### The Pieces

##### Piece One: The Doctrine of Necessary Strength
Kreia did not invent it. Mandalore the Vindicated did. But Kreia recognized it as the first credible secular philosophy of Force-independence, and she ensured it spread. Her agents within Mandalorian academic circles (Mandalorians have scholars; most outsiders don't know this) amplified the Doctrine's intellectual framework. She wanted the galaxy to have a functional philosophy of self-reliance in place before she cut the cord. The Mandalorians are doing her ideological groundwork without knowing it.

##### Piece Two: Revan's Research
Kreia pointed Revan at the Star Maps because she needed someone to find the Star Forge and understand the Rakatan collapse. Revan was the sharpest mind she'd ever taught. If he could decode the Rakatan failure and reach the same conclusions she had, she would have an ally. If he reached different conclusions, she would at least have a map of the counterarguments she'd need to defeat. Revan's deterioration was not part of her plan. His obsession with "the pattern beneath the Force" may be closer to Kreia's own thinking than either of them realizes, but his mental instability makes him an unreliable instrument.

\column

##### Piece Three: The Ashen Campaigns
Kreia did not order the Ashen Campaigns. Malak did. But Kreia ensured that the Sith holocron containing the coordinated Force-channeling techniques reached Revan at the moment when the Revanchist war effort was stalling. She did not force anyone to use the techniques. She provided the option and let desperation do the rest. The Ashen Campaigns accelerated the Force's deterioration, which advanced Kreia's timeline by decades. Every dead zone is a place where the Force has already been severed. Every dead zone is a prototype.

##### Piece Four: The Concordat Itself
Kreia did not plan the Concordat, but she recognized its value immediately. A government that combines Mandalorian military self-reliance with Republic administrative infrastructure is a government that could, theoretically, function without the Force. If the Concordat survives long enough to prove that a post-Force civilization is viable, Kreia's argument becomes unanswerable. She has agents ensuring the Concordat remains stable enough to serve as her proof of concept, and unstable enough that no one faction can consolidate power and stop her.

##### Piece Five: The Triumvirate
Nihilus is the scalpel. If Kreia needs to drain the Force from a world or a region, Nihilus can do it. She does not deploy him lightly. Each use risks losing control of him, because Nihilus's hunger is not fully contained by the mask she gave him. But as a weapon of last resort, a way to accelerate Force severance in a specific location, he is irreplaceable.

Sion is the shield. He guards Kreia's physical operations, eliminates threats to her network, and serves as a deterrent. He is also, in Kreia's view, a test case: a being who sustains himself through the dark side without relying on the Force's broader ecosystem. If Sion can survive the Force's death, then other dark-side practitioners can too. If he can't, she needs to know that before she proceeds.

##### Piece Six: The Force-Sensitive Population
The Sentinel Program, the Jedi diaspora, the Sith remnant Force users scattered across the galaxy: these are the people who will suffer most when the Force is severed. Kreia has no plan to save them. She has a plan to ensure their suffering is survivable. Her agents within the Jedi Order quietly promote research into non-Force healing techniques. Her operatives in the Concordat support Force-monitoring programs that (while ostensibly authoritarian) also produce data on how Force-sensitive individuals adapt to dead-zone exposure. Kreia is running experiments through proxies, and the proxies don't know they're her lab assistants.

### What Makes This Different from KOTOR II

In KOTOR II, Kreia's plan hinged on the Exile: a single individual who had been severed from the Force and survived. The Exile was proof that Force severance was survivable. Kreia's endgame was to use the Exile's wound as a template.

In the Concordat AU, Kreia does not have an Exile. She has something larger: a galaxy that is already experiencing Force severance on a massive scale, through the dead zones and the Force crisis. She doesn't need a template. She has an ongoing, real-time experiment with millions of data points.

Her plan is not to sever the Force in one dramatic act. It is to manage the severance that is already happening: accelerate it where useful, study its effects, prepare the galaxy's infrastructure and philosophy for the end state, and ensure that when the Force finally dies (or is killed), civilization survives.

{{note
She is patient. She has been patient for forty years. She can wait a few more.

## The Jedi — Military Structure and the Warlike Transformation

### The Jedi Before the Wars: A Monastic Order With a Problem

Before the Mandalorian Wars, the Jedi Order was not a military organization. It was a monastic educational institution with peacekeeping authority. Jedi served as diplomats, mediators, investigators, and when necessary, combatants, but they did not maintain standing military units, a chain of command, or a strategic doctrine.

The Order's internal organization followed three traditional paths:

**Jedi Guardians** specialized in lightsaber combat and direct action. They served as the Order's martial arm: bodyguards for diplomats, first responders to violent crises, and the closest thing the Order had to soldiers. Guardians were respected but not dominant. The Council historically preferred Consulars and Sentinels for leadership positions.

**Jedi Consulars** specialized in Force study, diplomacy, and negotiation. They managed the Order's relationships with the Republic, with planetary governments, and with non-Jedi Force traditions. Consulars filled most Council seats and dominated the Order's intellectual life.

**Jedi Sentinels** blended Force training with practical non-Force skills: intelligence gathering, technical expertise, medicine, and field survival. They operated in the spaces between Guardian muscle and Consular diplomacy, often working alone or in small teams in the Outer Rim.

The three paths were not rigid castes. A Jedi could train in multiple disciplines, and many did. But the paths shaped how the Order thought about itself: scholars and diplomats at the center, warriors at the perimeter, specialists in between.

### What the War Changed

When Revan walked out of the Temple with four hundred Jedi, the Order's organizational assumptions collapsed overnight.

Revan did not build a monastic community with lightsabers. He built a military. The process of building it transformed the Jedi who joined him.

\column

**The Revanchist Military Structure (3959-3955 BBY)**

Revan adopted Republic military ranks but overlaid them with Jedi operational roles. The result was a hybrid structure that no one was fully comfortable with:

| Rank | Role |
|:-----|:-----|
| Field Marshal Revan | Supreme commander. Strategic direction, left execution to Malak and field commanders. |
| General Malak | Second in command. Operational authority over all ground and fleet combat. |
| Jedi Commanders | Jedi Knights commanding mixed units of Jedi and Republic regulars. ~40 at peak. |
| Jedi Captains | Mid-rank officers commanding company-level units or serving as fleet attachés. |
| Jedi Lieutenants | Junior officers, typically recent Padawans. High casualty rate. High burnout rate. |
| Jedi Specialists | Non-command roles: healers, intelligence analysts, battle meditation practitioners. |
| Republic Line Officers | Non-Jedi military officers serving alongside and under Jedi commanders. |

The most controversial rank was Jedi Commander, because it placed Jedi in direct authority over non-Force-sensitive soldiers. Roughly forty Jedi held Commander rank at the Revanchist peak. Some adapted to military command. Others treated their troops as extensions of their own will, using the Force to coordinate movements in ways that were effective but dehumanizing to the soldiers involved.

### How the Jedi Became Warlike Without a "Fall"

In canon KOTOR, Malachor V serves as the crucible: the Jedi who fight there are exposed to the dark side on a catastrophic scale, and many fall. Without Malachor, this AU needs a different mechanism. The answer is not a single event but a process.

**The instrumentalization of the Force.**

The Jedi who joined the Revanchists did not fall to the dark side. Most of them never touched Sith sorcery, never opened a holocron, never sought forbidden power. What they did was worse, in a sense, because it was rational.

They learned to treat the Force as a military asset.

A Jedi Guardian who uses the Force to enhance her reflexes in combat is using the Force as a personal tool. This is normal. This is what Guardians do.

A Jedi Commander who uses battle meditation to coordinate three hundred soldiers is using the Force as a command-and-control system. This is qualitatively different. The soldiers become extensions of the Commander's will. Their fear, their pain, their individual combat decisions are overridden by the Commander's Force-enhanced coordination. The soldiers survive at higher rates. They also stop being soldiers and become instruments.

A group of sixty Jedi who channel together to produce an Ashen strike are using the Force as a strategic weapon. They are not fighting. They are not defending. They are operating a weapons system that happens to be powered by their bodies and the Force. The distinction between this and Sith ritual warfare is intent, not effect.

{{descriptive
The Jedi who participated in the Ashen Campaigns did not fall. They militarized. They developed a professional competence with violence that is not the dark side but is not the Jedi way either. They follow orders. They calculate acceptable casualties. They speak in military jargon about Force operations the way a Republic artillery officer speaks about turbolaser barrages. They have not lost their connection to the light side of the Force. They have stopped thinking about the Force in terms of light and dark at all. It is a tool. You use it. You maintain it. You deploy it where it produces the best tactical result.
}}

This is the transformation the Council feared, and it is worse than a fall to the dark side, because there is nothing to redeem. A fallen Jedi can be brought back to the light. A militarized Jedi doesn't think they've gone anywhere. They think they've grown up.

\column

### Post-Concordat Jedi Factions

The Jedi Order in the Concordat Era is not one organization. It is at least five, wearing the same robes and disagreeing about everything.

##### 1. The Council Loyalists (~200 Jedi)
Remain on Coruscant and at satellite enclaves (Dantooine, Telos, smaller sites). Follow the Council's directive: study the Force crisis, minimize Force use that might accelerate deterioration, do not take sides in the Concordat political conflict. Led by the surviving Council members: Masters Vrook, Vandar, Zez-Kai Ell, and Lonna Vash.

The Council's position is internally coherent and externally paralytic. They believe the Force is a finite resource being consumed by warfare, and that every act of Force use (especially combat use) contributes to the crisis. Their prescription is restraint, study, and patience.

Their critics (which is everyone) call this cowardice dressed as wisdom. The Council calls it the only responsible position available to people who understand what's at stake.

##### 2. The Remnant Jedi (~200 Jedi)
Serve Revan's government-in-exile. Maintain the position that the Concordat is illegitimate and that the Jedi's duty is to defend the Republic, meaning the true Republic, not Malak's hybrid state. Led in practice by Master Dorak and by a council of senior Jedi Knights who coordinate with Kaeranth's military operations.

The Remnant Jedi have adopted the Revanchist military structure and continue to use Jedi Commander and Jedi Captain ranks. They see themselves as the Jedi Order's martial tradition preserved and applied. The Council sees them as the militarization problem made permanent.

##### 3. The Concordat Advisors (~80 Jedi)
Serve Malak's government in various capacities: military advisors, healers in Concordat medical facilities, administrators in post-war reconstruction. Most did not join Malak out of enthusiasm. They joined because the Council offered no path to meaningful action and the Remnant offered only guerrilla warfare.

These Jedi exist in a difficult position. Malak's Force-monitoring programs treat them as simultaneously useful and suspect. They advise a government that increasingly restricts Force-user autonomy. Some have rationalized this: Malak provides order, and order is a precondition for healing the Force. Some are quietly miserable. A few are feeding information to the Remnant or the Council.

##### 4. The Sentinel Program Operatives (~300, not all fully Jedi-trained)
Atris's creation, Kavar's responsibility. These are Force-sensitive individuals trained in accelerated courses to serve as field operatives. Many are not traditional Jedi at all: they were recruited outside normal channels, from populations the Order had overlooked or rejected. A significant number are non-human species, including some Sith Purebloods (which created enormous controversy within the Council when it became known).

The Sentinels are the most diverse and least doctrinally coherent Jedi-adjacent group. Some have absorbed Jedi philosophy. Some have absorbed the military pragmatism of their trainers. Some have absorbed nothing but technique and are essentially Force-capable mercenaries with Council authorization.

##### 5. The Independents (~several dozen confirmed, unknown total)
Jedi who have left the Order entirely. Some renounced their vows publicly. Some simply stopped reporting. Some disappeared into civilian life on frontier worlds. Some became bounty hunters, merchants, teachers, or farmers. A few have been recruited by Kreia's network without knowing who they're working for.

The Independents are not a faction. They are an absence: the Jedi the Order failed to hold. Each Independent's reasons for leaving are personal, and for player-character purposes, those reasons are some of the most fertile territory in the setting.

### Atris and the Handmaidens

Atris occupies a unique position: technically still a Council member (she has not been formally expelled), practically independent, and philosophically drifting toward something that is neither Jedi orthodoxy nor Sith heterodoxy.

\column

Her Echani Handmaidens are a private force of approximately thirty warriors trained to fight and subdue Force users. Their combat methodology draws on Echani tradition (where physical movement is communication and expression) and on Atris's research into Force-suppression techniques. They are, in effect, an anti-Jedi strike team created by a Jedi.

The Council tolerates this because Atris frames the Handmaidens as a "contingency": a safeguard against the possibility that militarized Jedi or rogue Force users become too dangerous for conventional forces to handle. The Council tolerates it also because Atris is frightening to argue with.

The Handmaidens themselves are loyal to Atris with an intensity that concerns outside observers. Atris does not abuse this loyalty, but she relies on it in ways that a healthier leader would question.

# Section E
## Tone Calibration

### The Rule

This setting is grimdark in its history and materially honest in its present. It is not nihilistic. The difference matters.

**Grimdark means:** The institutions are broken. The leaders are compromised. The wars produced atrocities that no faction can justify and every faction tries to. The Force is dying and no one knows how to stop it. Power corrupts. Idealism gets people killed. The galaxy is run by exhausted pragmatists, traumatized veterans, and true believers whose faith makes them dangerous.

People still fall in love. Parents still protect their children. A Mandalorian warrior teaches a refugee kid to fight and doesn't ask for anything in return. A Jedi healer works in a Concordat clinic knowing the government monitors her and heals people anyway. A Sith Pureblood engineer on Nar Shaddaa builds water purifiers for a slum community because someone has to. A Republic soldier who served in the Ashen Campaigns adopts a war orphan from a world his unit destroyed.

The setting's horror gives the heroism weight. The heroism gives the horror meaning. Without the darkness, courage is trivial. Without the courage, darkness is a parody.

### Writing Guidelines for Tone

When drafting the setting book:

**History is honest.** The Mandalorian Wars, the Ashen Campaigns, Cathar, Serroco: describe them with unflinching specificity. Do not sanitize. Do not moralize. Let the reader feel the weight and draw their own conclusions.

**The present is contested.** The current moment is not hopeless. It is volatile. Things could get better or worse depending on what people do. Every chapter about the present should contain at least one thread of genuine possibility: a reform movement, an act of courage, a community that works despite everything.

**Characters are complicated.** Malak is building something monstrous and something functional simultaneously. Revan is losing his mind and may be the only person who understands what's happening to the Force. Canderous is fighting for a beautiful idea that is killing his people. Kreia may be right about everything except the part that matters most. Write every major figure as someone a player could admire and fear in the same conversation.

**Small moments carry the weight.** The setting book should include moments of ordinary human (and alien) dignity alongside the political and military material. A kolto medic sharing her rations with a patient. A Mandalorian father singing his daughter to sleep in armor he hasn't taken off in three days. A Jedi Sentinel sitting alone in a cantina, trying to remember what it felt like when the Force worked. These moments are not softening. They are the point.

**The Force crisis is grief.** For Force-sensitive characters, the dying Force is not an abstract strategic problem. It is like losing a sense. Like going deaf in a world built for music. Write it that way. The political and military consequences of the Force crisis matter, but the personal devastation is what makes it real.

# Section F
## Campaign Framing

### The Actual Play Emphasis

The first Concordat Era actual play (the podcast Elizabeth will host and cast) will emphasize political intrigue, military operations, and space opera. The setting book should present all four major campaign types (political, military, Force mystery, frontier survival) as equally viable, but its framing energy should lean toward the first three.

In practice, this means:

##### Faction politics get the most page space 
The Concordat, the Remnant, the Neo-Mandalorians, and the Triumvirate should each feel like organizations a player character could serve, oppose, infiltrate, or reshape. Their internal contradictions should generate plot hooks on every page.

##### Military material is grounded and specific 
Fleet sizes, supply logistics, the security gradient, garrison life, mercenary culture: all of this should feel real enough that a player running a military character has material to work with. Not a wargaming supplement, but enough texture that "my character is a Concordat naval officer" or "my character deserted from the Remnant" comes with specific, playable context.

##### Space opera tone in the present tense
The setting's present should feel like a galaxy where things happen at opera scale: grand betrayals, impossible alliances, desperate missions behind enemy lines, duels that decide the fate of systems. The grimdark history provides the foundation; the present-tense writing should have the energy and forward momentum of a story that hasn't been decided yet.

##### The Force is "Mythical"
The Force crisis is the setting's spine, but it should function as an ongoing background mystery rather than the primary focus of every chapter. It comes up everywhere, in medicine, in politics, in daily life, but the book doesn't dwell on it. It haunts. It's the question no one can answer, present in every conversation about the future.
