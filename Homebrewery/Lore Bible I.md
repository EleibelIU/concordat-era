# Section 1: Calendar


The book uses standard BBY (Before the Battle of Yavin) dating for absolute chronology, consistent with KOTOR-era material. The Concordat government maintains its own internal calendar measured from the signing of the Concordat (1 CE, Concordat Era), which the Revanchist Remnant refuses to recognize.

In-universe, the calendar is political. Using Concordat Era dating signals loyalty to Malak's government. The Remnant uses the old Republic Standard Calendar. The Neo-Mandalorians use their own reckoning based on Mandalore cycles. This is deliberate: how a character dates a letter tells you who they answer to.

# Section 2: The Great Hyperspace War

The Great Hyperspace War in this AU is not the distant ancient history it is in standard canon. It ended twelve years before the campaign starts. The veterans are middle-aged. The orphans are young adults. The trauma is living memory.



The Sith Empire under Naga Sadow launched an invasion of the Republic that came closer to succeeding than any official history admits. Sith battle fleets struck the Core Worlds. Sith sorcerers performed ritual warfare on a scale the galaxy had never seen: mass sacrifice, reality distortion, Force-severing techniques that could strip Jedi of their abilities mid-battle.

On **Ossus**, the Jedi Archives burned. Ten thousand Jedi fought the Sith war machine, and when the massed rituals peaked, the defenders felt something unprecedented: silence. Not the quiet of a calm mind, but the silence of a severed connection. For minutes that stretched like hours, the Force vanished from Ossus. Jedi who had relied on precognition for decades suddenly fought blind. Jedi healers watched patients die under their hands. Some Jedi simply collapsed, unable to process the absence.

The Republic won because the Sith rituals were unstable. The sorcery that severed Jedi also consumed Sith practitioners. Naga Sadow's forces imploded as their own weapons devoured them. The Republic counter-invasion shattered what remained of the Sith Empire and drove the survivors into the Unknown Regions and the fringes of Wild Space.

## What It Cost

The victory was pyrrhic. Trade routes were severed. Entire sectors were depopulated. The Core Worlds bore bombardment scars that would take generations to repair. And the Jedi whispered about something worse: the Force itself was damaged. Not just on Ossus, but spreading. Places where Sith rituals had been performed became dead zones: regions where the Force was absent or distorted. Healers found their abilities unreliable. Seers reported fragmented visions. The connections that Jedi had relied on for millennia were fraying.

The Republic needed resources to rebuild. Cortosis for hull plating. Durasteel for infrastructure. Kolto for the millions of wounded. Fuel for the shattered fleet. Food for the displaced.

The richest resource worlds in the Outer Rim belonged to the Mandalorian clans.

# Section 3: The Mandalorian Wars

## The Doctrine of Necessary Strength (3963 BBY)

Three months after the Treaty of Coruscant ended the Great Hyperspace War, Mandalore the Vindicated called every major clan to Dxun. Ordo, Vizla, Skirata, Cadera, Fett, Kryze. They expected a war cry.

He showed them a Jedi dying.

{{descriptive
The hologram was a battlefield recording from Ossus. A Twi'lek Jedi Master named Rell Odar, one of the finest duelists of his generation, fighting Sith warriors with extraordinary skill. Then the Sith ritual hit. His lightsaber flickered. His enhanced speed vanished. His precognition evaporated. He stood there, confused, and blaster bolts cut him down.

Mandalore replayed it three times.
}}

The argument: for ten thousand years, the galaxy had built its civilization on the assumption that the Force existed and that Force users would protect it. The Sith had just demonstrated that assumption could be destroyed. The Jedi Council debated in their temples while people starved. The Republic Senate issued decrees that no fleet could enforce. The galaxy was fragile, dependent, and one catastrophe away from collapse.

The Mandalorians, who had never relied on the Force, who fought with iron and will and discipline, were the only people who could teach the galaxy to survive without supernatural crutches.

Jaden the Elder of Clan Skirata gave it language: *Strength is not given. Strength is forged. Those who rely on gifts from the cosmos will be weak when those gifts are taken away. Only those who forge their own strength can survive in a galaxy without mercy.*

The Doctrine of Necessary Strength was not a declaration of war. It was a philosophy of intervention. The Mandalorians would demonstrate self-reliance by testing the galaxy's ability to defend itself, and offer their methods to those who proved willing to learn.

The clans adopted it within days. Not unanimously. Clan Kryze argued that demonstration could be achieved through diplomacy rather than siege. Cassus Fett of Clan Fett saw an opportunity for territorial expansion dressed in ideological clothing and said so openly, earning both contempt and respect for his honesty. But the core held: the Mandalorians would move outward, and the galaxy would learn or fall.

## The Mandalorian Expansion: Years One Through Three (3962-3960 BBY)

#### The Siege of Taris (3962 BBY)

**Taris** was the template. The Mandalorians surrounded the ecumenopolis, issued a challenge -- prove you can defend yourselves without the Republic -- and waited. For four months they held the perimeter while Coruscant promised relief that never came.

When the Tarisian militia tried to break the siege, the Mandalorians crushed them in six hours. Then they did something no one expected: they offered the survivors training. A third accepted. When those soldiers returned weeks later, they were transformed. Disciplined, effective, dangerous. They still lost the rematch, but they'd earned something the Republic had never given them: Mandalorian respect.

Taris surrendered not because it was conquered but because the Mandalorians offered something the Republic couldn't: protection that actually arrived. In exchange, Taris provided resources and a commitment to self-defense infrastructure.

Two-thirds of Taris accepted the arrangement. The Undercity -- the lowest levels, home to the displaced and the desperate -- accepted almost unanimously. For people the Republic had already abandoned, Mandalorian pragmatism was an improvement.

The upper city resisted. Tarisian nobles who'd relied on Republic trade agreements and Senate connections found themselves irrelevant. Some fled to Coruscant. Some hired mercenaries. Some adapted.

#### The Onderon Campaign (3961 BBY)

**Onderon** was the proof of concept. The beast-riders of Onderon fought the Mandalorian advance with a ferocity that earned genuine admiration. Clan Ordo bore the brunt: Canderous Ordo, young and aggressive, led the assault on the Onderon jungle and lost thirty warriors in the first week to beast-rider ambushes. He called it the best fighting he'd ever seen.

Half of Onderon earned alliance through combat rather than subjugation. The beast-riders became Mandalorian auxiliaries, keeping their traditions and customs while integrating into the clan military structure. Queen Talia of Onderon was permitted to maintain her throne on the condition that Onderon raised a standing militia to Mandalorian standards.

The arrangement worked. Onderon became the model for what the Mandalorians called *aliit'alor* -- honoured integration. A conquered world that fought well was not subjugated but adopted.

#### Dantooine (3961 BBY)

**Dantooine** fell without a shot. The Mandalorians established a presence on the plains outside the Jedi Enclave and waited. Colonists came to them. Young farmers who looked at the enclave -- where Jedi Masters debated whether using the Force was accelerating its death -- and at the Mandalorian camp -- where instructors taught blade combat and survival skills to anyone willing -- and made a pragmatic choice.

The Jedi Enclave Council protested. The Mandalorians invited them to stop the migration by offering something better. The Council debated this for three weeks. By then, half the settlement had relocated.

#### Serroco (3960 BBY)

**Serroco** traded cortosis mining access for defense. Pure pragmatism. The native Stereb population had been caught between Republic mining corporations (primarily Czerka) and Mandalorian expansion. They chose the side that would actually garrison their cities rather than extract their resources from orbit.

The Serroco Resource Pact became a model for economic integration. Mandalorian clans provided military infrastructure. Serroco provided cortosis, fuel, and food. The arrangement was efficient, functional, and exploitative in ways neither side acknowledged openly.

#### The Administrative Crisis

By Year Three, the Mandalorians held eighty systems and couldn't run any of them. Taris experienced food distribution failures within months. Trade networks collapsed under Mandalorian military administration. Their warrior culture had no framework for civilian governance, economic management, or bureaucratic coordination.

Cassus Fett saw this first. A cold-eyed logistician who'd always been more comfortable with spreadsheets than with honor codes, Fett argued that the Mandalorians needed to recruit administrators -- non-warriors who could manage grain distribution and taxation. The traditionalists called this weakness. Fett called it mathematics.

Veera Ordo, Canderous's younger sister, supported Fett. She'd been arguing for administrative partnership since before the expansion. She was also the first prominent Mandalorian to suggest that the Doctrine of Necessary Strength might apply to governance as well as warfare: if the Mandalorians couldn't govern, they should learn, not pretend the problem didn't exist.

The Republic, meanwhile, did nothing. The Senate debated jurisdiction while worlds fell. The Jedi Council argued about whether the Force was too damaged to risk using in combat, whether intervening would make the fractures worse, whether intervention was even possible when half their combat-trained Knights could no longer trust their precognition.


## Year Four: Cathar and the Turning (3959 BBY)

#### The Razing of Cathar

The Cathar refused the Mandalorian offer. Not with negotiation or with the organized militia resistance Taris had attempted. The Cathar fought a guerrilla campaign -- brutal, decentralized, and devastating to Mandalorian supply lines. They used their homeworld's dense forests and canyon systems to ambush Mandalorian patrols. They poisoned water supplies. They targeted officers.

The Mandalorian response escalated over months. What began as a standard pacification became a systematic campaign against the civilian infrastructure that supported the guerrillas. Mandalorian doctrine held that a people who refused to fight honorably -- in open combat, with identifiable combatants -- forfeited the protections of honorable war. The Cathar fought as insurgents, and the Mandalorians treated them as insurgents.

Cassus Fett commanded the Cathar operation. His methods were efficient and monstrous. He separated Cathar populations from their forest strongholds through controlled burning. He established concentration zones where civilians were held while Mandalorian forces swept the guerrilla networks. He did not order the massacre that followed.

What happened at the Cathar Concentration at Ves'kaan was a breakdown of discipline, not a strategy. Mandalorian forces under Clan Jendri, enraged after three months of insurgent ambushes that had killed their chieftain's children, entered the Ves'kaan zone and killed approximately fourteen thousand Cathar civilians over two days. Fett's official report classified it as "collateral pacification." He did not punish the perpetrators. He did not condemn the action. He noted in his private correspondence to Mandalore that "the Cathar problem is resolved" and moved to the next system.

The galaxy learned about Ves'kaan from refugees who escaped through the forest. The holonet footage -- recorded by a Cathar teenager named Sylvar Juhani with a stolen Mandalorian recorder -- showed burning villages, mass graves, and Mandalorian warriors firing into crowds. It was the first time most Republic citizens saw what the Expansion looked like when a world refused to cooperate.


#### The Revanchists Form

Revan watched the Cathar footage in the Coruscant Temple. He'd been arguing with the Council for a year. The footage ended the argument.

He walked out of the Temple with four hundred Jedi behind him. Not all of them had seen the footage. Some followed because they believed the Council's inaction was a betrayal of Jedi principles. Some followed because they were young and angry and Revan was the most charismatic person they'd ever met. Some followed because they'd been watching the Force deteriorate and believed that passivity was killing them faster than war would.

Revan's first act was to address his followers in the plaza outside the Temple. He did not give a rousing speech. He said: "The Council believes that inaction will preserve the Force. I believe that the Force is dying because we have allowed suffering we could have prevented. Both positions may be wrong. But only one of them requires us to do nothing while fourteen thousand people are buried in mass graves."

Four hundred Jedi became the Revanchists. Within three months, Republic military units joined them -- volunteers, defectors from garrisons that had been ordered to stand down, fleet officers who'd been filing protest reports that disappeared into Senate committees.

#### Serroco Atrocity (3958 BBY)

The Mandalorians demonstrated that Cathar was not an anomaly. When the native Stereb population of Serroco began aiding Revanchist intelligence operations, the Mandalorian garrison commander -- acting on standing orders to maintain security at any cost -- ordered the orbital bombardment of Serroco's native cities. Not the Republic mining installations. Not the military facilities. The Stereb population centers.

Twelve cities burned. The cortosis mines were untouched.

The message was legible from orbit: the infrastructure matters. The people are replaceable.

**Serroco** radicalized the Revanchists in ways that Cathar had not. Cathar was a massacre born of lost discipline. Serroco was a calculated strategic decision. Malak, who had been managing logistics and troop movements while Revan planned grand strategy, looked at Serroco and began thinking about how wars end. Not how you win battles, but how you make wars stop.

## The Revanchist War: Years Four Through Six (3959-3957 BBY)

#### Revan and Malak: The Division

The war revealed what Revan and Malak actually were. Revan was a philosopher-general who understood war as a problem of knowledge. He identified which planets mattered, how to frame the conflict for public support, where to apply pressure to fracture Mandalorian alliances. His strategic plans were elegant, ambitious, and frequently disconnected from the logistical reality of feeding an army in contested space.

Malak made the plans work. He understood supply lines, troop movements, attrition rates, and the brutal mathematics of a war fought across hundreds of systems. When historians later reviewed Revanchist victories, they found Revan's name on the strategic directives and Malak's signature on the tactical execution orders. Revan would identify that Jebble needed to fall. Malak would figure out how to take it with acceptable losses. Revan inspired the troops. Malak kept them alive.

The Battle of Jebble (3958 BBY) was the template. Revan's intelligence network identified Jebble as a Mandalorian supply nexus. His plan called for a diversionary attack on three neighboring systems to draw Mandalorian reserves, followed by a precision strike on Jebble's orbital defenses. Malak executed the plan, adapted it twice when intelligence proved faulty, and took the system in nine days with a casualty rate of twelve percent. Revan called it a vindication of Revanchist strategy. Malak called it expensive.

#### Revan's Obsession

Revan became fascinated with the Mandalorian philosophy. Not because he agreed with it, but because the Doctrine of Necessary Strength represented something the Jedi had never confronted: a coherent moral framework that rejected the Force as a foundation for civilization. He spent increasing time in captured Mandalorian archives, studying their texts, tracing the intellectual lineage from ancient Taung warrior-poetry to Mandalore the Vindicated's modern doctrine.

Then he found references to the Star Forge.

The trail began in Mandalorian records of pre-expansion scouting expeditions into the Unknown Regions. Mandalorian scouts had discovered ruins on several worlds -- structures too old to be Republic, too sophisticated to be Mandalorian, built by a species that had mastered technology the galaxy couldn't replicate. The Rakata. An ancient species that had built an empire powered by the Force itself, and whose technology might hold the key to understanding why the Force was dying.


Revan became convinced that understanding the Rakata -- their technology, their relationship to the Force, why their civilization collapsed -- was the only path to stopping the Force's deterioration. He was not wrong about the connection. He was wrong about everything else.

By late 3958, Revan was spending more time in research than in command meetings. He chased Star Map fragments across the Outer Rim. He acquired Sith holocrons from black-market dealers and from Korriban tomb raiders. He cross-referenced Rakatan architectural signatures with Sith historical records. His personal journal from this period, later recovered by Kaeranth, reads like the work of a man who has found a thread in a tapestry and believes that if he pulls hard enough, the entire design will reveal itself.

Malak watched his commander disappear into research while the war ground on. At first he justified it: Revan was pursuing force multipliers, anything that could end the conflict decisively. But Revan stopped attending operational briefings. He stopped reading casualty reports. He talked about infinity engines and dark-side reactors while Malak talked about supply shortages and desertion rates.

They were fighting different wars.

#### The Woman in the Archive

What Revan did not know, and what Malak did not discover until much later, was that the trail of breadcrumbs leading to the Star Forge had been laid deliberately.

Kreia had been Revan's first teacher at the Coruscant Temple. Not his primary instructor -- that was Master Zhar -- but the historian who supervised his early research into Force philosophy. She recognized in the young Revan something she had spent decades looking for: a mind that asked *why* the Force existed, not merely *how* to use it.

When Revan left the Temple to fight the Mandalorians, Kreia was already pursuing her own research into the Force's deterioration. She had reached a conclusion that the Jedi Council would have called heresy: the Force was not simply damaged by the Sith rituals. The rituals had revealed a pre-existing vulnerability. The Force had always been fragile. The galaxy had simply never stressed it enough to notice.

Kreia did not point Revan at the Star Forge out of malice. She pointed him at the Rakata because the Rakatan collapse -- an entire civilization destroyed when its relationship with the Force broke down -- was the historical precedent she needed Revan to find. If Revan understood what had killed the Rakata, he would understand what was killing the Force now. And if he understood that, he might reach the same conclusion Kreia had already reached.

She was patient. She had been patient for a long time.



## The Ashen Campaigns: Years Six and Seven (3957-3956 BBY)

This is the Concordat Era's answer to Malachor V. Not a single battle. Not a single weapon. A doctrine.

#### The Escalation

By Year Six, the Revanchist war effort was stalling. The Mandalorians recruited from every conquered world, turning former Republic citizens into effective soldiers within weeks. Force abilities were becoming unreliable: Malak had felt his own powers flicker in combat, had seen dead zones where Jedi abilities stopped working mid-engagement. The Revanchists were fighting with conventional forces supplemented by increasingly unpredictable Force users.

Malak commissioned a study of Revanchist Force-user effectiveness. The results were classified. The summary: Force-augmented combat units had suffered a thirty-one percent decline in operational reliability over two years. Precognition failures had caused friendly-fire incidents. Healing failures had increased field mortality. The Force was not a force multiplier anymore. It was a liability that occasionally worked.

Revan, from whatever research station he was operating from, sent Malak a package. Inside were the decoded techniques from a Sith holocron -- not a weapon, but a methodology. The ancient Sith had understood how to channel the Force through multiple practitioners simultaneously, creating effects that no individual user could

\page
{{Ch1,tab}}

achieve. The holocron described how the Sith had used massed ritual to crack Jedi defenses during the Great Hyperspace War.

Revan's attached note read: *They used this to destroy. We can use it to win. The difference is intent.*

Malak looked at the methodology and saw something Revan hadn't: scale. If you could coordinate Force users to amplify each other's abilities, you could compensate for individual unreliability through sheer numbers. Twenty unreliable Force users channeling in concert might produce one reliable result.

He was right. The first coordinated Force operation -- the Battle of Omonoth -- used thirty-seven Revanchist Jedi channeling together to generate a telekinetic shockwave that disabled a Mandalorian orbital defense platform. It worked. The Jedi involved reported headaches, nosebleeds, and a "hollow" sensation in the Force that lasted for days.

The second operation used the technique offensively on a planetary surface. The third cratered a mountain.

By the fifth, the Revanchists had developed a tactical doctrine around coordinated Force strikes: *Ashfall Operations*, named for the particulate debris that choked the atmosphere after a strike. The Jedi who participated called them Ashen Campaigns. The name stuck.


#### What the Ashen Campaigns Did

The Ashen Campaigns were effective. They broke Mandalorian defensive positions that conventional forces couldn't crack. They terrorized Mandalorian garrisons. They turned the tide of the war in sectors where the Revanchists had been losing for years.

They also tore wounds in the Force that have never healed.

Each coordinated Force strike consumed something. Not just the energy of the practitioners, but something in the fabric of the Force itself. The Jedi who participated described it as spending currency that couldn't be replenished. After an Ashen strike, the affected area became a dead zone -- a region where the Force was absent or distorted, where Force-sensitive individuals felt nauseous or disoriented, where the connection between living things and the Force was severed.

The first dead zones were small. A battlefield. A crater. A few square kilometers. By the end of the Ashen Campaigns, entire regions of space were compromised. The Burning of **Duro** -- an Ashen strike aimed at a Mandalorian shipyard in orbit -- created a dead zone that encompassed the planet's upper atmosphere. Duro's surface, already polluted by centuries of industry, became something worse: a world where the Force was gone, where Force-sensitive refugees reported constant pain, where the ecosystem began dying in ways that biologists couldn't explain because the mechanisms were metaphysical.

Forty-one confirmed Ashen operations were conducted over fourteen months. The Revanchists won thirty-four of the associated engagements. Twelve planets sustained permanent Force damage. An estimated six hundred thousand civilians died in the operations or their immediate aftermath. An unknown number of Force-sensitive individuals were killed, driven mad, or permanently severed from the Force by proximity to the strikes.

Malak authorized every operation. He read the damage reports. He signed the next order.

His reasoning was consistent: the Mandalorians would not stop. The Republic could not sustain a conventional war. The Force was dying anyway. If using it as a weapon accelerated the death of something that was already dying, the calculation was acceptable. Every Ashen strike shortened the war. Every shortened week saved conventional casualties that would have been spent in grinding attrition.

The mathematics were defensible. The mathematics were monstrous. Malak knew both things simultaneously and did not stop.


#### The Second Siege of Onderon (3956 BBY)

The Ashen Campaigns reached their apex and their moral nadir at **Onderon**. The beast-riders, who had been Mandalorian allies since Year Two, had fortified Onderon into a Mandalorian stronghold. Conventional assault would have cost fifty thousand Revanchist lives.

Malak ordered a full Ashen strike on the Onderon jungle. Sixty Jedi channeled together. The strike worked: it shattered the Mandalorian defensive perimeter and created a corridor for ground assault. It also killed the jungle. Thousands of square kilometers of forest died in minutes as the Force was ripped out of the ecosystem. The beasts that the riders had bonded with -- creatures connected to their riders through the Force -- went mad, turning on their riders, on civilians, on each other.

The beast-riders, who had earned Mandalorian respect through honorable combat, died screaming alongside their animals in a forest that was consuming itself.

Revanchist ground forces entered the dead zone and took Onderon in three days. Fourteen Jedi who participated in the strike were hospitalized. Two never recovered their Force sensitivity. One, a Miraluka named Veld Arannis, lost her Force sight permanently and spent the rest of the war blind.

Onderon became the word that ended arguments. When someone questioned the cost of the Ashen Campaigns, proponents said "Onderon" and meant victory. Opponents said "Onderon" and meant atrocity. Both were right.

#### Who Broke First

The Mandalorians did not break because of the Ashen Campaigns. They broke because Malak understood something about Mandalorian culture that Revan never had: the Mandalorians did not fear death, but they feared irrelevance.

The Ashen Campaigns demonstrated that the Force -- the thing the Doctrine of Necessary Strength had dismissed as a crutch -- could be used to annihilate Mandalorian forces with an efficiency that training and discipline could not match. The Mandalorians had built their philosophy on the premise that skill and will were superior to supernatural power. The Ashen Campaigns proved that premise wrong. Not through individual Jedi heroism, but through industrialized Force application -- the exact kind of systematic strength the Mandalorians respected.

Malak was not just winning battles. He was defeating the Doctrine of Necessary Strength on its own terms. And that was something the Mandalorians could not survive.

## Year Seven: The Dhas'kara (3955 BBY)

Malak had spent two years studying Mandalorian culture while commanding the Revanchist military. He learned their honor codes, their customs, their respect for strength demonstrated through personal combat. He learned about Dhas'kara -- single combat challenges that could resolve disputes between clans, even challenges to Mandalore himself. 

\page

{{Ch1,tab}}

He understood the Right of Challenge: anyone could challenge Mandalorian authority by proving superior individual strength.

He did not tell Revan. Revan was deep in the Unknown Regions by then, chasing Star Map fragments, sending intermittent transmissions about Rakatan infinity engines. Their last substantive conversation had been six months prior.

Malak sent a transmission to Mandalore the Vindicated. He invoked Dhas'kara. If Malak won, Mandalore would hear his terms. If Mandalore won, the Revanchists would surrender. The Mandalorian clans were stunned. No outsider had ever invoked the sacred challenge. Under their own laws, Mandalore could not refuse without destroying his authority.

They fought in orbit above Dxun for four hours. No Force powers. Malak refused to use them. He insisted on meeting the Mandalorian standard: iron and will.

Mandalore the Vindicated shattered Malak's jaw with a beskad strike and severed his sword arm. Malak's blade found Mandalore's throat first.

Victory. By rights, Malak could demand anything. He demanded partnership.

#### The Concordat

Malak stood over Mandalore with blood pouring from where his jaw had been, his severed arm cauterized by emergency systems, and offered terms.

The Concordat: Mandalorian clans provide military strength and maintain their warrior culture. Republic systems provide economic administration and civilian governance. Resources flow to where they're needed. Defense goes to worlds that maintain militias and contribute to collective security. No more Senate debates while worlds burn. No more Mandalorian warriors managing grain distribution.

Mandalore, alive through Malak's mercy and bound by Ka'halar -- in Mandalorian tradition, the life-debt owed by a defeated opponent who is spared by his victor, a bond as binding as blood oath -- accepted.

Not all the clans followed. Canderous Ordo and the traditionalists saw it as surrender dressed in alliance and walked. They formed the Neo-Mandalorian Coalition: a rejection of both the Concordat and the Remnant, committed to pure Mandalorian independence through honorable combat.

The pragmatists joined immediately. Cassus Fett's faction, calculating as always, saw the economic logic and moved first. Much of Clan Vizla followed, as did Veera Ordo, who had been arguing for administrative partnership for years and finally had an ally with the authority to implement it.

# Section 4: The Concordat Government

## The Chancellor-General

The Republic Senate ratified the Concordat by the narrowest margin in its history. Malak was appointed Supreme Chancellor-General -- a new position combining civil and military authority. Constitutional scholars protested. Military pragmatists celebrated. Most citizens were too exhausted to care.

Malak's model was not tyranny. It was the Consulate: a government that maintained democratic forms while concentrating executive authority in a leader who could act. The Senate continued to meet. Senators continued to debate. Legislation continued to pass. But the Chancellor-General held emergency powers that had no expiration date, military authority that superseded civilian jurisdiction in "contested territories" (a category that Malak's office defined), and the power to appoint military governors to systems deemed "insufficiently stabilized."

In practice, the Concordat functions as a military-administrative hybrid. The Core Worlds operate under something resembling normal Republic governance, with senators, courts, and civil services. The Mid Rim is governed by a patchwork of military governors, Mandalorian clan authorities, and surviving Republic administrators. The Outer Rim territories under Concordat control are garrison states, with Mandalorian forces providing security and Republic-trained bureaucrats managing resource extraction and distribution.

## Malak the Man

Malak wears prosthetics for his jaw and arm. In Mandalorian culture, prosthetics mark a warrior who paid the price for victory. In Republic culture, they mark a man who is no longer whole. Malak exists in both readings simultaneously, and the ambiguity is useful.

He governs from Coruscant, from the same Senate chambers where Republic Chancellors governed before him. He holds audiences. He meets with senators. He reviews military reports. He sleeps four hours a night and drinks caf that his aide, Lieutenant Commander Vynn Telos, describes as "weaponized."

He is not a monster. He is a man who made a series of decisions that each seemed necessary at the time and that, taken together, have built something that looks increasingly like an authoritarian state. He monitored Force-sensitive individuals because unreliable Force users were a genuine security risk. He expanded military governor authority because contested territories needed rapid decision-making. He curtailed press freedoms because wartime propaganda was destabilizing newly integrated Mandalorian worlds.

Each decision had a defensible justification. Each decision made the next one easier.

\column

Malak is aware of what he is becoming. He has private conversations -- recorded in journals that are not public -- where he asks himself whether the Concordat is a transitional government or a permanent one. He does not have an answer. The galaxy is not stable enough to test the question.

## Internal Factions Within the Concordat

**The Integrationists** follow Veera Ordo and elements of Cassus Fett's faction. They believe the Concordat should evolve into a genuine hybrid government with permanent Mandalorian representation in Republic institutions. They push for cultural exchange programs, joint military academies, and the legal recognition of Mandalorian customs within Republic law.

**The Restorationists** are former Republic senators and administrators who accepted the Concordat as a wartime necessity and want to restore full democratic governance as soon as the security situation permits. They support Malak's authority reluctantly and push constantly for timelines, sunset clauses, and the restoration of Senate oversight. Their leader in the Senate is Senator Lorso of Telos, a pragmatist who despises Malak personally but recognizes that removing him would create a power vacuum.

**The Militarists** are Malak's core supporters: military officers, Mandalorian chieftains, and security-state architects who believe the galaxy's crisis requires permanent centralized authority. They argue that democratic deliberation is a luxury the galaxy cannot afford while the Force is dying and three hostile factions contest Concordat territory.

**Mandalore the Vindicated** occupies a unique position. Bound by Ka'halar to Malak, he serves as a formal advisor with the title of Lord Marshal -- a Mandalorian term reforged for Republic consumption. He attends Concordat military councils. He provides insight into Mandalorian clan politics. He is, in essence, a hostage dressed as a partner. He knows this. Malak knows this. Neither acknowledges it publicly. Mandalore's presence legitimizes the Concordat to Mandalorian clans. His captivity guarantees their compliance. The arrangement is stable so long as no one examines it too closely.

# Section 5: The Revanchist Remnant

## The Remnant

The Revanchist Remnant holds approximately twenty-three systems, mostly agricultural worlds, former Jedi enclaves, and Outer Rim settlements that refused to recognize the Concordat's authority. They survive on guerrilla warfare, Jedi Council sympathy, and the moral authority of being the faction that didn't compromise with the people who burned Cathar.

Day-to-day governance falls to a triumvirate (a word they avoid, given other associations): Admiral Kaeranth commands the military. Master Dorak, a Jedi historian who left the Council to join Revan, manages civilian administration. Carth Onasi, a Republic captain who defected after the Senate ratification vote, serves as diplomatic liaison and intelligence coordinator.

The Remnant's military consists of three battle groups -- roughly forty capital ships and a network of hidden bases in the Outer Rim. They cannot fight the Concordat in open battle. They fight supply-line raids, intelligence operations, and propaganda campaigns aimed at Concordat worlds where dissatisfaction is highest.

Their greatest weapon is legitimacy. The Remnant claims to be the true Republic, and a significant number of people across the galaxy believe them. Every Concordat excess -- every civil liberty curtailed, every military governor who oversteps, every Force-sensitive individual detained for "security evaluation" -- feeds Remnant recruitment.

## Admiral Kaeranth

Saul Kaeranth served under both Revan and Malak during the Mandalorian Wars. He was a career naval officer who'd risen through fleet command during the Great Hyperspace War and transferred his loyalty to the Revanchists when the Republic Senate refused to authorize military action against the Mandalorian expansion.

Kaeranth watched Malak consolidate power after the Concordat with the eye of a man who has seen strongmen before. He watched the Senate's authority erode. He watched Force-sensitive individuals placed under surveillance. He recognized the pattern: the Republic was stable, but it was no longer the Republic.

He made quiet contact with officers who felt the same way. When Revan returned from the Unknown Regions -- alone, gaunt, carrying crates of datacrons and speaking in sentence fragments about things he'd seen -- Kaeranth was ready. He extracted Revan with three battlegroups, enough firepower to discourage pursuit, and enough loyalist personnel to establish a government-in-exile.

At campaign start, Kaeranth is the Remnant's spine. He maintains fleet readiness. He manages the hidden base network. He negotiates quietly with sympathetic Concordat officers who might defect if the political winds shift. He is a military pragmatist who chose the Remnant out of principle and stays because someone has to keep the lights on while Revan is... whatever Revan is becoming.

His relationship with Revan is strained. Kaeranth extracted a hero. What he got back was a man consumed by research, intermittently brilliant and frequently incoherent, who speaks about Rakatan technology and "the pattern beneath the Force" with the intensity of a prophet and the specificity of a fever dream.

Kaeranth protects Revan from the Remnant's rank and file. He filters Revan's communications. He interprets Revan's strategic directives into actionable orders when they're coherent and quietly shelves them when they're not. He does this because the Remnant needs its figurehead, and because he still believes -- against mounting evidence -- that the person he followed into war is still in there somewhere.

## Revan's Return

Revan spent approximately two years in the Unknown Regions, chasing fragments of Rakatan Star Maps and the trail of the Star Forge. He found things. He has not shared all of what he found, and what he has shared does not always make sense.

What is clear: Revan located at least three Star Map fragments. He discovered evidence of the Rakatan Infinite Empire's collapse -- a civilization that had mastered Force-powered technology and then lost the Force entirely, with catastrophic results. He believes the Rakatan collapse and the current Force crisis share a common mechanism.

What is less clear: Revan's mental state. He speaks in long monologues about "the pattern" -- a structure he claims to perceive beneath the Force, something deeper than the light-side/dark-side binary, something that connects the Rakatan collapse, the Sith rituals, the Ashen Campaigns, and the current Force death. He sketches diagrams on any available surface. He sometimes fails to recognize people he's known for years. He sometimes demonstrates insights so precise and so far ahead of anyone else in the room that Kaeranth wonders if genius and madness are the same thing viewed from different angles.

Revan has just returned to the Remnant's fringe territories. He has not yet addressed the Remnant's leadership. He has not yet taken command. Kaeranth is managing the political implications of having a prophet-leader who may or may not still be competent.

The Remnant's rank and file are split. Some worship Revan as a returned savior. Some fear he's become the thing the Jedi Council always warned about: a Force user who chased forbidden knowledge and lost himself. Some are loyal to Kaeranth and don't particularly care what Revan thinks anymore -- the Admiral keeps them fed and alive, which is more than Revan has done in two years.

# Section 6: The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition

Canderous Ordo rejected the Concordat as dishonor. Mandalore had lost in single combat and accepted subservience rather than death. The Concordat was not partnership; it was surrender with better marketing.

The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition holds no fixed territory in the traditional sense. They control a network of hidden bases, mobile fleets, and raiding corridors across the Outer Rim and Wild Space. - **Traditionalist clans** (Ordo loyalists, fragments of Clan Jendri, Clan Cadera's orthodox wing) who believe the Concordat violated Mandalorian honor

- **Refugees and outcasts** who ended up with the Coalition because no one else would take them

Life in the Coalition is hard. Resources are scarce. Discipline is enforced through combat challenges that leave the losers dead or maimed. Canderous's vision of Mandalorian purity is romantic and increasingly detached from the material reality of feeding and supplying a nomadic military force.

The Neo-Mandalorians are not villains. They are people committed to an ideal that the galaxy may have outgrown, and they have not yet decided what they're willing to sacrifice to preserve it.

# Section 7: Kreia, the Triumvirate, and the Shadow

## Kreia / Darth Traya

Kreia was a Jedi Master. A historian. A philosopher. A teacher of teachers.

She taught Revan before he was famous. She taught dozens of Jedi Knights across a career spanning forty years. She specialized in the study of the Force's nature -- not its application, but its ontology. What is the Force? Why does it exist? What is its relationship to free will? Does the Force have intentions, and if so, are those intentions compatible with sentient freedom?

The Jedi Council tolerated her questions because her scholarship was impeccable and because the questions had no practical consequences. Then the Great Hyperspace War happened, and the questions became urgent.

Kreia's research led her to a conclusion that the Council would not have survived hearing: the Force was not merely damaged by the Sith rituals. The rituals exposed a structural vulnerability. The Force was a system, and like all systems, it could be broken. The Sith had found one method. The Ashen Campaigns found another. The Rakatan collapse, which Kreia had studied independently before Revan found the Star Maps, represented a third.

Each instance followed the same pattern: sentient beings used the Force as a tool, consumed it in the process, and the Force did not regenerate. The Force was not infinite. It was not divine. It was a resource, and the galaxy was spending it faster than it could replenish.

Kreia's next conclusion was the one that made her Darth Traya: if the Force was finite, and if sentient beings would always weaponize it, then the Force was not a gift. It was a trap. The galaxy's dependence on the Force guaranteed that every major conflict would consume more of it, until there was nothing left. The only way to save sentient life was to sever its connection to the Force entirely. Not destroy the Force (she doubted that was possible), but cut the chain between the Force and the living galaxy, so that sentient beings could survive on their own terms.

She vanished from Jedi records in 3956 BBY -- during the height of the Ashen Campaigns. She had been watching her thesis proven correct in real time.

## The Triumvirate -- Reimagined

Kreia did not build the Sith Triumvirate from Sith. She built it from the wreckage.

**Darth Nihilus** was born in the Ashen Campaigns. His original name is lost; what remains is the void. He was a Force-sensitive civilian on **Duro** during the Burning -- the Ashen strike that created the largest confirmed dead zone in the current galaxy. When the strike hit, it ripped the Force out of the surrounding area. Most Force-sensitives caught in the blast died or were severed permanently. Nihilus survived, but the void that the strike carved into the Force carved itself into him.

He became hunger. Not a metaphor: a literal, physical need to consume Force energy from living things. Without feeding, his body deteriorated. With feeding, he could sustain himself, but each feeding expanded the void. Kreia found him on the ruins of Duro's surface, a shambling figure in a broken envirosuit, surrounded by dead wildlife that had been drained of all Force connection.

Kreia recognized what he was: living proof that the Force's relationship to life was parasitic. Nihilus consumed the Force from living things because the Force had been consumed from him, and the void demanded equilibrium. He was not evil. He was a wound that walked.

She offered him a mask. The mask (a Sith artifact she had acquired during her decades of research) contained and focused his hunger, preventing it from expanding uncontrollably. In exchange, Nihilus served as Traya's weapon of last resort: a being who could drain the Force from an entire world, if she required it.

**Darth Sion** came from the Revanchist ranks. A Jedi Knight who fought in the Ashen Campaigns and was destroyed by them -- not killed, but shattered. His body was broken in the Second Siege of Onderon: caught in the periphery of the coordinated Force strike, his bones fractured, his organs failed, his nervous system was severed.

He should have died. He refused. The dark side -- the rage, the grief, the guilt of what the Ashen Campaigns had done -- held his body together through sheer force of will. Every moment was agony. The pain was the price of survival, and Sion paid it continuously because the alternative was admitting that the war had killed him and that everything he'd suffered was for nothing.

Kreia found him in a field hospital that the Revanchist medics had abandoned because there was nothing left to treat. She offered him a purpose: the war had broken him, and the Force had let it happen. She would give him something to direct his pain at. A reason to keep his shattered body moving.

Sion serves as Traya's enforcer. He is not subtle. He is not political. He is rage given form, pointed at whoever Traya identifies as an obstacle. He is also, beneath the pain and the fury, a veteran who has never processed what was done to him and what he did in return.

## The Shadow Faction

The Sith Triumvirate is not a government. It is not an army. It is a network.

Kreia operates through proxies, intermediaries, and carefully placed agents within every major faction. She has contacts in the Concordat military. She has sympathizers in the Jedi Order (scholars who've reached conclusions adjacent to her own). She has operatives in the Remnant (people positioned to monitor Revan's progress and, if necessary, ensure his discoveries serve Kreia's ends rather than his own). She has agents among the Neo-Mandalorians (warriors who've begun to suspect that the Doctrine of Necessary Strength and Traya's philosophy share uncomfortable common ground).

The Triumvirate's military assets are small: a hidden fleet of perhaps thirty ships, crewed by a mixture of former Sith military personnel, disillusioned Revanchists, Mandalorian deserters, and Force-sensitive individuals who have been severed from the Force by the crisis and blame the Jedi, the Sith, or both.

They do not know that someone is coordinating these elements toward a unified purpose.

# Section 8: The Sith

## The Sith Empire's Remnants

The Sith Empire was destroyed in the Great Hyperspace War, but its people, its military, and its traditions were not. The Republic counter-invasion that followed the war was supposed to dismantle the Sith Empire permanently. It succeeded in shattering its political structure. It failed to account for the millions of Sith soldiers, administrators, priests, engineers, and civilians who survived.

The Sith Fleet that escaped the Great Hyperspace War was not a unified force. It was a collection of fragments: individual captains, squadron commanders, and flotilla leaders who fled into the Unknown Regions, Wild Space, and the galaxy's ungoverned margins. Some surrendered to the Republic and were absorbed into penal or resettlement programs. Some established hidden enclaves on the fringes of known space. Some became pirates. Some found service with the Mandalorians, the Hutts, or corporate interests that valued military competence and didn't ask questions about war crimes.

By the campaign start, "Sith military" is not a single organization. It's a spectrum:

- **The Remnant Fleet** under various warlords -- former Sith naval officers who maintain small flotillas and operate as mercenaries, pirates, or independent powers in the Outer Rim and Wild Space
- **The Triumvirate's assets** -- vessels and personnel quietly recruited by Kreia's network
- **Integrated Sith** -- former Sith military personnel who've been absorbed into the Concordat, the Remnant, or other factions and serve openly (with varying degrees of discrimination and suspicion)
- **The Korriban garrison** -- a small force of Sith traditionalists who maintain the tomb world and its academies, operating largely independently and claiming religious authority over Sith cultural heritage

## Sith Purebloods in the Concordat Era

The Sith species -- the red-skinned, Force-sensitive people who gave the Sith Order its name -- are a diaspora in search of an identity.

The old caste system organized Sith Pureblood society into Kissai (priests and scholars), Massassi (warriors), Zuguruk (engineers and builders), and Grotthu (slaves and laborers). This system was the backbone of the Sith Empire. When the Empire fell, the system fractured.

**The Kissai** were the priestly-scholarly caste, keepers of Sith sorcery and religious tradition. After the Empire's collapse, surviving Kissai scattered. Some retreated to **Korriban**, where they maintain the tombs and the old rituals. Some integrated into Republic academic institutions, trading on their knowledge of ancient history while carefully obscuring their Force traditions. Some found new purposes: a Kissai historian on Coruscant who teaches Sith languages at the university; a Kissai alchemist on Nar Shaddaa who sells Force-enhanced pharmaceuticals to the highest bidder; a Kissai priestess on Manaan who serves the Order of Shasa as an advisor on Force metaphysics.

**The Massassi** warrior caste suffered worst. The Republic counter-invasion specifically targeted Massassi military formations. Survivors who didn't flee were killed or imprisoned. The Massassi who escaped carry a collective memory of genocide -- not the metaphorical kind, but the literal: Republic forces executed surrendering Massassi soldiers on multiple documented occasions during the counter-invasion, on the grounds that Massassi warriors were too dangerous to imprison.

Surviving Massassi are scattered, traumatized, and angry. Some serve as mercenaries. Some joined the Mandalorians (who respect warrior culture regardless of species). Some serve the Triumvirate's hidden fleet. A significant number have settled in the Outer Rim, where prejudice against Sith species is less organized and where physical strength is valued regardless of its source.

**The Zuguruk** engineers adapted most successfully. Technical skills are portable, and the galaxy always needs people who can build and maintain things. Zuguruk engineers found work in Republic shipyards, Czerka industrial operations, and independent outfits across the galaxy. They are the most assimilated Sith caste -- and the most likely to deny their heritage publicly while maintaining it privately.

The old caste system is dead in practice, but its ghost shapes Sith Pureblood identity. A Pureblood's caste background still determines how other Purebloods read them, even if the formal hierarchy no longer functions. A Kissai-descended Pureblood on Coruscant who works as a professor and a Massassi-descended Pureblood on Nar Shaddaa who works as a bouncer share a species, a homeworld of origin, and almost nothing else.

**Blood ideology** -- the belief that Sith Pureblood heritage carries inherent Force sensitivity and spiritual significance -- persists among traditional communities and has been weaponized by multiple factions. The Triumvirate uses blood ideology to recruit Pureblood operatives. The Concordat uses blood ideology as a justification for monitoring Pureblood populations (Force-sensitive individuals from a historically hostile culture = security risk). Anti-Sith bigots use blood ideology to justify discrimination.

Sith Purebloods who've integrated into Republic society navigate this daily. They are too Sith for the Republic and too Republican for the Sith. Their children face a choice: assimilate fully and lose their heritage, or maintain their identity and face suspicion. There is no comfortable middle ground.

**Intermarriage** between Sith Purebloods and Humans (a practice as old as the original Dark Jedi Exiles who colonized Sith space thousands of years ago) has produced a large population of mixed-heritage individuals. In the old Empire, mixed heritage was a source of status if the Sith bloodline was strong and a source of shame if it was weak. In the Concordat Era, mixed-heritage individuals are sometimes the most effective cultural bridges between communities -- and sometimes the most ruthlessly discriminated against by purists on both sides.

# Section 9: The Jedi Order

## The Fracture

The Jedi Order tried to remain neutral in the Concordat's formation and failed on every front.

The Council's position was coherent in theory: the Force was damaged, and using it in combat might accelerate the damage. The Jedi should focus on understanding the crisis, healing what could be healed, and avoiding actions that could make things worse.

In practice, this meant watching the galaxy burn while debating methodology.

By the campaign start, the Order has hemorrhaged members in every direction:
- Approximately two hundred Jedi serve the Revanchist Remnant, including several former Council members
- Roughly eighty Jedi serve the Concordat as military advisors, healers, or administrators, rationalizing that at least Malak acts while the Council debates
- An unknown number have joined the Triumvirate's network or been recruited by Kreia's agents
- Several dozen have renounced the Order entirely and live as private citizens
- The Council retains perhaps five hundred active Jedi, down from several thousand before the Mandalorian Wars

## The Sentinel Program

The Sentinel Program was born from desperation and designed by a perfectionist.

**Atris** conceived the program. A Jedi historian and Council member, Atris had spent years studying the intersection of Force technique and non-Force martial traditions -- particularly Echani combat philosophy, which treated physical movement as a form of expression and communication that didn't depend on the Force. She theorized that Force-sensitive individuals could be trained to use their abilities in a more limited, more controlled, and more resilient fashion if their training incorporated non-Force combat as a foundation rather than a supplement.

The theory was elegant. The implementation was rushed, desperate, and ethically catastrophic.

The Sentinel Program identifies Force-sensitive individuals who fall outside the Jedi Order's traditional recruitment age and background. It trains them in accelerated courses -- months rather than years -- combining basic Force technique with martial combat, intelligence tradecraft, and field survival. The program produces operatives who can fight effectively when their Force abilities work and survive when they don't.

**Master Kavar** runs the program's field operations and hates every moment. Kavar was a decorated Jedi Guardian who fought in the early stages of the Mandalorian Wars before the Council recalled him. He believes in the Sentinel Program's necessity and despises its methods. He watches teenagers learn in months what should take years, knowing he is creating future problems to solve present crises.

The Sentinel Program has produced approximately three hundred operatives over two years. Of those:
- Roughly two hundred are deployed in the field across Remnant-adjacent territories
- Thirty-seven have been killed in action
- Twelve have deserted, their current locations unknown
- Eight have shown signs of dark side corruption
- Four have been confirmed killed by Concordat intelligence operatives who classified them as "enemy combatants"

The program's existence is officially secret and practically public knowledge. The Concordat treats it as evidence that the Jedi Council is waging a proxy war against the legitimate government. The Remnant sees it as inadequate half-measures. The Neo-Mandalorians see it as proof that the Jedi have learned nothing from the war -- they are still trying to solve problems by creating more Force users.

**Atris** did not remain with the program after its establishment. She returned to her private academy on **Telos**, where she trains Echani Handmaidens -- elite warriors specifically equipped to subdue Force users. Her stated goal is to create a mechanism that prevents Force users from threatening the galaxy, whether they are Jedi, Sith, or something else. She has collected Sith holocrons to study the enemy. The holocrons are studying her back.

Atris is not a villain. She is a scholar who has spent too long staring into the dark and is beginning to mistake the dark for clarity. She is also, possibly, the most dangerous person in the Jedi Order -- not because of her power, but because of her certainty.

# Section 10: Mandalorian Life

## Before the Wars

Life for the average Mandalorian before the Expansion was defined by three things: the clan, the Resol'nare, and the frontier.

The **Resol'nare** -- the six core tenets of Mandalorian identity -- required every Mandalorian to wear armor, speak Mando'a, defend their clan, raise their children as Mandalorians, contribute to the clan's welfare, and answer the call of Mandalore. These were not abstractions. They structured daily life the way religious law structures an observant community.

A Mandalorian farmer on **Dxun** woke before dawn, worked the kkanmur grain fields (a hardy Dxun cereal crop that grows in the moon's dense humidity) in beskar-composite work armor (functional, not ceremonial -- the climate on Dxun required it), spoke Mando'a at home and Basic for trade, trained with weapons for an hour each evening, and taught their children to do the same. The rhythm was agricultural and martial simultaneously.

Mandalorian society before the wars was not wealthy. The clans controlled resource-rich worlds in the Outer Rim, but their economy was extractive rather than industrial. They mined cortosis and beskar. They hunted. They raided when times were lean. They traded with whoever offered fair terms and fought whoever offered unfair ones.

The social structure was pyramidal: Mandalore at the apex, the great houses (Ordo, Vizla, Fett, Skirata, Kryze, Cadera) as major political blocs, and hundreds of smaller clans as the base. Clans allied with houses, houses competed for influence, and Mandalore adjudicated disputes through a combination of personal authority and the Right of Challenge. It was a functional society, not a utopia. Domestic disputes, clan feuds, resource conflicts, and disagreements about the Resol'nare's interpretation were constant.

Women and men held equivalent social status in most clans (warriors were warriors, farmers were farmers, and gender was less relevant than competence). Some clans maintained gendered divisions of labor; others did not. The Mandalorian adoption tradition -- *gai bal manda*, taking a child of any species into a clan -- meant that Mandalorian demographics were more diverse than outsiders assumed. A Twi'lek raised Mandalorian was Mandalorian. A human born on Coruscant who never wore armor was not.

## After the Concordat

The Concordat split Mandalorian society along a fault line that was already present but had been papered over by the Expansion's momentum.

**Concordat Mandalorians** live in what they might describe as "the best version of the compromise" and what Neo-Mandalorians call "domestication." Mandalorian clans allied with the Concordat maintain their warrior culture, their armor, their training traditions, and their clan structures. They also now answer to Republic administrative authority on matters of civilian governance, taxation, resource allocation, and interstellar law.

In practice, this means a Concordat Mandalorian warrior trains with her clan, serves in a joint military unit with Republic regulars, answers to a Mandalorian chieftain for matters of honor and to a Republic officer for matters of strategy, and goes home to a settlement where the grain distribution is managed by a Republic bureaucrat instead of a Mandalorian quartermaster.

Some find this livable. The grain arrives on time. The settlements have medical facilities. The children learn in schools that teach both Mando'a and Basic. Veera Ordo's vision of a functioning hybrid society is becoming real in some places.

Others find it suffocating. The armor is the same, but the meaning has changed. A Mandalorian warrior serving the Concordat is an employee, not a free warrior. The Resol'nare says to answer Mandalore's call -- but Mandalore answers to Malak, so whose call are you really answering?

**Neo-Mandalorian** life is harder and freer. Canderous Ordo's Coalition operates as a nomadic military force: mobile bases, raiding fleets, and temporary settlements on uninhabited moons and asteroids. Resources are scarce. Discipline is maintained through combat challenges. The Resol'nare is observed with fierce orthodoxy because it's the only structure they have left.

A Neo-Mandalorian warrior wakes in a barracks carved into an asteroid, trains with whatever weapons are available, raids a supply convoy if the fleet commanders have identified a target, and spends the evening maintaining armor that is increasingly difficult to repair without access to Mandalorian forges that the Concordat now controls.

Children born into the Coalition know nothing but this life. They are raised hard, trained young, and told that they are the true inheritors of Mandalorian tradition. Whether this is liberation or deprivation depends on who you ask.

# Section 11: Kolto, Manaan, and Medicine

## Kolto Economics

Kolto is the galaxy's primary healing substance. It accelerates cellular regeneration, treats trauma, and in sufficient concentration can heal injuries that would otherwise require extensive surgery or Force-assisted healing. Before the Force crisis, kolto was one healing option among several -- important but not irreplaceable, because Jedi healers could treat conditions kolto couldn't reach.

With Force healing unreliable, kolto has become the single most strategically valuable substance in the galaxy. Every faction needs it. Only one world produces it.

**Manaan.**

## Manaan's Position

**Manaan** maintains neutrality through monopoly. The Selkath government -- operating from the surface city of Ahto -- sells kolto to every faction at prices the Selkath set. The Concordat buys kolto. The Remnant buys kolto. The Neo-Mandalorians buy (or steal) kolto. The Sith remnant fleets buy kolto through intermediaries. Whoever has credits gets kolto. Whoever has more credits gets more.

This arrangement makes Manaan the most politically important neutral world in the galaxy and the most tempting target for every faction. The Selkath know this. They maintain their independence through a combination of legal architecture (kolto export contracts that would void if Manaan's sovereignty were violated), military deterrence (a defensive fleet funded by kolto revenue), and the threat that if Manaan were conquered, the Selkath would destroy the kolto processing infrastructure rather than let any faction monopolize the supply.

The kolto itself comes from deep-sea deposits in the ocean beneath Ahto City, near the Hrakert Rift. The Selkath maintain that these deposits are connected to a deep ecological and spiritual system they are not obligated to explain to outsiders. They are correct, and this is not a negotiating tactic.


## Internal Manaan Politics

Manaan's neutrality is not monolithic. The Selkath are not unified.

The Ahto City Council governs through consensus, but consensus is fraying. Factions within the Council include:
- **Isolationists** who want to reduce kolto exports and focus on Selkath self-sufficiency
- **Commercialists** who want to increase exports and use the revenue to build Manaan into a galactic financial center
- **Interventionists** who argue that Manaan's neutrality enables atrocities on both sides and that the Selkath should use their kolto monopoly as leverage to force peace negotiations
- **The Order of Shasa** -- a Force-sensitive tradition indigenous to the Selkath -- who argue that the kolto's connection to the Force means the Force crisis is a Selkath problem, not merely a galactic one

Foreign embassies in Ahto City compete for influence. The Concordat embassy is the largest and best-funded. The Remnant operates a smaller diplomatic mission. Various corporate interests (Czerka prominent among them) maintain commercial offices. Intelligence operatives from every faction circulate through Ahto City's markets and cantinas.

## Kolto Synthesis

Multiple factions have attempted to synthesize kolto outside Manaan. All have failed to replicate the substance's full effectiveness. Synthetic variants heal physical wounds but lack kolto's deeper regenerative properties -- the ones that the Selkath attribute to the substance's connection to Manaan's ecosystem and, by extension, to the Force.

The Windfall Protocol scenario involves one such attempt to understand and modify kolto's properties. The results are not what anyone expected.

# Section 12: The Freighter Incident

## Premise

The PCs are crew, passengers, or hitching rides on a freighter traveling a major hyperlane. The ship drops out of hyperspace six days early. In the middle of nowhere. Sensors show they're at the edge of a dead zone: one of those regions where the Force doesn't work right and reality gets strange.

This is the campaign's first taste of why the Force death crisis is terrifying on a practical level. Dead zones don't just affect Force users. They make hyperspace navigation fail. The lanes are going dark. The infrastructure the galaxy relies on to function -- travel, trade, communication -- is rotting from the inside.

Three other ships are stuck here:
- A **Concordat military transport**
- A **Remnant courier vessel**
- A **Neo-Mandalorian raiding ship**

Nobody can leave. Hyperdrive navigation doesn't work near dead zones. The PCs and these three crews need to figure out how to escape together, or wait for rescue that is weeks away at best.

The dead zone is expanding. Slowly but measurably. In three days it will engulf all the ships. The clock is ticking.

## The Options

Players face at least three paths (and will invent more):

- **Find the dead zone's center and shut it down.** Sensors suggest the dead zone is radiating outward from a fixed point. That point is a Sith ritual site from the Great Hyperspace War -- one of the places where Sith sorcery cracked the Force during the original invasion. Getting there means entering the dead zone. For Force-sensitive characters (especially species like Sith Purebloods or Miraluka who are biologically entangled with the Force), this is existentially dangerous.
- **Rig the hyperdrives to navigate in dead zone conditions.** Theoretically possible. Extremely dangerous. Requires engineering cooperation between ships crewed by people who are nominally at war with each other.
- **Send a distress signal and wait.** The signal might reach someone. Rescue might arrive before the dead zone swallows them. Neither is guaranteed, and the expanding dead zone means waiting is a death sentence for Force-dependent crew.
- **Something the players invent.** The scenario is designed to reward creative solutions.

## The NPCs

##### Key NPCs

**Lt. Commander Varik** (Concordat Transport). Professional. Efficient. Will not start trouble unless given a reason. His daughter is aboard and she's Force-sensitive. The dead zone exposure is killing her. Varik will do anything to save her -- cooperate with enemies, betray allies, sacrifice strangers. He represents the Concordat at its best (a father protecting his child) and its worst (a man who will burn the galaxy for one person). The players get to decide which version of Varik they meet.

Daaro is honorable but pragmatic -- he will challenge players to prove their worth, and he respects enemies who show courage.

## Why It Works

The Freighter Incident is a bottle episode. Four ships, one crisis, three factions, and a ticking clock. It forces moral choices, faction exposure, and organic plot hooks based on what the players choose to do.

Did they steal the Remnant's data? Now both the Remnant and the Concordat want them. Did they save Varik's daughter? The Concordat owes them. Did they honor Mandalorian codes? The Neo-Mandalorians remember. Did they enter the dead zone and survive? They know something about the Force crisis that most people don't.

The scenario teaches the world by forcing the players to interact with it. By the time they escape the dead zone, they've met representatives of every major faction, made enemies or allies, and established their characters' values under pressure. That's the foundation for everything that follows.

## Campaign Trajectory (Context for the Book)

The Freighter Incident starts small and personal. By session 10, the PCs realize they've chosen sides in a cold war. By session 20, they're key players in faction politics. By session 40, they're making decisions that determine which government rules the galaxy. The book should frame the Freighter Incident as the first domino without revealing the full cascade.

# Section 13: Galactic Scale and Sovereignty

## Scale

Drawing on the proportional logic of historical empires mapped to a galaxy of millions of inhabited systems:

**The Concordat** controls approximately four hundred major systems and claims authority over another six hundred in various stages of integration or contestation. The Core Worlds, most of the Colonies region, and significant portions of the Inner Rim and Expansion Region are firmly Concordat. Mid Rim territories are a patchwork. The Outer Rim is contested, with Concordat authority diminishing with distance from established garrisons.

**The Revanchist Remnant** holds twenty-three systems outright and maintains covert bases in another fifteen. Their territory is scattered across the Outer Rim, connected by hidden hyperspace routes and guerrilla supply networks. Economically, they survive on agricultural output, sympathizer donations, and captured Concordat supplies.

**The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition** controls no fixed systems but maintains a nomadic presence across the Outer Rim and Wild Space. They have temporary bases on perhaps a dozen worlds and asteroid installations across a region spanning thousands of light-years.

**The Sith Triumvirate** holds nothing publicly. Their assets are hidden, mobile, and deniable.

**Hutt Space** encompasses approximately three hundred systems in the Outer Rim, operating under Hutt clan authority. Hutt Space has been neutral in the Concordat conflict, though individual Hutts have sold weapons, intelligence, and logistics support to every side.

**Unaligned space** is vast. Thousands of systems in the Outer Rim, Wild Space, and the Unknown Regions answer to no galactic authority. Some are genuinely independent. Some are too remote or poor to interest any faction. Some are controlled by local warlords, corporate outfits, or criminal syndicates.

\column

## The Sovereignty System (GM Mechanic Context)

The galaxy operates on a security gradient:

**High Security** zones (Core Worlds, major Concordat systems) are patrolled by Concordat military forces and Republic-legacy law enforcement. Crime exists but is suppressed. Force-sensitive individuals are monitored. Military governor authority overrides civilian courts in designated territories.

**Medium Security** zones (Mid Rim, contested territories) are policed by whoever holds the local garrison -- Concordat military, Mandalorian clan patrols, Remnant guerrilla forces, or corporate security operations (Czerka maintains its own paramilitary forces on worlds where it has mining interests). Law enforcement is inconsistent and politically determined.

**Low Security** zones (Outer Rim frontiers, raiding corridors) are policed by whoever has the most guns in local space. Hutt enforcers, Exchange syndicates, Mandalorian raiding fleets, independent bounty hunters, and Sith remnant warlords all fill the vacuum in different sectors. "Law" is whatever the local power structure says it is.

**Null Security** zones (Wild Space, Unknown Regions, dead zones) have no organized authority. Survival depends entirely on the crew you have and the ship you're flying.
