# The Concordat Era

## What This Is

A Star Wars KOTOR-era alternate history TTRPG setting book for Fate Condensed, built for an actual play podcast. The project reimagines the post-Mandalorian Wars galaxy through Napoleonic historical parallels, political thriller structure, and frontier survival stakes.

The setting book lives in `Homebrewery/Setting Book.md`. This repo is the source of truth. Homebrewery has persistent syncing issues; use it for rendering only.

**System:** Fate Condensed. Force-Sensitive Extra costs 1 stunt slot. Force Instability mechanic gives the GM a standing compel against Force-related aspects. Force fails roughly one-third of the time in normal space, two-thirds near dead zones, and requires a fate point to attempt in total dead zones.

**Campaign Year:** 4 CE (Concordat Era). Three years after the Concordat's founding.

**Setting Thesis:** The Concordat Era is a setting about what happens when the foundations everyone built their lives on turn out to be temporary. The Force is failing. The Republic bargained away its principles for survival. The Mandalorians were right about self-reliance but wrong about everything else. Three governments claim legitimacy; none deserve it without qualification. Players enter a galaxy where the old answers no longer work and no one has agreed on the new ones yet.

**Tone:** Political thriller crossed with frontier survival story. Post-revolutionary, not post-apocalyptic. Think the Directory period after the French Revolution, or the exhausted, paranoid pragmatism of Europe between 1815 and 1848.

**Player Promise:** You will play characters who matter in a galaxy that has not decided what it is yet. Your beliefs, loyalties, and choices will collide with a political order that is still wet cement. You will not be handed a villain to fight. You will find people with defensible positions doing indefensible things, and you will have to decide where you stand.

## The Force Crisis

The Force is failing. Damage originated during the Great Hyperspace War when Sith performed mass ritual warfare. The Ashen Campaign's forty-one strikes deepened the damage.

**Dead Zone Scale:**
- **Partial:** Force works intermittently, roughly half the time
- **Significant:** Force resists use; precognition fragments; healing takes longer
- **Total:** Force absent; Miraluka go blind; all Force abilities fail; lightsabers still cut (plasma weapons, not Force-powered)

**Material Consequences:** Hyperspace navigation unreliable. Force healing fails without warning. Seers report silence. Kolto (sole alternative to Force healing) is the galaxy's most valuable resource. Zones are expanding.

**Competing Theories:**
- **Jedi scholars:** Using the Force may accelerate the damage
- **Sith scholars:** Light/dark binary was always wrong; the Force is failing because ten thousand years of treating it as a tool broke the relationship
- **Revan's scar tissue theory:** The Force operates as an ecosystem, not a consciousness; dead zones are an immune response; Force users are the pathogen
- **Jolee Bindo's observation:** The Shadowlands Force ecology is stable and untouched; the crisis is a disruption problem, not systemic failure

## Napoleonic Parallels

This setting draws structural, political, and character-level parallels to the Napoleonic era. These inform every major faction, character, and institution.

### Government and Power
- **The Consulate.** Malak holds the title of Supreme Chancellor-General (First Consul). Three consuls govern: Malak (military/executive), Mandalore the Vindicated (Mandalorian integration), and the Senate Speaker (civilian administration). The structure mirrors Napoleon's three-consul system where one consul held real power and the others served as institutional cover.
- **Emergency powers with no expiration.** The Concordat maintains wartime authority three years after the war ended. The Senate ratified it. No sunset clause exists. Napoleon extended consular authority the same way.
- **Military governors.** Malak appoints military governors across Concordat territories. Centralization at the cost of local autonomy.
- **The Office of Force Affairs.** A surveillance apparatus targeting Force-sensitive citizens. Parallels the Napoleonic police state under Fouché.
- **Hybrid civil-military administration.** The Concordat merges Republic administrative systems with Mandalorian military hierarchy. Napoleon blended Revolutionary civil institutions with military command structure the same way.

### Military and Warfare
- **The Ashen Campaign as artillery doctrine.** Force weaponization deployed at strategic scale across forty-one strikes. Napoleonic artillery won battles through concentration of firepower at decisive points. The Ashen Campaign did the same with the Force, and broke the galaxy's infrastructure in the process.
- **Professional militarization.** The Revanchist Wars created a professional standing army with Jedi generals, replacing the Republic's peacetime patrol navy. Parallels the rise of professional military corps replacing aristocratic militia systems.
- **Kaeranth as career officer.** Admiral Kaeranth entered Republic fleet service at nineteen in a peacetime navy. The Great Hyperspace War made him. His career tracks the trajectory of French officers who trained under the ancien régime and fought under Napoleon.

### Character Parallels
- **Malak as Napoleon.** Pragmatic authoritarian who seized power through martial proof (the Dhas'kara single combat) rather than democratic process. Lost his jaw and sword arm (Napoleon's physical decline). Governs through productivity, institutional reform, and ruthless scheduling. Maintains democratic theater while holding absolute authority. His daily routine, administrative obsession, and the gap between public image and private exhaustion are modeled on Napoleon's consular period.
- **Revan as the exile-genius.** Brilliant strategist who left for the Unknown Regions and returned diminished, obsessed with patterns no one else can see. The mind fractures. Lucid periods produce predictions that come true; incoherent periods produce star charts and Rakatan transcriptions. A Napoleon at St. Helena who chose exile before anyone forced it.
- **Kaeranth as Berthier.** Napoleon's indispensable chief of staff, the operational mind who translated strategic brilliance into actionable orders. Kaeranth does the same for Revan. Without him, the fleet scatters in weeks.
- **The Concordat's internal factions** mirror post-revolutionary French political divisions: Militarists (expand by force), Consolidationists (hold current territory), Restorationists (return to democratic governance), Expansionists (economic growth through new systems).
- **Foreign threat justifying internal control.** The Force crisis and Outer Rim instability justify centralization of power, surveillance, and suspension of civil liberties. Every authoritarian era uses the same logic.

### Tonal Period
The setting occupies the Directory/Congress of Vienna period: governments trying to stabilize after total warfare, questioning whether the old systems can hold. The mood is exhausted institutional pragmatism, not revolutionary fervor.

## Literary Parallels and Influences

### Historical Writing
- **Napoleonic biography style.** Character sections (Alek, Revan, Kaeranth) follow the structure of long-form historical biography: childhood, capabilities, institutional relationships, key decisions, daily routines, contradictions. Andrew Roberts's *Napoleon: A Life* and Adam Zamoyski's *Napoleon: A Life* are tonal models.
- **Military history scholarship.** Supply chain collapse, administrative failure, attrition calculus, and the way wars reshape societies receive the same analytical weight as battles and Force powers.

### Literary Fiction
- **The Silmarillion.** Prose rhythm and cosmological weight. Character entries read as encyclopedic lore, not character sheets. Grand statements grounded by specific detail.
- **King Lear.** Revan's mental fracture. A brilliant mind coming apart, producing prophecy and incoherence in alternation. The rank and file don't know which Revan they'll get on any given day.
- **Classical tragedy.** No faction is pure villain. Each holds defensible positions while doing indefensible things. The Concordat provides stability at the cost of liberty. The Remnant fights for principles but is losing. The Coalition rejects compromise and pays in irrelevance.
- **KOTOR II philosophy.** Kreia's arguments about the Force as enslavement. The question of whether detachment and passion are both traps. The Sith Triumvirate's intellectual project (managed severance of the Force) taken seriously as philosophy, not dismissed as villainy.

### Genre Structure
- **Political thriller.** Factions' internal contradictions, competing agendas, intelligence networks, operatives working toward unclear goals. Every character hook is a potential campaign thread.
- **Frontier survival.** Outer Rim content emphasizes self-reliance, practical problem-solving, and the texture of life outside central governance. Kolto costs, hyperspace failures, mechanical repairs.
- **Theological debate as plot.** The Force crisis is framed philosophically and materially. Institutions debate its nature. Ordinary people deal with its consequences. No character section avoids the question.

## Skills

Two skills govern work in this repo. Both are mandatory when their conditions apply.

### Brainstorming (Design Before Implementation)

**Trigger:** Any creative work. New sections, new characters, new worlds, new species entries, structural changes, feature additions. If you are about to write new content or restructure existing content, brainstorm first.

**Hard gate:** Do not write prose, create sections, or modify structure until the design has been presented and approved. This applies to every task regardless of perceived simplicity.

**Process:**
1. Explore project context (read relevant chapters, check cross-references)
2. Ask clarifying questions one at a time (prefer multiple choice)
3. Propose 2-3 approaches with trade-offs and a recommendation
4. Present the design in sections scaled to complexity
5. Get approval before proceeding
6. Write a design doc to `docs/superpowers/specs/YYYY-MM-DD-<topic>-design.md`
7. Self-review the spec for placeholders, contradictions, ambiguity, scope
8. Get user review of the written spec
9. Proceed to implementation

**Anti-pattern:** "This is too simple to need a design." Every project goes through brainstorming. Simple designs can be a few sentences, but the approval gate is non-negotiable.

**Scope check:** If a request describes multiple independent subsystems, decompose first. Each sub-project gets its own spec-plan-implementation cycle.

### Stop Slop (Prose Quality)

**Trigger:** Every piece of prose written or edited. All new text, all revisions, all expansions. Run this as a final pass before completing any writing task.

**8 Rules:**
1. Cut filler phrases. No throat-clearing openers, emphasis crutches, or adverbs.
2. Break formulaic structures. No binary contrasts, negative listings, dramatic fragmentation, rhetorical setups, false agency.
3. Use active voice. Every sentence needs a human subject doing something. No passive constructions. No inanimate objects performing human actions.
4. Be specific. No vague declaratives. Name the specific thing. No lazy extremes ("every," "always," "never").
5. Put the reader in the room. "You" beats "People." Specifics beat abstractions.
6. Vary rhythm. Mix sentence lengths. Two items beat three. No em dashes.
7. Trust readers. State facts directly. Skip softening, justification, hand-holding.
8. Cut quotables. If it sounds like a pull-quote, rewrite it.

**Quick checks before delivering prose:**
- Any adverbs? Kill them.
- Any passive voice? Find the actor, make them the subject.
- Inanimate thing doing a human verb? Name the person.
- Sentence starts with a Wh- word? Restructure it.
- "Here's what/this/that" throat-clearing? Cut to the point.
- "Not X, it's Y" contrasts? State Y directly.
- Three consecutive sentences match length? Break one.
- Paragraph ends with punchy one-liner? Vary it.
- Em-dash anywhere? Remove it.
- Vague declarative? Name the specific implication.

**Score 35+/50** on Directness, Rhythm, Trust, Authenticity, Density. Below 35: revise before delivering.

## Mission Statement and Current Goals

### Core Mission
Build a TTRPG setting book that treats Star Wars with the analytical seriousness of historical scholarship and the emotional weight of literary fiction. Every sentence serves two audiences: players looking for character hooks and GMs looking for campaign fuel. The book reads as an in-universe document produced by a reasonably informed galactic citizen, with GM-facing annotations where needed.

### Prose Standards
- **Stop Slop compliance.** Score 35+/50 on the Stop Slop rubric (Directness, Rhythm, Trust, Authenticity, Density). See the Skills section above for the full checklist.
- **Star Wars names.** Use the Star Wars Name Generator API (`https://vqcjvexnwxnwvaoeofka.supabase.co/functions/v1/generate-names`) when creating new named characters. No names that sound generated or Earth-derived.

### Content Quality Standards

The expanded Alek section (Ch8), Kaeranth section (Ch9), Clan Ordo entry (Ch10), and Force Crisis POVs (Ch6) define the quality floor. Study them before writing new content. The patterns below are extracted from those sections.

#### Detail Density

Every section must contain specific, verifiable facts. No unnamed NPCs. No unquantified problems. No undated events.

- **Characters need:** age, homeworld, titles, named mentors/rivals/staff, specific Force abilities with current degradation, physical description of injuries or distinguishing features.
- **Factions need:** headcount, territory count, named leaders at multiple levels, specific ongoing operations with targets and timelines.
- **Worlds need:** population centers by name, controlling authority, economic base with specific commodities, named NPCs who run things locally.
- **Species need:** social structure with named institutions, communication methods, specific cultural practices (not generic), named communities in the current era.

Test: if you removed all proper nouns, numbers, and dates from a section and it still made sense, the section lacks detail.

#### Actionable Campaign Material

Every paragraph must contain at least one element a GM can turn into a session: a decision pending, a conflict present, a secret to discover, a leverage point, a problem to solve, or a resource to compete over.

**Ratio targets by section type:**

| Section Type | Worldbuilding | Hooks | Example |
|-------------|--------------|-------|----------|
| Origins / biography | 65% | 35% | Alek's childhood, Temple training, career arc |
| Current operations | 40% | 60% | Kaeranth's planned supply depot raid, staff disagreements |
| Final operational paragraph | 30% | 70% | Clan Ordo's economic crisis, Fett rivalry, recruitment losses |
| POV / perspective sections | 25% | 75% | Force crisis stakeholder perspectives (Ch6) |

**The Ch6 Force Crisis test:** Each stakeholder perspective in Ch6 presents a named person with a specific problem that players can enter. "The Baran Do Sages issued their first incorrect forecast in 1 CE; three storms hit populated areas without warning" gives a GM a mission (investigate the failure), a faction contact (the Sages), a timeline (1 CE), and stakes (civilian casualties). A vague statement like "the Force is weakening and people are scared" gives a GM nothing. Every sentence in the book should pass this test.

#### Subsection Templates

**Character entries follow this structure:**
1. Opening paragraph: current title, age, location, one-sentence power dynamic
2. **Origins** — formative experience with dates, named mentors, entry point to their institution
3. **Force Capabilities** (if applicable) — specific abilities, training lineage, current degradation tied to campaign mechanics
4. **Institutional Relationships** — 1-2 named subsections ("Alek and the Jedi Council," "Kaeranth and Revan") showing political positioning with specific friction points
5. **Current Operations** — daily routine, decision-making context, 2-3 named crises with quantified stakes
6. **Player-facing closing** — direct statement of what players encounter, what this character needs, what they offer

**Faction/clan entries follow this structure:**
1. Opening: leader name, headcount, territory, one-sentence position
2. Core argument or ideology — stated as belief, not description
3. Quantified Problem 1 — named, numbered, with specific mechanism
4. Quantified Problem 2 — named, numbered, with specific mechanism
5. Named rival relationships with stated pressure mechanisms
6. GM-facing paragraph: what players find here regardless of faction, what they must navigate, what the entry point looks like

**World entries follow this structure:**
1. Opening: controlling authority, strategic importance, one defining condition
2. Geography and population centers — named districts, settlements, or regions with distinct character
3. Political situation — who governs, who contests, what the current tension is (quantified)
4. Economy and daily life — specific commodities, costs, what ordinary residents deal with
5. Force/crisis impact — how the Force crisis or the wars changed this world specifically
6. Player hooks — 3-4 specific scenarios tied to the world's unique conditions

**Species entries follow this structure:**
1. Opening: population status, homeworld, one defining cultural trait
2. Social structure — governance, family organization, named institutions
3. Communication and culture — specific practices, not generic descriptions ("lekku movement conveys emotional subtext that Basic cannot express" beats "they have a rich culture")
4. Current era pressures — what the wars, the Force crisis, or the Concordat did to this species specifically
5. Diaspora and galactic role — where they live now, what roles they fill, how other species treat them
6. Player character implications — what a PC from this species knows, believes, fears, and brings to a crew

## Formatting Conventions

### Homebrewery Markdown
- `\page` for page breaks
- `{{pageNumber,auto}}` on every page
- `{{Ch2,tab}}` for chapter tab markers
- `{{footnote ...}}` for page footnotes
- `{{descriptive ...}}` for in-world flavor text boxes
- `{{note ...}}` for GM-facing sidebars
- `#` for chapter titles, `##` for major sections, `####` for character names, `#####` for subsections within character entries

### Visual Design
- **Display font:** Playfair Display (Didone serif, Napoleonic influence)
- **Body font:** Crimson Pro (humanist serif, 9-11pt two-column)
- **Technical font:** Inter (stat blocks, tables, page numbers)
- **Accent font:** Cormorant Garamond Italic (epigraphs, in-world quotes)
- **Color palette:** Parchment Cream (#F2EBD9), Void Black (#1A1A1E), Burnished Gold (#C4A35A), Republic Crimson (#8B2D2D), Officer Blue (#3D5A73), Campaign Green (#6B7055)
- **Visual philosophy:** Classicism under stress. Clean geometry and sharp type hierarchy give authority. Texture, wear, and tonal shifts remind you the galaxy is fraying.

### File Conventions
- This repo is the canonical source. Homebrewery copies drift.
- The glossary in the Setting Book appendix tracks all named characters, locations, factions, and terms with page numbers. Update it when adding or modifying content.
- The Table of Contents lists page numbers derived from `\page` break counts. Update it when page counts shift.
- Character names must sound like Star Wars. Use the Star Wars Name Generator API (`https://vqcjvexnwxnwvaoeofka.supabase.co/functions/v1/generate-names`) when creating new named characters. Do not invent names that sound generated or Earth-derived.

## Working With This Project

When editing the Setting Book:
1. Read the relevant chapter section before making changes.
2. Check cross-references: characters, events, and factions mentioned in one chapter appear in others. Changing a detail in Chapter 9 may require updates in Chapters 7, 8, 11, and the glossary.
3. After adding or removing content, recount `\page` breaks and update the TOC if page numbers shifted.
4. After modifying character sections, update the glossary entry (descriptions and page numbers).
5. Run a Stop Slop pass on new prose: scan for em dashes, adverbs, passive voice, throat-clearing, binary contrasts, and filler phrases.
6. Every sentence should serve as a potential player or GM hook. If a sentence adds atmosphere without providing actionable campaign material, cut it or revise it until it does both.

## Subagent and Orchestration Guidelines

### When to Use Subagents

Use the `Explore` subagent for:
- Searching the Setting Book for cross-references before making changes (character mentions, faction references, event citations across chapters)
- Gathering page numbers across multiple chapters to update the glossary or TOC
- Reading multiple files in parallel to build context (lore bible, phase1 design notes, style guide)
- Verifying consistency of a character's details across chapters

Specify thoroughness: `quick` (spot check), `medium` (relevant sections), `thorough` (full audit).

### Orchestration Patterns for This Repo

**Single chapter edit:**
1. Read the target chapter section
2. Read cross-referenced chapters (characters/events that appear elsewhere)
3. Make edits
4. Run Stop Slop pass on new prose
5. Update TOC if page count changed (count `\page` breaks)
6. Update glossary entries for any modified characters, locations, or terms

**New world or species entry:**
1. Brainstorm: read existing entries in Ch21/Ch22 for format and depth
2. Read the lore bible and factions-and-species supplement for source material
3. Propose structure and content outline, get approval
4. Write the entry
5. Run Stop Slop pass
6. Add glossary entries
7. Update TOC page numbers
8. Check if the world/species is mentioned in other chapters; add cross-references if needed

**Multi-chapter expansion (e.g., expanding all species entries):**
1. Brainstorm the full scope: which entries, what depth, what order
2. Decompose into independent sub-tasks (one per entry)
3. For each entry: read existing content → write expansion → Stop Slop pass → glossary update
4. After all entries: recount `\page` breaks, update full TOC, verify cross-references

**Cross-reference audit:**
- Use `Explore` subagent with `thorough` to find all mentions of a character/faction/term across the full Setting Book
- Compare details for consistency (ages, dates, titles, relationships)
- Fix discrepancies

## Fate Condensed System Reference

- 19 skills in a pyramid (1 Great +4, 2 Good +3, 3 Fair +2, 4 Average +1)
- Force-Sensitive Extra costs 1 stunt slot (2 free stunts instead of 3)
- Force techniques: Telekinesis, Precognition, Force Healing, Battle Meditation, Force Empathy, Force Concealment, Lightsaber Mastery, Force Persuasion
- Force Instability: GM has a standing compel against Force-related aspects
- Dead zone failure rates: ~1/3 normal space, ~2/3 near dead zones, fate point required in total dead zones
- Miraluka get Force Empathy free (Force perception is their baseline existence)
- Kolto: mild consequence clears immediately, moderate clears after 1 scene instead of 1 session
- Character creation driven by 6 narrative questions, not stats
