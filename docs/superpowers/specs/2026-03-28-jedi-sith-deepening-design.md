## Section 1: Chapter 5 Light/Dark Expansion

**Location:** End of existing Chapter 5, before the transition to Chapter 6.
**Length:** ~3-4 pages.

### Content: Six Positions on the Light Side and Dark Side

The Force has moral dimensions that scholars and practitioners interpret differently. Present six positions, each grounded in a specific tradition:

1. **Jedi Orthodox Position.** The light side is the Force's natural state. The dark side is a corruption introduced by selfish use. Calm, selflessness, and harmony align you with reality. Passion and aggression distort the Force. The Jedi Council teaches this as doctrine.

2. **Sith Imperial Position.** The dark side is the Force's true nature. The light side is a cage built by cowards. Passion reveals truth. Power earned through struggle is the only honest relationship with reality. The Sith Empire encoded this in its training academies.

3. **Keeper of Adas Position.** The Force has no sides. Light and dark are labels the Jedi and the Exile-era Sith imposed on a phenomenon that predates both traditions by millennia. The pre-Exile Sith practiced emotional engagement with the Force without categorizing emotions as light or dark. Rage at injustice and joy at creation both served the pattern.

4. **Secular/Mandalorian Position.** The Force is a natural phenomenon with no moral content. Calling it "light" or "dark" is like calling gravity "good" or "evil." Force users project their own ethics onto an energy field and then claim the energy field validates their ethics. The Mandalorians find this circular and unconvincing.

5. **Kreia's Nihilism.** Both the light side and the dark side are traps. The Force itself is a prison. It binds the galaxy to a deterministic pattern, puppeting civilizations toward outcomes that serve the Force's own continuity. True freedom requires breaking free of the Force entirely. Kreia taught this to a small number of students. Most of them are dead.

6. **The Wound Perspective.** The Force crisis has created a new position by default. In dead zones, where the Force is absent, neither "side" exists. Beings who have lived through dead zone exposure report a clarity they describe as neither light nor dark. Some seek this state deliberately. Others fear it as a form of spiritual death.

### Design notes
- Present each position in 2-3 paragraphs. Name the tradition, state the claim, give the reasoning.
- Do not editorialize which position is "correct." The setting treats this as an open question.
- End with a player-facing note: your character's position on this question shapes how they relate to every Force user they meet.

## Section 1B: Revan and Malak — The Light/Dark Question Made Personal

**Location:** End of the new light/dark positions section in Chapter 5, after the six positions and before the player-facing note. Alternatively, could work as a standalone subsection of Chapter 5 titled "Two Case Studies" or "The Men Who Defy the Categories."
**Length:** ~2-3 pages.

### Why This Section Exists

Players familiar with Star Wars will arrive with assumptions about Revan and Malak. In canon KOTOR, both fell to the dark side and became Sith Lords. In this AU, neither fell. But neither remained clean Jedi, either. They occupy the uncomfortable territory between the six positions outlined above, and that territory is where most player characters will live. Revan and Malak are the setting's most visible examples of what happens when the light/dark binary fails to describe a person.

### Content: Revan

**What he was.** A Jedi who defied the Council to fight a war he believed the Order was morally obligated to fight. The most gifted strategist of his generation. A scholar who studied Sith artifacts, Rakatan technology, and Force phenomena the Council classified as forbidden.

**His relationship to the dark side.** Revan studied Sith holocrons, decoded Sith ritual techniques, and sent those techniques to Malak for use in the Ashen Campaigns. He pursued Rakatan technology in the Unknown Regions for two years and came back diminished. He never declared himself a Sith. He never adopted the Sith Code. He used dark side knowledge the way an engineer uses a dangerous material: with purpose, not devotion. The Council would say this distinction is meaningless. Revan would say the Council's categories are the problem.

**What each faction considers him.** The Jedi Council considers Revan a cautionary tale: a brilliant Jedi who touched forbidden knowledge and lost himself, proof that the dark side corrupts regardless of intent. The Revanchist Remnant considers him their founder and figurehead, though rank-and-file opinion ranges from worship to concern that he's become incoherent. The Concordat considers him a security threat and a destabilizing element. The Mandalorians respect his strategic mind but note that he left the war before it ended and let Malak do the hard work.

**The unanswered question.** Is Revan a dark side user? He never raged into power. He never sought domination. He studied, researched, applied. His current state (intermittent brilliance, obsessive focus on Rakatan patterns, deteriorating coherence) could be dark side corruption, could be the psychological cost of two years alone in the Unknown Regions, or could be something the existing light/dark framework has no name for. The setting does not answer this. Your character will form their own opinion.

### Content: Malak

**What he was.** Revan's operational counterpart: a Jedi general who understood logistics, troop movements, and the mathematics of attrition warfare. The man who challenged Mandalore to single combat and won the war through iron rather than the Force.

**His relationship to the dark side.** Malak authorized the Ashen Campaigns: forty-one coordinated Force strikes that killed six hundred thousand civilians and permanently damaged twelve planets. He used Sith ritual channeling techniques that Revan decoded from a holocron. He did this with full awareness of the cost, reading the damage reports and signing the next order. He did not rage. He calculated. He chose. The Ashen Campaigns used dark side methodology (massed ritual channeling derived from Sith combat techniques) applied through military discipline. Malak would say he used a tool. The dead zones on twelve planets would say the tool used him.

**What he refused.** Malak never used the Force in his Dhas'kara against Mandalore. He fought with iron and will. He wears prosthetics where his jaw and sword arm were. He chose to win the war's decisive moment without the Force at all. This choice defines him as much as the Ashen Campaigns do. A man who authorized Force superweapons and then refused to use the Force when his own life was at stake.

**What each faction considers him.** The Concordat considers him Supreme Chancellor-General: a necessary strongman holding the galaxy together. The Revanchist Remnant considers him a traitor who seized power and betrayed democratic principles. The Jedi Council considers him a fallen Jedi who never technically fell, which is worse because they cannot point to a clean moment of corruption. The Mandalorians consider him Mandalore's victor and honor-bound partner, the outsider who earned respect through blood. The Keepers of Adas, if asked, would note that Malak used Sith techniques without understanding the tradition they came from, which makes him dangerous in a way that an actual Sith Lord would not be.

**The unanswered question.** Malak never touched the dark side in the way the Jedi define it. He never surrendered to passion. He never sought personal power for its own sake. He authorized atrocities through rational calculation and genuine belief that fewer people would die this way. The Jedi Code has no category for a man who commits monstrous acts while maintaining emotional equilibrium. Is Malak proof that the dark side operates through cold logic as well as hot rage? Or is he proof that the light/dark binary is inadequate to describe what war does to decent people?

### Design Notes
- This section functions as a bridge between the abstract six positions and the lived reality of the setting. The positions are theoretical. Revan and Malak are what those theories look like when tested by war.
- Do not resolve the questions. State the facts, present each faction's reading, and leave the player to decide.
- Heavy cross-reference potential: Chapter 7 (history), Chapter 8 (Concordat), Chapter 9 (Remnant), Chapter 11 (Jedi Order), Chapter 14 (Jedi Way). Do not repeat content from those chapters. Reference it. The goal is to reframe what players already know through the lens of the light/dark question.
- Follow stop-slop rules. This section in particular must avoid the pull-quote trap: Revan and Malak invite dramatic one-liners. Resist.

## Section 2: Chapter 14 Jedi Expansion

**Location:** Integrated into existing Chapter 14 ("The Jedi Way"). New subsections added after existing content.
**Length:** ~5-6 pages of new material.

### Content A: Lived Practice (~3 pages)

Concrete detail about what Jedi actually do day-to-day beyond meditation and diplomacy:

**Morning practice.** What a typical Jedi's day looks like at a temple versus deployed in the field. Specific meditation forms (Breath of the Whills, Walking Meditation of Telos, the Dantooine Open-Sky method). How these differ and what each trains.

**Healing arts.** How Force healing works mechanically in this setting. Limitations (you accelerate the body's own processes; you cannot regrow limbs or cure genetic conditions). The triage protocols Jedi healers developed during the Mandalorian Wars. How healing works (or fails) near dead zones.

**Teaching and apprenticeship.** The Master-Padawan structure. How Masters select students (or don't — some traditions assign pairs, others let them form organically). What training looks like year by year: early years emphasize perception and emotional regulation, middle years add physical training and combat, later years focus on specialization within the three paths.

**Service roles.** Jedi served as mediators, agricultural consultants (using Force perception to diagnose crop blight), search-and-rescue specialists, and archivists. Detail 2-3 of these roles with enough specificity that a player could build a character around one.

### Content B: Lightsaber Forms (~1.5 pages)

The seven classical forms, adapted for this setting's power level and era:

- **Form I: Shii-Cho** — foundational, wide sweeping motions, effective against multiple opponents, taught to every Padawan
- **Form II: Makashi** — precision dueling, developed for lightsaber-versus-lightsaber combat, favored by Consulars
- **Form III: Soresu** — defensive, energy-conservative, designed to outlast opponents, dominant form post-Mandalorian Wars because survival mattered more than aggression
- **Form IV: Ataru** — acrobatic, Force-enhanced movement, high energy cost, dangerous near dead zones because the Force-assisted leaps fail without warning
- **Form V: Shien/Djem So** — redirecting enemy attacks back at them, physically demanding, favored by Guardians
- **Form VI: Niman** — balanced, generalist, the "diplomat's form" — competent at everything, dominant at nothing
- **Form VII: Juyo/Vaapad** — channels controlled aggression, borders on dark side use, restricted by the Council, the most controversial form

Each form gets a short paragraph: what it looks like, who practices it, and how the Force crisis affects it (forms that rely on Force enhancement are less reliable now).

### Content C: Structural Contradictions (~1.5 pages)

The Jedi Order's internal fractures, stated as specific contradictions players can engage with:

- **Detachment vs. service.** The Code teaches non-attachment, but Jedi form deep bonds with their Padawans, their units, the populations they protect. The Order never resolved this tension. It produced guilt in Jedi who loved and self-deception in Jedi who claimed they didn't.
- **Pacifism vs. militarization.** The Order existed to maintain peace. Then it fought a war. The Revanchists argue the war was necessary. The Council Loyalists argue it was a betrayal. Both are correct.
- **Knowledge vs. control.** The Jedi Archives contained restricted sections. The Council withheld information from Knights it deemed "not ready." This paternalism drove some Jedi to seek knowledge outside the Order, and some of those seekers found the Sith.
- **Universalism vs. institutional loyalty.** The Code says the Force connects all beings. The Order says you should listen to the Council. When these conflict, which do you follow?

## Section 3: Chapter 15 Sith Expansion

**Location:** Integrated into existing Chapter 15 ("The Sith Tradition"). Major expansion of the Keepers of Adas subsection.
**Length:** ~4-5 pages of new material (expanding existing ~2.5 pages to ~7-8 pages total).

### Content A: Expanded Keeper Practices (~3-4 pages)

The Keepers operated through specific practices tied to Korriban's environment:

**The Chaining.** Keepers bound spirits of the dead to specific locations using carved runestones called *tuk'ata markers*. The ritual required blood from a living Sith species member, spoken invocation in Old Sith, and physical contact with the burial site. Spirits didn't serve the Keeper. They answered when they chose, and they lied frequently. Keepers spent decades learning which spirits gave reliable information and which ones baited the living into traps. This was the foundational Keeper skill.

**Meditation of Erosion.** Keepers didn't empty themselves (Jedi method) or rage into power (Imperial Sith method). They sat in concentrated dark side energy and let it permeate them over time. Like stone absorbing heat. The technique deepened a Keeper's Force connection without emotional extremes. The cost was physical: brittle bones, light sensitivity, shortened lifespans. Korriban's ecology gave power and took flesh in return.

**Korriban's ecology as partner.** The Valley of the Dark Lords was an ecosystem, not a cemetery. Dark side energy pooled in specific geological formations. Certain mineral deposits concentrated it; certain cave systems channeled it. Tuk'ata hounds were symbiotic Force predators that fed on the same ambient energy. Shyrack caves served as natural amplifiers for ritual work. Keepers mapped this ecology over millennia the way botanists map a forest. They tracked which tombs were "hot" (saturated, dangerous, powerful) and which were "cold" (depleted, safe, useless). Mutualistic relationship: Keepers maintained the tombs, the tombs sustained the Keepers' power.

**Additional rituals:**
- *The Marking* — scarification ritual for apprentices. Cuts treated with powdered Korriban minerals created permanent sensitivity to dark side concentrations. Marked Keepers felt buried artifacts or active spirits the way some people feel barometric pressure changes.
- *Tomb Singing* — collective vocal ritual at solstice points. Groups chanted in the Old Sith harmonic scale (quarter-tones human vocal cords cannot reproduce). The singing "fed" dormant spirits and prevented them from becoming hostile. After the Exile, when the Keepers stopped singing, the tombs went feral.
- *The Asking* — dangerous divination where a Keeper opened their mind to a specific dead lord's spirit. Unlike the Chaining, the Keeper went to the spirit rather than summoning it. Success: visions of the past or fragments of lost knowledge. Failure: possession, madness, or death. The greatest Keepers performed Askings of Marka Ragnos himself.

### Content B: The Exile's Corruption (~1 page)

What the Dark Jedi Exiles did to each practice. Which survived intact, which got weaponized, which went underground. The Kissai caste preserved some fragments. The Zuguruk alchemists repurposed others. Most died with the practitioners who refused to teach their conquerors.

### Content C: Present-Day Keepers (~0.5 page)

Tiny numbers, operating in secret, reconstructing practices from fragments. The tombs no longer respond the way they used to because the Force is failing. What does a Keeper do when Korriban itself goes quiet?

## Section 4: New Chapter — Sith Sorcery & Alchemy

**Location:** New chapter inserted after current Chapter 15. Becomes Chapter 16. Current chapters 16-21 renumber to 17-22.
**Length:** ~6-7 pages.

### Structure

**Opening Frame (~0.5 page).** Sith sorcery is two disciplines, not one. The Jedi conflated them deliberately because banning something is easier when you give it one name.

**The Kissai School: Ritual Channeling (~2-2.5 pages).**
- Practitioners: Kissai priestly caste of the original Sith species.
- Method: Collective emotional channeling. Groups of 3+ in emotional synchronization. A single Kissai could perform minor workings; real power required the collective.
- Mechanism: Spoken incantations in Old Sith, ritual circles, blood offerings (practitioners' own). Kissai sorcery pushed emotion into the Force and shaped it with collective will. The Force didn't flow through them; they pushed it.
- Capabilities: Perceptual illusions (not holograms — actual alterations affecting Force-sensitives and non-sensitives alike). Battle meditation variants that demoralized enemies. Communication with the dead (related to but distinct from the Keepers' Chaining — louder and less precise). Dark side energy storms across battlefields.
- Visual signature: Shadows that moved wrong, temperature drops, sounds at nausea-inducing frequencies.
- Present day: Almost entirely lost. Fragments in oral traditions among scattered Sith species communities. Occasional catastrophic failures when someone reconstructs a ritual from tomb inscriptions without training.

**The Zuguruk School: Alchemy (~2-2.5 pages).**
- Practitioners: Zuguruk builder caste.
- Method: Infusing physical objects and living creatures with dark side energy through prolonged Force manipulation. Individual work, not collective. Weeks, months, years per creation.
- Mechanism: Forges, laboratories, vivisection chambers. Required specific materials (metals, minerals, biological components from Korriban's geology). Sustained Force channeling while physically working the material.
- Capabilities: Sith swords (Force-saturated metal that resists lightsabers). War beasts (terentateks, sithspawn, weaponized tuk'ata variants). Talismans storing dark side energy. Poisons that attack through the Force and biochemistry simultaneously. Alchemized architecture (the tombs of the Valley are dangerous because the alchemy is baked into the stone).
- Key distinction from Kissai: Zuguruk creations persist after the alchemist dies. Kissai rituals end when practitioners stop. This is why Korriban's tombs remain dangerous millennia later.
- Present day: Most feared school because the products survived when the practitioners didn't. Sith swords, amulets, and corrupted creatures still surface. No one alive fully understands the forging techniques. Partial knowledge circulates — dangerous, not controlled. The Concordat classifies Zuguruk artifacts as military-grade contraband.

**Why Everyone Is Afraid (~1 page).**
- The Jedi: sorcery proves the dark side can do things the light side cannot replicate.
- The Concordat: Zuguruk artifacts are weapons that don't require a Force-sensitive wielder.
- The Revanchists: Revan saw what sorcery did during the Mandalorian Wars and never spoke about it afterward.
- The Mandalorians: fought sithspawn on Korriban's surface and lost companies.
- The Force crisis wildcard: with the Force failing, no one knows what happens to existing sorcery. Do old workings weaken? Destabilize? Does a Zuguruk-forged tomb become more dangerous when ambient Force fluctuates?

## Writing Constraints

All new prose must follow stop-slop rules:
- No filler phrases or throat-clearing openers
- Active voice throughout (human subjects doing things)
- No adverbs
- No em dashes
- No "not X, it's Y" contrasts
- Vary sentence rhythm (no three consecutive sentences of matching length)
- No punchy one-liner paragraph endings
- Specific over abstract (name the thing, don't gesture at it)
- Trust readers (no softening, no hand-holding)
- No pull-quote-sounding lines

All content in Homebrewery markdown format, matching existing conventions: `\page`, `\column`, `{{note}}`, `{{descriptive}}`, `{{footnote}}`, `{{pageNumber,auto}}`, `{{Ch#,tab}}`.
