# The Concordat Era — Phase 1: Design Document

## BOOK THESIS, TONE, AND PLAYER PROMISE

**Thesis:** The Concordat Era is a setting about what happens when the foundations everyone built their lives on turn out to be temporary. The Force is failing. The Republic bargained away its principles for survival. The Mandalorians were right about self-reliance but wrong about everything else. Three governments claim legitimacy; none deserve it without qualification. Players enter a galaxy where the old answers no longer work and no one has agreed on the new ones yet.

**Tone:** Political thriller crossed with frontier survival story, set in a science-fantasy universe where the supernatural infrastructure is collapsing. The mood is post-revolutionary, not post-apocalyptic. Governments function. Trade moves. People argue in cantinas about whether Malak is a tyrant or a realist. The dread is structural, not aesthetic: the galaxy looks mostly normal, but the load-bearing walls are cracked. Think the Directory period after the French Revolution, or the exhausted, paranoid pragmatism of Europe between 1815 and 1848.

**Player Promise:** You will play characters who matter in a galaxy that has not decided what it is yet. Your beliefs, loyalties, and choices will collide with a political order that is still wet cement. You will not be handed a villain to fight. You will find people with defensible positions doing indefensible things, and you will have to decide where you stand. The Force is unreliable, which means your character's skill, relationships, and convictions carry more weight than any supernatural gift. This is a campaign about what you build when the old structures fail.

## REFINED TABLE OF CONTENTS

### Part I: Welcome to the Concordat Era

**Chapter 1 — To the Players**
A short foreword. What kind of game this is. What to expect. An invitation to bring complicated characters with strong opinions.

**Chapter 2 — How to Use This Book**
What this book contains and what it doesn't. What counts as player-safe knowledge. What the campaign rewards. A brief note on system (Fate) without turning into a rules discussion.

**Chapter 3 — The Galaxy at a Glance**
A present-tense snapshot of the Concordat Era in roughly 1,500 words. What is broken. What is contested. What makes this AU distinct from baseline Old Republic expectations. The paragraph you'd read aloud at Session Zero to set the table.

### Part II: How We Got Here

**Chapter 4 — A History of the Concordat Era**
A narrative timeline from the Great Hyperspace War through the current campaign year. Not bullet points — a story with momentum, organized in clear phases:

- Phase I: The Great Hyperspace War and the Breaking of the Force
- Phase II: The Mandalorian Expansion and the Doctrine of Necessary Strength
- Phase III: The Revanchist War (Revan, Malak, and the Counter-Offensive)
- Phase IV: Cathar, the Divergence, and Malak's Single Combat
- Phase V: The Concordat and the Fracture of the Republic
- Phase VI: The Present — Year One of the Concordat Era (Campaign Start)

### Part III: The Galaxy Now

**Chapter 5 — Life in the Concordat Era**
What daily existence feels like. How the damaged Force changes medicine, travel, governance, religion, and trust. What people argue about in cantinas, what they fear, which institutions still function and which are facades. Class, labor, displacement, veterans, refugees.

**Chapter 6 — The Major Powers**
Full faction profiles, each containing: what they claim, what they do, internal contradictions, what ordinary citizens think of them, what kind of character comes from or opposes them, and how they relate to the damaged Force.

- 6.1 — The Concordat (Malak's hybrid state)
- 6.2 — The Revanchist Remnant (Revan's government-in-exile)
- 6.3 — The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition (Canderous Ordo's traditionalists)
- 6.4 — The Jedi Order and Its Fractures
- 6.5 — The Sith Triumvirate and Sith-Aligned Powers
- 6.6 — Independent Powers and Wildcards (Hutt Space, the Exchange, neutral systems, corporate interests)

**Chapter 7 — The Force in Crisis**
What the average person believes is happening to the Force. What Jedi think. What Sith think. What scholars, healers, soldiers, and bartenders think. What Force instability looks like in practice: dead zones, power surges, emotional bleed, failed healing, unreliable precognition. How this changes combat, spirituality, governance, and philosophy. Player-facing: effects without hidden causes.

### Part IV: Places

**Chapter 8 — Worlds, Regions, and Frontiers**
Key planets and sectors relevant to the campaign. Not an encyclopedia — each entry explains why this place matters now, who controls it, what happened there recently, and what kind of trouble finds you there. Priority locations:

- Coruscant (Concordat capital, uneasy seat of power)
- Dxun / Onderon (Mandalorian heartland, site of the Dhas'kara)
- Taris (first Mandalorian conversion, test case for Concordat governance)
- Dantooine (Jedi enclave, Remnant-adjacent, contested)
- Manaan (kolto source, Selkath sovereignty, everyone's leverage point)
- Korriban (Sith tomb world, Triumvirate shadow)
- Cathar (war wound, memorial, political flashpoint)
- The Outer Rim Frontier (resource worlds, lawless sectors, where the campaign likely starts)
- Additional worlds as needed: Serroco, Ossus, Dxun moon, Nar Shaddaa

### Part V: Peoples

**Chapter 9 — Peoples and Species of the Concordat Era**
Substantial, character-creation-focused entries. Each species/people gets: what most people assume about them, what is actually true, how the Concordat Era has changed their situation, internal diversity, and character hooks.

Priority species:

- Humans (the default, but regionally and politically fractured in ways that matter)
- Mandalorians (a culture, not a species — who counts, who doesn't, and why that's a fight)
- Sith Purebloods (diaspora, post-imperial identity, blood ideology, assimilation, subversion of the usual portrayal)
- Twi'leks (exploitation, resilience, diaspora politics)
- Cathar (genocide survivors, cultural rebuilding, political symbolism)
- Selkath (kolto monopoly, neutrality under pressure, oceanic isolationism cracking)
- Miraluka (Force-dependent species in a Force-failing galaxy — existential crisis)
- Echani (martial culture, Mandalorian rivalry, Concordat alignment questions)
- Zabrak / Iridonians (clan structure, mercenary culture, frontier pragmatism)
- Wookiees (Czerka exploitation, honor culture, Concordat/Remnant split)
- Duros (spacefaring tradition, trade disruption, navigational expertise in a fractured galaxy)
- Additional species as needed

### Part VI: Belief and Meaning

**Chapter 10 — Belief, Religion, and Philosophy**
How people make sense of a galaxy where the Force is dying. Jedi orthodoxy and its fractures. Sith philosophy without a Sith Empire. The Mandalorian Doctrine of Necessary Strength as a secular creed. Local religions, syncretic movements, Force cults, anti-Force sentiment. What different groups mean when they say "strength," "freedom," "duty," "sacrifice." Prophecy, heresy, practical faith, and the politics of belief.

### Part VII: The Material Galaxy

**Chapter 11 — War, Trade, Technology, and Survival**
How war is fought when Force users are unreliable. Supply chains for kolto, cortosis, durasteel, fuel, and food. Ship travel in a galaxy with damaged hyperspace routes. Military bureaucracy under the Concordat. Mercenary culture. The economics of a fractured galactic order. Medicine without reliable Force healing. What people eat, how they get paid, how they move between systems, and who profits from the chaos.

### Part VIII: Your Character

**Chapter 12 — Making a Character for the Concordat Era**
Questions to answer during character creation. What kinds of people fit this campaign. Suggested loyalties, secrets, obligations, traumas, ambitions. Group cohesion: why a crew stays together. Advice on building characters with strong beliefs who can still function in a mixed party. Fate-compatible without becoming a Fate chapter.

### Part IX: Where We Begin

**Chapter 13 — Starting Scenarios**
Two campaign entry points, presented with enough context to be compelling without spoiling hidden truths.

- 13.1 — The Freighter Incident (main campaign entry)
- 13.2 — The Windfall Protocol (one-shot / alternate entry, Sith-side kolto station scenario)

Each includes: premise, tone, what kind of characters fit, what a player knows going in, and what themes it foregrounds.

### Appendices

**Appendix A — Glossary and Proper Noun Guide**
Clear, concise definitions for players new to Old Republic material.

**Appendix B — Suggested Reading / Playing**
Optional. For players who want to engage with KOTOR, KOTOR II, or relevant EU material to deepen their understanding. Brief, curated, not required.

## CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER DESIGN BRIEFS

### Chapter 1 — To the Players
**Purpose:** Set expectations. Make the player feel welcomed and oriented, not lectured. Establish that this is a campaign about political complexity and personal stakes, not dungeon-crawling or lightsaber power fantasy.
**Length:** ~500–800 words.
**Key moves:** Name the tone (political, personal, high-stakes). Acknowledge that some players will know KOTOR lore and some won't. Promise that both will find purchase. Invite characters with strong beliefs and messy loyalties. Make clear: there is no designated villain faction.
**Risk to avoid:** Sounding like marketing copy or a mission statement. This should read like a trusted GM talking to you before the first session.

### Chapter 2 — How to Use This Book
**Purpose:** Practical orientation. What's in this book, what's player-safe, what to focus on for character creation.
**Length:** ~400–600 words.
**Key moves:** Tell players this is written from a "reasonably informed citizen" perspective. Flag that mysteries exist and the book preserves them. Point to the chapters most useful for character creation (Chapters 6, 9, 10, 12). Mention Fate briefly as the system without letting the chapter become mechanical.
**Risk to avoid:** Becoming a meta-essay about the book itself. Keep it functional.

### Chapter 3 — The Galaxy at a Glance
**Purpose:** The elevator pitch in prose form. A player who reads only this chapter should understand what the Concordat Era is, why it's unstable, and what makes it different from "standard Old Republic."
**Length:** ~1,500–2,000 words.
**Key moves:** Present tense. Name all five major powers quickly and sharply. Establish the Force crisis as a material fact, not background flavor. Make the reader feel the political instability as something that affects daily life, not just senate votes. End with a sense of possibility: the galaxy hasn't decided what it is yet.
**Risk to avoid:** Becoming a condensed history chapter. This is a snapshot, not a summary. Save causation for Chapter 4.

### Chapter 4 — A History of the Concordat Era
**Purpose:** The backbone of the setting. A player who reads this understands how the galaxy got here and why each faction believes what it believes.
**Length:** ~5,000–7,000 words. The longest chapter.
**Key moves:** Write it as a narrative with forward momentum. Each phase should build on the last. The Mandalorian Expansion should feel like a political earthquake, not a list of conquered planets. Revan and Malak should feel like real people making defensible choices that lead somewhere dark. The Concordat itself should feel like a genuine, imperfect solution to a real crisis, not a plot device. Cathar should land as a turning point without being reduced to a single atrocity.
**Risk to avoid:** Lore-dump voice. Every paragraph must earn its place by changing the reader's understanding. If a paragraph only restates what came before or fills space with mood, cut it.

### Chapter 5 — Life in the Concordat Era
**Purpose:** Ground the setting in material reality. A player who reads this knows what it feels like to live in this galaxy — not just the politics, but the texture.
**Length:** ~2,500–3,500 words.
**Key moves:** Cover medicine, trade, travel, food, labor, class, veterans, refugees, policing, corruption. Use the damaged Force as a lens: what breaks when Force healing fails? When precognition is unreliable? When the infrastructure that relied on Jedi mediation no longer functions? Give players material to build characters from: what job might your character have? What does poverty look like here? What does privilege look like?
**Risk to avoid:** Becoming a generic "daily life in sci-fi" section. Every detail should connect to the Concordat Era's specific fractures.

### Chapter 6 — The Major Powers
**Purpose:** The faction chapter. Players need to understand each faction well enough to feel loyalty, opposition, or complicated ambivalence.
**Length:** ~5,000–7,000 words total across all subsections.
**Key moves per faction:**
- **The Concordat:** Malak's hybrid state. A republic in name, an authoritarian pragmatist regime in practice. Functional, stable, and eroding civil liberties. What Napoleon's Consulate would look like in space. Internal tensions between Malak's military pragmatists, former Republic administrators trying to preserve institutions, and Mandalorian clans chafing under civilian oversight.
- **The Revanchist Remnant:** Revan's government-in-exile. Claims to be the true Republic. Twenty systems, guerrilla warfare, Jedi Council support. Revan himself is increasingly absent, chasing ancient technology in the Unknown Regions. The people running the Remnant day-to-day are true believers holding a losing position. Internal tension: how long do you fight for a leader who isn't there?
- **The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition:** Canderous Ordo's traditionalists. Reject the Concordat as dishonor. Reject the Remnant as weakness. Want to prove Mandalorian independence through combat. Raid both sides. Internal tension: honor culture vs. material survival. Not everyone who followed Canderous agrees on what "true Mandalorian values" means.
- **The Jedi Order:** Fractured, scared, and hemorrhaging members. The Council debates whether using the Force accelerates its death. The Sentinel Program arms non-Jedi Force sensitives — a terrible compromise. Some Jedi joined Revan. Some joined Malak. The ones who stayed argue about everything. Internal tension: institutional paralysis vs. individual conscience.
- **The Sith Triumvirate:** Darth Traya (Kreia) and her project to hasten the Force's death. Adapted for this AU — no Malachor V as origin point. Nihilus and Sion exist but their origins and relationship to the Force crisis must be rethought for this timeline. The Triumvirate operates in shadow. Most people don't know it exists. Player-facing: present the rumors, the edges, the whispers. Don't reveal the full architecture.
- **Independent Powers:** Hutt Space, the Exchange, Czerka Corporation, neutral systems. The people who profit from the chaos or try to stay out of it. These matter because players may come from or operate within these spaces.

**Risk to avoid:** Writing any faction as a monolith. Every faction should contain people a player could admire and people a player could oppose.

### Chapter 7 — The Force in Crisis
**Purpose:** The single most important chapter for distinguishing this AU from baseline Star Wars. If a player reads only one chapter beyond the overview, it should be this one.
**Length:** ~2,500–3,500 words.
**Key moves:** Layer the explanations. Start with what ordinary people notice (healers failing, Jedi acting strange, dead zones on battlefields). Move to what Jedi and Sith believe is happening (competing theories, none confirmed). Describe practical effects: combat without reliable precognition, medicine without Force healing, governance without Jedi mediation, spirituality without a responsive Force. End with what this means for player characters: your relationship with the Force is personal, uncertain, and politically charged.
**Risk to avoid:** Revealing the GM-level truth about what's causing the crisis. This chapter presents the mystery, not the answer. Also avoid making the Force crisis sound "cool" — make it feel like a genuine loss that reshapes how people live.

### Chapter 8 — Worlds, Regions, and Frontiers
**Purpose:** Geographic grounding. Players need to picture the places where the campaign happens.
**Length:** ~3,500–5,000 words.
**Key moves:** Each world entry should answer: Who controls it? What happened here recently? Why does it matter now? What kind of trouble finds you here? What does it feel like? Prioritize worlds relevant to the starting scenarios and the factional map. Manaan gets special attention because kolto is the galaxy's most contested resource. Taris gets attention as the test case for whether the Concordat model works. Dantooine matters as a Jedi world caught between factions.
**Risk to avoid:** Planet-of-the-hat entries. Every world should feel shaped by the Concordat Era's specific pressures, not just recycled from canon descriptions.

### Chapter 9 — Peoples and Species
**Purpose:** Character creation fuel. The most directly useful chapter for a player building a character.
**Length:** ~4,000–6,000 words.
**Key moves:** For each species, cover what most people assume, what's actually true, how the Concordat Era changed things, and character hooks. Sith Purebloods get the deepest treatment: diaspora, blood ideology, assimilation, post-imperial identity fracture, intermarriage, cultural memory, how others see them vs. how they see themselves. Mandalorians get treatment as a culture rather than a species. Miraluka get attention as a Force-dependent people facing existential crisis in a Force-failing galaxy.
**Risk to avoid:** Monoculture descriptions. "The Cathar are fierce warriors" is exactly what this chapter must not sound like. Every species entry should feel like it describes a people, not a stat block.

### Chapter 10 — Belief, Religion, and Philosophy
**Purpose:** Interior life. What people believe and why. This is where the setting's ideological texture lives.
**Length:** ~2,500–3,500 words.
**Key moves:** Cover Jedi orthodoxy and its fractures. Sith philosophy as a living tradition, not a caricature. The Mandalorian Doctrine of Necessary Strength as a secular creed with real adherents outside Mandalorian culture. Local and syncretic beliefs. Anti-Force secular movements. What different traditions say about suffering, duty, freedom, and power. How the Force crisis has created new heresies and new orthodoxies.
**Risk to avoid:** Reducing belief systems to bullet points. These should feel like things real people argue about, not faction abilities.

### Chapter 11 — War, Trade, Technology, and Survival
**Purpose:** Material reality. How the galaxy functions (or fails to function) at the level of supply chains, medicine, transportation, and economics.
**Length:** ~2,500–3,500 words.
**Key moves:** Kolto economics. Cortosis as a strategic resource. How hyperspace travel works when routes are damaged. Military structure under the Concordat vs. the Remnant vs. Neo-Mandalorian raiding parties. Mercenary culture. What happens to veterans. How medicine works when Force healing is unreliable. The role of droids when organic labor is scarce.
**Risk to avoid:** Becoming a technical manual. Every logistical detail should connect to a human (or alien) consequence that matters for play.

### Chapter 12 — Making a Character
**Purpose:** Direct, practical guidance for character creation.
**Length:** ~2,000–2,500 words.
**Key moves:** A list of questions to answer (not a questionnaire — narrative prompts that generate interesting answers). Suggested character types that fit the campaign. Advice on loyalties: who do you work for? Who do you owe? Who do you fear? Advice on secrets: what does your character hide? What do they believe that might be wrong? Group cohesion: why does this crew stick together? Mention Fate aspects and skills lightly without turning it into a mechanics chapter.
**Risk to avoid:** Generic "here are some archetypes" advice. Every suggestion should be specific to the Concordat Era.

### Chapter 13 — Starting Scenarios
**Purpose:** Campaign entry points. A player reading these should feel excited to play and have a clear sense of what their first session involves.
**Length:** ~1,500–2,500 words total.
**Key moves:**
- **The Freighter Incident:** (Main campaign start.) Needs development from your notes — I'll need more detail from you on what this scenario involves. Based on the campaign's tone, I'm assuming a politically charged inciting event that pulls a mixed crew into factional conflict. The entry should cover: where you are, what you know, what just went wrong, and why you can't walk away.
- **The Windfall Protocol:** (One-shot / alternate entry.) Adapted from your kolto base scenario. Sith-side characters waking in a compromised kolto station with contradictory missions. Present the premise and tone without revealing the empathy bleed mechanic or the modified kolto's full properties. Player-facing: you wake up in a dark station with people you don't know, and you all have different orders.
**Risk to avoid:** Spoiling scenario secrets. These are invitations, not briefings.

### Appendix A — Glossary
**Purpose:** Reference. Every proper noun a new player might stumble on, defined in one to three sentences.
**Length:** ~1,500–2,500 words depending on entry count.
**Key moves:** Alphabetical. Concise. Each entry should function independently. Cross-reference sparingly.

### Appendix B — Suggested Reading/Playing
**Purpose:** Optional enrichment for interested players.
**Length:** ~300–500 words.
**Key moves:** Recommend KOTOR I and II as games. Mention relevant comics or novels briefly. Frame as "if you want more texture" rather than "required homework."

## OPEN QUESTIONS AND LORE DECISIONS TO RESOLVE BEFORE DRAFTING

### 1. The Freighter Incident
Your source material mentions this as a starting scenario but doesn't describe it. I need:
- What is the Freighter Incident?
- Where does it take place?
- Who are the PCs in this scenario? (Mixed faction? Single faction? Civilian?)
- What's the inciting event?
- What makes it a good campaign opener?

### 2. The Sith Triumvirate — AU Adaptation
In canon, Nihilus and Sion are products of Malachor V's Mass Shadow Generator. In your AU, Malachor V doesn't happen. You've noted the Triumvirate is "thematically different" in this version. I need:
- How did Nihilus become a wound in the Force in this AU? What event or process created him?
- How does Sion's condition originate without Malachor?
- Is the Trayus Academy still on Malachor, or located elsewhere?
- How public is Traya/Kreia's existence? Is she known to any faction, or operating entirely in shadow?
- What is the Triumvirate's relationship to the Great Hyperspace War's Force damage? Are they products of it? Exploiters of it? Both?

### 3. The Force Crisis — Mechanism and Scope
Your material establishes the Force is "broken" and "dying" but I need to calibrate:
- Is this a gradual worsening, or has it plateaued?
- Are there geographic patterns? (Dead zones near old Sith ritual sites? Spreading outward from the Core? Random?)
- Can Force users still use the Force at all, or is it intermittent? What's the baseline reliability?
- Is the damage to the Living Force, the Cosmic Force, or both? (Or do people in this AU even make that distinction?)
- Is there any faction or group that has found a partial workaround?
- What does the average non-Force-sensitive person notice? Just that Jedi are less effective? Or are there material effects they can observe?

### 4. The Timeline — Specific Years
Your material uses relative dating ("Year One," "Year Four," "Year Seven"). I need to decide:
- Do we use a Concordat-relative calendar (Year 1 of the Concordat Era, etc.)?
- What is the current campaign year? (How many years since the Concordat was signed?)
- What year abbreviation system do we use? (CE for Concordat Era? Something else?)

### 5. Malak — Character and Governance
- How visible is Malak's authoritarianism? Does he maintain democratic theater (elections, a functioning Senate), or has he dispensed with the pretense?
- What is Malak's personal attitude toward Force users within the Concordat? Your material mentions "Force-sensitive individuals increasingly monitored." How far has this gone?
- Does Malak use the Force himself, or has he partially or fully abandoned it?
- What is Malak's public image vs. private reality?

### 6. Revan — Absence and Obsession
- How absent is Revan from the Remnant at campaign start? Gone for weeks? Months? Years?
- What is Revan chasing? The Star Forge specifically, or something broader?
- Does Revan communicate with the Remnant at all?
- Who runs the Remnant day-to-day in Revan's absence?
- Has Revan shown any signs of dark side corruption, or is this a different kind of deterioration?

### 7. The Jedi Sentinel Program
- How many Sentinels has the program produced?
- What is the failure rate? (You mention "half-trained Force users without Jedi ethics.")
- Have any Sentinels gone rogue, defected, or caused incidents?
- How does the wider galaxy view the Sentinel Program? Is it public knowledge?

### 8. Cathar
- Your material references "Cathar happens" as a turning point in Year 4. In canon, Cathar was a Mandalorian genocide site. What exactly happened at Cathar in this AU?
- Is it the same event (Mandalorian massacre of the Cathar people), or has the event been adapted?
- What was Malak's response to Cathar, and how did it shape his later decisions?

### 9. Mandalorian Culture — Internal Detail
- Ka'halar (life-debt) and Dhas'kara (single combat challenge) — are these your original terms or adapted from existing Mando'a? Are there other Mandalorian cultural terms we should establish?
- What is Mandalore the Vindicated's status after the Concordat? Alive and bound by ka'halar — but where? Is he a political figure within the Concordat, or sidelined?
- How do Concordat-aligned Mandalorian clans (Cassus Fett, Veera Ordo) relate to Malak's government in practice? Are they military partners, subordinates, or something more complicated?

### 10. Kolto and Medicine
- Is kolto still the primary healing substance, or has bacta emerged as an alternative?
- How has the Force crisis affected kolto's effectiveness? (Your Windfall scenario implies a connection between kolto and the Living Force.)
- Is Manaan neutral, or has it been pressured into alignment?

### 11. Scope and Scale
- How many systems does the Concordat control? (Rough order of magnitude.)
- How many does the Remnant hold? (Your material says "maybe twenty.")
- How large is the Neo-Mandalorian Coalition's territory?
- What percentage of the galaxy is ungoverned, contested, or controlled by minor powers?

### 12. Admiral Saul Karath
- In canon, Karath is a villain who defects to the Sith. In your AU, he extracts Revan and helps form the Remnant. What is Karath's current role?
- Is he a true believer in the Remnant, or is he a military pragmatist who chose the least-bad option?
- What is his relationship with Revan now?

## CANON RESEARCH AREAS THAT WOULD STRENGTHEN THE BOOK

### 1. Mandalorian Clans and Social Structure
Deeper research into canon Mandalorian clan structure (Ordo, Vizla, Skirata, Fett, Kryze, Cadera) would let me write more textured Mandalorian material. Your AU already names several clans and gives them distinct political positions. I should map which canon associations players might bring (Fett = bounty hunter lineage, Kryze = pacifist reformers in later eras, Vizla = Death Watch) and decide how the AU handles those expectations.

### 2. Selkath Society and Manaan Politics
The kolto scenario makes Selkath culture load-bearing for the setting. Canon gives us some material (the Ahto City courts, the progenitor creature in KOTOR's underwater sequences, the Order of Shasa). Research here would help build a richer Selkath entry and make Manaan feel like a real place with internal politics, not just "the kolto planet."

### 3. Sith Pureblood Culture and Caste System
Canon establishes a caste system (Kissai priests, Massassi warriors, Zuguruk engineers) and a complex history of interbreeding with Human Dark Jedi exiles. This is essential for writing the subverted Sith Pureblood entry. Research into how SWTOR handles Pureblood identity would help me write the diaspora and post-imperial material you've requested.

### 4. The Echani and Their Relationship to Combat
Canon Echani culture treats combat as communication and expression. Players familiar with Brianna (the Handmaiden from KOTOR II) or General Yusanis will bring expectations. Research here helps write an Echani entry that respects the canon while adapting it for the Concordat Era.

### 5. KOTOR II Philosophy and Kreia's Arguments
Kreia/Traya's philosophical positions are central to this AU. Her arguments about the Force as enslavement, about the will of the Force overriding individual choice, about strength through self-reliance — these are the intellectual engine of the Sith Triumvirate's project and echo in the Mandalorian Doctrine of Necessary Strength. Close research into her dialogue and philosophy would strengthen Chapters 7 and 10.

### 6. Czerka Corporation
Czerka appears in both KOTOR games as a ruthless megacorporation involved in slavery, resource extraction, and weapons dealing. In a fractured Concordat Era, corporate actors like Czerka would fill power vacuums. Research into Czerka's operations would strengthen Chapter 6 (Independent Powers) and Chapter 11 (Trade and Economics).

### 7. Specific World Details
For Chapter 8, I'd benefit from deeper research on:
- **Taris** (pre-bombardment social structure, the Undercity, class divisions)
- **Onderon** (Beast Riders, the Onderonian monarchy, Dxun's role)
- **Nar Shaddaa** (criminal economy, refugee populations, Exchange operations)
- **Ossus** (Jedi library world, post-burning condition)
- **Korriban** (tomb valley, academy, spiritual significance)

### 8. Force Traditions Beyond Jedi and Sith
Canon establishes several non-Jedi, non-Sith Force traditions in this era: the Jal Shey, the Luka Sene (Miraluka), the Baran Do (Kel Dor), the Zeison Sha, the Aing-Tii. Any of these could enrich Chapter 10 (Belief) and provide character hooks for players who want Force-sensitive characters outside the Jedi/Sith binary.

### 9. Hyperspace Route Politics
Canon establishes that hyperspace lanes are strategic assets. The Hydian Way, the Perlemian Trade Route, and the Mandalorian Road matter for the factional map. Research into which routes connect which major systems would strengthen the geographic and economic material in Chapters 8 and 11.

### 10. Droid Culture and Labor
In a galaxy with disrupted organic labor and unreliable Force infrastructure, droids matter more. Research into how droids function in the Old Republic era (HK-series, utility droids, droid rights) would add texture to Chapters 5 and 11.
