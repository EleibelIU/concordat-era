# THE CONCORDAT ERA

## A Setting Book for the Concordat Wars Campaign

---

# PART I: WELCOME

---

## Chapter 1: To the Players

You are about to play in a galaxy where the old certainties have failed.

Twelve years ago, a war between the Republic and the Sith Empire cracked the Force itself. Five years after that, a second war between the Republic and the Mandalorian clans cracked everything else: the government, the economy, the military, the Jedi Order, and any remaining agreement about how civilization ought to work.

The Concordat Era is what grew in the wreckage. A new government, forged from a battlefield bargain between a Jedi general and a Mandalorian warlord, holds the galaxy's center. Two rival factions contest its authority from the margins. The Jedi Order is fractured. The Sith, presumed destroyed, are not gone. And the Force continues to deteriorate in ways no one fully understands, which means that medicine, travel, communication, warfare, religion, and daily life are all becoming less reliable in ways that most people can feel but few can explain.

All three major governments believe they're saving the galaxy. All three have done things that demand accounting. The Concordat provides stability through authoritarianism. The Revanchist Remnant fights for democratic ideals while its leader disappears into obsession. The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition defends a code of honor that may be killing its own people. A hidden faction works toward an end none of the others see coming.

Your characters enter this world with their own histories, loyalties, and convictions. Some of you will have fought in the Mandalorian Wars. Some of you will have grown up during them. Some of you will be too young to remember a galaxy that worked properly and will assume this is normal.

Bring characters who believe things. Characters who owe debts they can't repay. Characters with scars, grudges, obligations, and reasons to get out of bed in the morning that have nothing to do with saving the galaxy. The campaign will give you opportunities to shape the fate of governments and peoples, but it will also ask you to decide what your character does when the kolto runs out, when the navigation computer fails in a dead zone, when the officer across the table offers a deal that would solve everything if you could live with the terms.

If you know the Knights of the Old Republic games, the Old Republic MMO, or the Expanded Universe material set in this era, you will recognize names, places, and ideas adapted for this setting. The Concordat Era is not a retelling of those stories. It is an alternate history that asks: what if the choices went differently? What if the war never cleanly ended? What if the people you expected to become heroes or villains became something more complicated instead?

If you don't know any of that material, you lose nothing. Every proper noun in this book is explained when it first appears. You do not need homework to play. You need a character with opinions and a willingness to see where those opinions take you.

The galaxy has not decided what it is yet. That decision belongs to the people living in it, and for the next several dozen sessions, some of those people are yours.

---

## Chapter 2: How to Use This Book

This book describes the Concordat Era as a reasonably informed person living in it would understand it. The history is what most educated citizens were taught. The faction descriptions reflect public knowledge, common opinion, and the kind of detail a well-traveled spacer or a curious university student might have. Some of it is accurate. Some of it is propaganda that everyone has heard so often it passes for truth. Some of it is genuinely contested, and the book will tell you when reasonable people disagree.

There are things this book does not contain. Hidden conspiracies that no one in the galaxy has uncovered. The true answer to what is killing the Force. The secret plans of people who have successfully kept their secrets. Your GM has that information. You will discover it in play, and the discoveries will mean more because you didn't read them in advance.

**For character creation,** start with Chapter 3 for the broad picture, then read the factions in Chapter 6 to find where your character fits (or doesn't fit) in the political landscape. Chapter 9, on species and peoples, will help you understand your character's cultural background. Chapter 12 offers direct guidance for building a character suited to this campaign.

**For understanding the setting,** read as much or as little as you want. Chapter 4 provides the full history. Chapter 5 describes daily life. Chapter 7 covers the Force crisis. None of these chapters will spoil the campaign's surprises, but together they'll give you the texture to make grounded, specific choices in play.

**On the Fate system:** this campaign uses Fate, but this book is not a Fate rulebook. You will find references to aspects, skills, and stunts in Chapter 12 where they're relevant to character creation, but the setting material is system-agnostic. The political and cultural details matter more than any mechanical element. If you understand who your character is, what they believe, and who they owe, the system will follow.

**A note on calendar dates:** this book uses two dating systems. BBY (Before the Battle of Yavin) is the standard galactic reference calendar used by historians and archivists. The Concordat government uses its own calendar measured from the signing of the Concordat: 1 CE, 2 CE, and so on. In practice, which calendar a person uses tells you who they work for. Concordat officials say "Year Four." Remnant sympathizers say "3952 BBY." Mandalorians track time by their own reckoning. Your character's choice of calendar is a small, free piece of worldbuilding.

# PART II: HOW WE GOT HERE

---

## Chapter 3: The Galaxy at a Glance

The year is 3952 BBY. The fourth year of the Concordat Era. Twelve years since the Great Hyperspace War broke the Force. The galaxy functions, barely, and no one agrees on what should come next.

### Three Governments, No Consensus

The **Concordat** governs from Coruscant. Its leader is Supreme Chancellor-General Malak, a former Jedi general who ended the Mandalorian Wars by defeating Mandalore the Vindicated in single combat and forging an alliance between Republic administration and Mandalorian military strength. The Concordat holds the Core Worlds, most of the Inner Rim, and significant portions of the Mid Rim. It provides stability, infrastructure, and a functional economy. It also monitors Force-sensitive citizens, appoints military governors to contested systems, and maintains emergency powers with no expiration date. Malak wears prosthetics where his jaw and arm used to be, the price paid for beating Mandalore with iron rather than the Force. Half the galaxy calls him a savior. The other half calls him a dictator. Both halves are working with incomplete information.

The **Revanchist Remnant** holds twenty-three systems in the Outer Rim and claims to be the legitimate Republic. Its figurehead is Revan, the philosopher-general who led the first Jedi revolt against the Council's inaction during the Mandalorian Wars. Revan spent two years in the Unknown Regions chasing ancient technology and has just returned, diminished and obsessed. The Remnant's actual operations are managed by Admiral Kaeranth, a career naval officer who extracted Revan from the Unknown Regions and keeps the faction alive through guerrilla warfare, intelligence operations, and the moral authority of being the government that didn't compromise with the people who burned Cathar. The Remnant fights for democratic principles. Its military strength is forty capital ships and a network of hidden bases. Its greatest weapon is the fact that every time the Concordat overreaches, someone in the affected system starts listening to Remnant recruiters.

The **Neo-Mandalorian Coalition** answers to Canderous Ordo, a Mandalorian traditionalist who rejected the Concordat as a betrayal of warrior honor. The Coalition holds no fixed territory. It raids from hidden bases across the Outer Rim and Wild Space, hitting both Concordat and Remnant shipping. The Neo-Mandalorians fight for the principle that strength must be earned through combat, not bargained away in treaties. Their ranks include traditionalist clans, mercenary companies, ideological purists, and people who ended up with the Coalition because no one else would have them. Life in the Coalition is hard, proud, and increasingly unsustainable.

None of these governments commands universal loyalty. Citizens across hundreds of worlds make daily choices about which authority to obey, which to resist, and which to ignore. On the frontier, the answer is often "none of the above."

### The Force Is Dying

Something is wrong with the Force, and no one agrees on what it is, how far it will go, or whether it can be stopped.

Jedi healers find their abilities unreliable. A technique that worked yesterday fails today, and a patient dies on a table that should have been routine. Seers report fragmented visions, contradictory prophecies, and stretches of silence where the Force offers nothing. Combat-trained Jedi feel their precognition stutter in the middle of a fight, and soldiers who depended on that precognition to stay alive have learned to flinch whenever their Jedi commander pauses.

Across the galaxy, dead zones mark places where the Force has gone entirely absent. Some of these zones date to the Great Hyperspace War, where Sith rituals tore holes in the Force's fabric. Others appeared during the Mandalorian Wars, in the wake of the Revanchists' coordinated Force strikes. In a dead zone, lightsabers still cut (they're plasma weapons, powered by crystals, not the Force itself), but everything else fails. Precognition. Telekinesis. Healing. Force-sensitive species like the Miraluka, who perceive the world through the Force rather than through eyes, go blind.

The zones are growing. Slowly, but measurably. Hyperspace navigation, which relies on routes mapped through Force-sensitive cartography stretching back millennia, has become unreliable near dead-zone boundaries. Ships drop out of hyperspace days early. Some don't drop out at all.

For most people, the Force crisis is a practical problem before it is a spiritual one. Medicine is more expensive because kolto (the galaxy's primary healing substance) can't be supplemented by Force healing the way it used to be. Travel is less reliable because hyperspace routes are degrading. Governance is harder because Jedi mediators, who once resolved disputes through Force-enhanced diplomacy, can no longer guarantee results. The old systems are failing, and the replacements aren't built yet.

For Force-sensitive people, the crisis is personal. Imagine losing your hearing in a world built for music. The Force was the medium through which you understood reality, and it's going away. Some Force users have adapted, learning to work with intermittent access. Some have been permanently severed, left with the memory of a sense they no longer possess. Some are angry. Some are afraid. Some have found new philosophies (the Mandalorian Doctrine of Necessary Strength has converts among former Jedi who've decided that the Force was always a crutch). Some have found new masters (the shadows at the galaxy's edges offer power to those willing to pay for it).

### A Galaxy in Transition

The Concordat Era is not a post-apocalypse. The holonet still broadcasts. Freighters still carry cargo. Cantinas still serve drinks. But the foundations have shifted, and everyone can feel it.

A merchant on Taris negotiates trade permits with a Mandalorian customs officer where a Republic bureaucrat used to sit. A veteran of the Ashen Campaigns works as a dock loader on Nar Shaddaa because the Revanchist military doesn't exist anymore and the Concordat doesn't want soldiers who know what they did. A Sith Pureblood professor on Coruscant teaches ancient history at the university and wears long sleeves to cover the red skin that makes strangers nervous. A Cathar teenager on a refugee transport practices with a vibroknife because Mandalorians destroyed her homeworld and the Republic didn't stop them and no one is going to protect her so she'll protect herself.

The old Republic is gone. What replaces it is still being decided. The major powers make their cases through armies, diplomats, spies, propaganda, and the occasional assassination. The minor powers make their cases through survival, commerce, crime, and the stubborn insistence of people who refuse to be governed by anyone they didn't choose.

Your characters walk into this. Armed, opinionated, and connected to a galaxy that hasn't finished breaking and hasn't started healing.

## Chapter 4: A History of the Concordat Era

### Prologue: The War That Broke the Sky

Twelve years before the present, the Sith Empire invaded the Republic.

The Sith Empire was not a vague threat from ancient legend. It was a state, governed from Korriban and Ziost in the Stygian Caldera, ruled by a species called the Sith and by the hybrid descendants of Dark Jedi exiles who had conquered that species seven thousand years earlier. Under Naga Sadow, its Dark Lord, the Empire launched a coordinated assault on the Republic's Core Worlds, its Jedi defenders, and the basic structure of the Force itself.

Sith sorcerers practiced ritual warfare at a scale the galaxy had never encountered. On Ossus, where the Jedi maintained their greatest library, ten thousand Jedi stood against the Sith war machine. When the massed rituals reached their peak, the defenders felt something that no Jedi had ever felt before: silence. The Force vanished from the battlefield. Lightsabers flickered. Precognition evaporated. Healers watched patients die under their hands. The silence lasted minutes, and in those minutes, the war's survivors would later say, the galaxy's old age ended.

The Republic won because the Sith rituals were unstable. The sorcery consumed its own practitioners. Naga Sadow's forces collapsed, and the Republic counter-invasion drove the survivors into the Unknown Regions. The Treaty of Coruscant ended hostilities.

The victory cost the Republic its confidence and the Jedi their certainty. Trade routes were severed. Sectors were depopulated. The Core Worlds bore bombardment scars. And the Force, the invisible architecture on which ten thousand years of civilization rested, was cracked. Places where the rituals had been performed became dead zones: regions where the Force was absent or corrupted, where Jedi abilities failed, where the natural connection between living things and the Force was gone.

The Republic needed to rebuild. It needed cortosis for hull plating, durasteel for infrastructure, kolto for millions of wounded, fuel for the shattered fleet, and food for the displaced. The richest resource worlds in the Outer Rim were controlled by people who didn't answer to Coruscant.

The Mandalorians.

### The Doctrine of Necessary Strength

Three months after the treaty, Mandalore the Vindicated summoned every major clan to the jungle moon of Dxun. Ordo, Vizla, Skirata, Cadera, Fett, Kryze. They expected a call to arms. He showed them a hologram instead.

The recording came from a battlefield unit on Ossus. A Twi'lek Jedi Master named Rell Odar, one of the finest duelists of his generation, fought Sith warriors with extraordinary skill. His lightsaber blurred. His movements were faster than human reflex allowed. Then the Sith ritual hit. The lightsaber flickered and died. The speed vanished. The precognition evaporated. Rell Odar stood confused, briefly and entirely human, and blaster bolts cut him down.

Mandalore replayed the recording three times.

His argument was simple. For ten thousand years, the galaxy had built everything on the assumption that the Force existed and that Force users would protect civilization. The Sith had just proved that assumption could be destroyed in seconds. The Jedi Council debated behind closed doors while worlds starved. The Republic Senate issued decrees no fleet could enforce.

The Mandalorians had never relied on the Force. They fought with iron, discipline, and will. They were the only people qualified to teach the galaxy what survival looked like without supernatural crutches.

Jaden the Elder of Clan Skirata put it into words that would reshape galactic politics for the next decade: *Strength is not given. Strength is forged. Those who rely on gifts from the cosmos will be weak when those gifts are taken away. Only those who forge their own strength can survive in a galaxy without mercy.*

The clans adopted the Doctrine of Necessary Strength within days. Not unanimously. Clan Kryze argued that demonstration didn't require siege. Cassus Fett saw territorial expansion dressed in philosophy and said so, earning contempt and respect in equal measure for his candor. But the core held. The Mandalorians would move outward. The galaxy would learn or fall.

### The Expansion (3962-3960 BBY)

The Mandalorians began with Taris, and they didn't fight the way anyone expected.

They surrounded the ecumenopolis, a planet-spanning city of billions, and issued a challenge: prove you can defend yourselves without the Republic. Then they waited. For four months, Coruscant promised relief that never arrived. When the Tarisian militia finally attempted to break the siege, the Mandalorians crushed them in six hours.

Then they offered the survivors training.

A third of the defeated militia accepted. Weeks later, those soldiers returned: disciplined, effective, dangerous. They lost the rematch, but they fought well enough to earn something the Republic had never offered them. Mandalorian respect.

Taris surrendered not to conquest but to a proposition. The Mandalorians would provide protection that actually showed up. In exchange, Taris would provide resources and commit to self-defense infrastructure. Two-thirds of the planet accepted. The Undercity, home to the displaced populations the Republic had already abandoned, accepted almost unanimously. For people Coruscant had never bothered to protect, Mandalorian pragmatism was an improvement over Republic promises.

Over the next three years, the template repeated across eighty systems. Onderon fell through combat: the beast-riders of Onderon fought the Mandalorian advance so fiercely that half of them earned alliance rather than subjugation. Clan Ordo's young commander, Canderous Ordo, lost thirty warriors in the first week of jungle fighting and called it the best combat he'd ever seen. Dantooine fell without a shot: the Mandalorians established camp near the Jedi Enclave and waited while colonists walked across the plain to learn from instructors who taught blade combat rather than meditation. Serroco fell through a handshake: the native Stereb population traded cortosis mining access for a garrison the Republic had never provided.

The Mandalorians could take worlds brilliantly. They could not run them at all. Taris experienced food distribution failures within months. Trade networks collapsed under military administration. Mandalorian culture had no framework for civilian governance, economic management, or the bureaucratic machinery a modern world requires. Cassus Fett, a cold logistician more comfortable with spreadsheets than honor codes, argued that the clans needed to recruit administrators. The traditionalists called this weakness. Fett called it mathematics. Veera Ordo, Canderous's younger sister, supported Fett. She'd argued for administrative partnership since before the expansion began and suggested that the Doctrine of Necessary Strength applied to governance: if the Mandalorians couldn't govern, they should learn, not pretend the problem was beneath them.

The Republic Senate, meanwhile, debated jurisdiction while worlds fell. The Jedi Council argued about whether the Force was too damaged to risk using in combat, whether intervention might widen the cracks, whether they were even capable of fighting when half their combat-trained Knights could no longer trust their own precognition.

### The Razing of Cathar (3959 BBY)

The Cathar refused the Mandalorian offer. Not through negotiation. Not through organized militia resistance. The Cathar fought a guerrilla campaign: decentralized, brutal, and devastating to Mandalorian supply lines. They used their homeworld's dense forests and canyon systems to ambush patrols. They poisoned water supplies. They targeted officers.

The Mandalorian response escalated over months. Mandalorian doctrine held that a people who refused to fight in the open, with identifiable combatants, forfeited the protections of honorable war. The Cathar fought as insurgents. The Mandalorians treated them as insurgents.

Cassus Fett commanded the Cathar operation. He separated populations from their forest strongholds through controlled burning. He established concentration zones where civilians were held while his forces swept guerrilla networks. His methods were efficient, systematic, and stripped of any pretense that the Doctrine of Necessary Strength cared about the people being taught.

Fett did not order the massacre that followed. What happened at the Cathar Concentration at Ves'kaan was a breakdown of discipline, not a strategy. Forces under Clan Jendri, enraged after three months of ambushes that killed their chieftain's children, entered the Ves'kaan zone and killed approximately fourteen thousand Cathar civilians over two days.

Fett classified the event as "collateral pacification" in his official report. He did not punish the perpetrators. He did not condemn the action. He noted in private correspondence to Mandalore that "the Cathar problem is resolved" and moved to the next system.

The galaxy learned about Ves'kaan from refugees who escaped through the forest. A Cathar teenager named Sylvar Juhani recorded the aftermath with a stolen Mandalorian unit: burning villages, mass graves, Mandalorian warriors firing into crowds. It was the first time most Republic citizens saw what the Expansion looked like when a world refused to cooperate.

### The Revanchists (3959 BBY)

Revan watched the Cathar footage in the Coruscant Temple. He'd been arguing with the Council for a year. The footage ended the argument.

He walked out of the Temple with four hundred Jedi behind him.

Some followed because they believed the Council's inaction was a betrayal of everything the Jedi were supposed to be. Some followed because they were young and angry and Revan was the most charismatic person they'd ever met. Some followed because they'd been watching the Force deteriorate and decided that passivity was killing them faster than war.

Revan addressed his followers in the plaza outside the Temple. He did not give a rousing speech. He said: "The Council believes that inaction will preserve the Force. I believe that the Force is dying because we have allowed suffering we could have prevented. Both positions may be wrong. But only one of them requires us to do nothing while fourteen thousand people are buried in mass graves."

Within three months, Republic military units joined the Revanchists: volunteers, defectors from garrisons ordered to stand down, fleet officers who'd filed protest reports that vanished into Senate committees. The Revanchists became an army.

Then the Mandalorians demonstrated that Cathar was not an isolated failure of discipline. On Serroco, where the native Stereb population had begun aiding Revanchist intelligence operations, the Mandalorian garrison commander ordered orbital bombardment of the Stereb population centers. Not the Republic mining installations. Not the military facilities. The twelve largest Stereb cities. The cortosis mines were untouched.

The message was visible from orbit: the infrastructure matters. The people are replaceable.

Cathar was a massacre born of rage. Serroco was a calculation. The distinction mattered to Malak, who was managing Revanchist logistics while Revan planned grand strategy. Malak looked at Serroco and began thinking about how wars end. Not how you win battles, but how you make the killing stop.

### Two Generals (3959-3957 BBY)

The war revealed what Revan and Malak actually were.

Revan understood war as a problem of knowledge. He identified which planets mattered, how to frame the conflict for public support, where to apply pressure to fracture Mandalorian alliances. His strategic plans were elegant, ambitious, and frequently disconnected from the logistical reality of feeding an army in contested space.

Malak understood supply lines, troop movements, attrition rates, and the mathematics of a war fought across hundreds of systems. When historians later reviewed Revanchist victories, they found Revan's name on the strategic directives and Malak's signature on the tactical execution orders. Revan identified that Jebble needed to fall. Malak figured out how to take it with twelve percent casualties. Revan inspired the troops. Malak kept them fed and alive.

They needed each other. The problem was that Revan stopped needing the war.

In captured Mandalorian archives, Revan found references to ruins in the Unknown Regions, structures too old to be Republic, too sophisticated to be Mandalorian, built by a species that had mastered Force-powered technology the galaxy couldn't replicate. The Rakata, an ancient civilization that had built an empire on the Force and then lost it, with catastrophic results. Revan became convinced that understanding the Rakatan collapse was the key to understanding the current Force crisis. He began chasing fragments of Rakatan Star Maps, ancient navigational monuments scattered across the galaxy, each one a potential signpost to the Rakata's greatest achievement: the Star Forge, a factory the size of a moon that produced fleets by drawing on the Force itself.

He was not wrong about the connection. He was wrong about everything else.

By late 3958 BBY, Revan spent more time in research than in command meetings. He acquired Sith holocrons from black-market dealers and tomb raiders. He cross-referenced Rakatan architectural signatures with Sith historical records. His personal journal from this period, later recovered by Admiral Kaeranth, reads like the work of a man who has found a thread in a tapestry and believes that if he pulls hard enough, the entire design will reveal itself.

Malak watched his commander vanish into scholarship while the war bled the Revanchists dry. At first he rationalized: Revan was pursuing force multipliers, anything to end the conflict. Then Revan stopped reading casualty reports. He talked about infinity engines while Malak talked about supply shortages and desertion rates.

They were fighting different wars. They stopped pretending otherwise around the time Revan left for the Unknown Regions and didn't say when he'd return.

### The Ashen Campaigns (3957-3956 BBY)

Malak commissioned a study of Force-user effectiveness in Revanchist combat operations. The results were classified. The summary: Force-augmented units had suffered a thirty-one percent decline in operational reliability over two years. Precognition failures caused friendly-fire incidents. Healing failures increased field mortality. The Force was not a force multiplier anymore. It was a liability that occasionally worked.

From whatever research station he was operating from, Revan sent Malak a package. Inside were decoded techniques from a Sith holocron: a methodology for channeling the Force through multiple practitioners simultaneously, producing effects no individual user could achieve. The ancient Sith had used massed ritual to crack Jedi defenses during the Great Hyperspace War. Revan's attached note read: *They used this to destroy. We can use it to win. The difference is intent.*

Malak looked at the methodology and saw scale. Twenty unreliable Force users channeling in concert might produce one reliable result. He tested the theory at the Battle of Omonoth: thirty-seven Revanchist Jedi channeling together to generate a telekinetic shockwave that disabled a Mandalorian orbital defense platform. It worked. The Jedi involved reported headaches, nosebleeds, and a hollow sensation in the Force that lasted days.

The second coordinated operation used the technique on a planetary surface. The third cratered a mountain. By the fifth, the Revanchists had built a tactical doctrine around what they called Ashfall Operations, named for the particulate debris that choked the atmosphere after a strike. The soldiers called them the Ashen Campaigns.

The Ashen Campaigns were effective. They broke Mandalorian defensive positions that conventional forces couldn't crack. They terrorized garrisons. They turned the war's tide in sectors where the Revanchists had been losing for years.

They also tore holes in the Force that have never healed.

Each coordinated strike consumed something. Not just the energy of the practitioners, but something in the Force itself. The Jedi who participated described it as spending currency that couldn't be replenished. After a strike, the affected area became a dead zone. The first dead zones were small. A battlefield. A crater. By the end of the campaigns, entire regions were compromised. The Burning of Duro, an Ashen strike aimed at a Mandalorian shipyard in orbit, created a dead zone that encompassed the planet's upper atmosphere. Duro's surface became a world where the Force was gone, where Force-sensitive refugees reported constant pain, where the ecosystem died in ways biologists couldn't explain because the mechanisms were not biological.

Forty-one confirmed Ashen operations were conducted over fourteen months. The Revanchists won thirty-four of the associated engagements. Twelve planets sustained permanent Force damage. An estimated six hundred thousand civilians died in the operations or their immediate aftermath.

Malak authorized every operation. He read the damage reports. He signed the next order.

His reasoning was consistent: the Mandalorians would not stop. The Republic could not sustain a conventional war. The Force was dying regardless. If weaponizing it shortened the war, the calculation was acceptable. Every week the war ended early saved conventional casualties that would have been spent in attrition.

The mathematics were defensible. The mathematics were monstrous. Malak held both truths simultaneously and did not stop.

The Ashen Campaigns reached their worst at the Second Siege of Onderon. The beast-riders, who had earned Mandalorian respect through honourable combat in Year Two, had fortified Onderon into a stronghold. Conventional assault would have cost fifty thousand lives. Malak ordered a full Ashen strike: sixty Jedi channeling together against the Onderon jungle.

The strike shattered the Mandalorian perimeter. It also killed the jungle. Thousands of square kilometers of forest died in minutes as the Force was ripped from the ecosystem. The beasts that the riders had bonded with through the Force went mad, turning on their riders, on civilians, on each other. The beast-riders, who had earned their place through the purest form of Mandalorian honor, died screaming alongside their animals in a forest that was consuming itself.

Revanchist ground forces entered the dead zone and took Onderon in three days. Fourteen Jedi who participated in the strike were hospitalized. Two never recovered their Force sensitivity. A Miraluka named Veld Arannis, who perceived the world through the Force rather than through eyes, lost her Force sight permanently. She spent the rest of the war blind.

Onderon became a word that ended arguments. Proponents said "Onderon" and meant the war's turning point. Opponents said "Onderon" and meant its lowest moment. Both were correct.

### The Dhas'kara (3955 BBY)

The Mandalorians did not break because of the Ashen Campaigns. They broke because Malak understood something about their culture that Revan never grasped: the Mandalorians did not fear death, but they feared irrelevance. The Ashen Campaigns had demonstrated that industrialized Force application could annihilate Mandalorian forces with an efficiency that training and discipline could not match. The Doctrine of Necessary Strength was built on the premise that will and skill were superior to supernatural power. Malak's campaigns disproved that premise using the Mandalorians' own standard of measurement: results.

Malak had spent two years studying Mandalorian culture: honor codes, customs, the tradition of Dhas'kara. In Mandalorian law, a dispute between clans, or even a challenge to Mandalore himself, could be settled through single combat. Anyone could invoke the Right of Challenge by proving superior individual strength. No outsider had ever done so.

Malak sent a transmission directly to Mandalore the Vindicated. He invoked Dhas'kara. If Malak won, Mandalore would hear his terms. If Mandalore won, the Revanchists would surrender.

Under Mandalorian law, refusing a properly invoked challenge destroyed the authority of the one who refused. Mandalore had no option.

They fought in orbit above Dxun for four hours. No Force powers. Malak refused to use them. He insisted on meeting the Mandalorian standard: iron and will, nothing else.

Mandalore the Vindicated shattered Malak's jaw with a beskad strike and severed his sword arm. Malak's blade found Mandalore's throat first.

By rights, Malak could demand anything, including Mandalore's death. He demanded partnership.

### The Concordat (3955 BBY)

Malak stood over his defeated opponent with blood pouring from where his jaw had been, his severed arm cauterized by emergency systems, and offered terms that no one on either side had expected.

The Concordat would be a partnership. Mandalorian clans would provide military strength and maintain their warrior culture. Republic systems would provide economic administration and civilian governance. Resources would flow where needed. Defense would go to worlds that maintained militias and contributed to collective security. The Senate would stop debating while worlds burned. Mandalorian warriors would stop trying to manage grain distribution. Each side would do what it was built for.

Mandalore, alive through Malak's mercy and bound by Ka'halar, the life-debt owed by a defeated warrior to the victor who spares him, accepted.

Not all the clans followed. Canderous Ordo saw surrender dressed in partnership and walked. He formed the Neo-Mandalorian Coalition: a rejection of both the Concordat and the Remnant, committed to Mandalorian independence through the only method he trusted. His sister, Veera Ordo, stayed. She'd argued for administrative partnership for years. Now she had an ally with the authority to build it.

Cassus Fett's faction, calculating as always, saw the economic logic and aligned with the Concordat immediately. Much of Clan Vizla followed. The pragmatists recognized a winning hand. The traditionalists called it capitulation and joined Canderous.

The Republic Senate ratified the Concordat by the narrowest margin in its history. Malak was appointed Supreme Chancellor-General, a title that combined civil and military authority and made constitutional scholars deeply uncomfortable. He wore prosthetics for his jaw and arm. In Mandalorian culture, prosthetics marked a warrior who'd paid the price for victory. In Republic culture, they marked a man who was no longer whole. The ambiguity served him.

Admiral Kaeranth watched from the fringes. He'd served under both Revan and Malak during the war. He watched the Senate's authority erode. He watched Force-sensitive citizens placed under monitoring programs. He recognized the architecture: a republic hollowing itself out to make room for a strongman.

Kaeranth made quiet contact with officers who recognized the same pattern. When Revan returned from the Unknown Regions, alone, gaunt, carrying crates of datacrons and speaking in fragments about things he'd seen, Kaeranth was ready. He extracted Revan with three battlegroups and enough loyalist personnel to establish a government-in-exile.

The Revanchist Remnant formed around Revan in the Outer Rim. It refused to recognize the Concordat's legitimacy. It claimed to be the True Republic: democratic, principled, and fighting for what Malak had abandoned.

### The Years Since (3955-3952 BBY)

Three years have passed. The Concordat has consolidated power across the Core Worlds and much of the Inner Rim. Military governors administer contested territories in the Mid Rim. Mandalorian clan forces patrol alongside Republic regulars in an arrangement that functions better than anyone expected and worse than anyone hoped. The economy has stabilized, fueled by resource extraction from the Outer Rim and the Mandalorian trade networks that replaced the Republic's shattered supply chains. Force-sensitive citizens are registered, monitored, and in some systems restricted in their movements. The Senate meets, debates, and passes legislation that the Chancellor-General signs or overrides as circumstances require.

The Remnant has survived three years of guerrilla warfare through Kaeranth's competence and the Concordat's unwillingness to spend the military resources necessary to eliminate it. Twenty-three systems in the Outer Rim provide agricultural output and shelter. The Jedi Council, officially neutral, maintains quiet channels to the Remnant's leadership. Revan has just returned from the Unknown Regions. He has not addressed the Remnant's command structure. Kaeranth manages the political implications of a figurehead who may no longer be fit to lead.

The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition has carved a nomadic existence out of the Outer Rim's margins. Canderous Ordo leads through personal combat prowess and the sheer force of his conviction. He has killed seven challengers to his authority in three years. The Coalition raids Concordat and Remnant shipping with equal enthusiasm. Its ranks grow with every Mandalorian who decides the Concordat has domesticated the warrior tradition into something unrecognizable.

The Jedi Order has fractured beyond recognition. Approximately five hundred Jedi remain loyal to the Council on Coruscant. Two hundred serve the Remnant. Eighty serve the Concordat as advisors and healers. Three hundred Force-sensitive operatives serve in the Sentinel Program, a Council-authorized accelerated training initiative designed by the Jedi scholar Atris and run by Master Kavar, producing field operatives in months instead of years. Several dozen Jedi have renounced the Order entirely and disappeared into civilian life. The Council debates whether using the Force accelerates its death. The Jedi in the field don't have the luxury of debate.

The Sith, presumed destroyed in the Great Hyperspace War, are not gone. The Sith Empire's political structure shattered, but its people, its military fragments, and its traditions survived in diaspora. Scattered warlords command small flotillas in the Outer Rim. Sith Pureblood communities exist across the galaxy, navigating prejudice, displacement, and the complicated question of what "Sith" means when the Empire is ash. In the shadows, something is consolidating these fragments toward a purpose that none of the three major governments have identified. The galaxy has forgotten about the Sith. The Sith have not forgotten about the galaxy.

The Force continues to deteriorate. Dead zones expand. New zones appear in places where no Sith ritual and no Ashen strike can explain them. Kolto prices climb because Force healing is unreliable and demand for conventional medicine outpaces supply. The Selkath on Manaan, the only world that produces kolto, maintain their neutrality with the leverage of a monopoly. Every faction courts them. Every faction has considered taking what they need by force. The Selkath know this. They have contingency plans that involve destroying their own processing infrastructure rather than letting any single faction control the supply.

This is the galaxy in 3952 BBY. Functional enough to sustain daily life. Unstable enough that a single incident in a contested sector could unravel the political order. The Freighter Incident, which begins the campaign, is that single incident.

Everything after it is up to you.

# PART III: THE GALAXY NOW

## Chapter 5: Life in the Concordat Era

### What Normal Looks Like

A Concordat citizen in the Core Worlds wakes in an apartment that hasn't changed much since before the wars. The holonet plays the morning news: Senate proceedings, trade reports, a segment on kolto prices that nobody wants to hear. She takes a transit shuttle to work, passes a recruitment poster for the Concordat Joint Defense Force (featuring a human soldier and a Mandalorian warrior standing shoulder to shoulder, a pairing that would have been absurd five years ago), and arrives at an office where her supervisor is a former Republic administrator and her department head is a Mandalorian clan liaison who still wears partial armor to meetings.

Normal, in the Core Worlds, means functioning. The infrastructure works. The power stays on. The markets are open. The courts process cases, though military tribunals have jurisdiction over an expanding list of "security matters." The Senate debates legislation, though the Chancellor-General's emergency powers mean that debate is sometimes theater. For a citizen who doesn't ask too many questions about Force-sensitive monitoring, about the military governor system in the Mid Rim, about where the Mandalorian patrol ships go when they leave Coruscant orbit, life is stable. Comfortable. Insulated from the war's consequences by distance and by Malak's administrative competence.

A citizen on the Outer Rim frontier lives a different life. She wakes in a prefab shelter on a mining world where the garrison changed hands twice in the last year. The local authority might be a Concordat military governor, a Mandalorian clan chief, a corporate security outfit from Czerka, or nobody at all. Medical care means kolto if you can afford it and field surgery if you can't. The holonet reaches the frontier in fragments: outdated broadcasts, pirate frequencies, Remnant propaganda, and Neo-Mandalorian recruitment calls that sound increasingly reasonable when the garrison hasn't been resupplied in three months.

Between these extremes, the galaxy's Mid Rim exists in a state of negotiated uncertainty. Governments overlap. Trade routes function in some sectors and collapse in others. A merchant planning a cargo run from Taris to Onderon must calculate which patrol zones she'll cross, which tariffs apply in each jurisdiction, whether her navigation charts account for the dead zone that appeared near the Perlemian Trade Route last month, and whether her Mandalorian transit papers will be honored by the Concordat naval checkpoint at the Taris-Onderon corridor. The calculation changes weekly.

### Medicine

Before the wars, a serious injury in Republic space meant a trip to a medical facility where a Jedi healer, a kolto tank, or both could treat injuries that would otherwise require invasive surgery. The combination of Force healing and kolto therapy could restore a patient from near-death to functional health in days.

Force healing is now unreliable. A Jedi healer might channel perfectly for one patient and fail for the next. Some healers report that the Force "resists" their efforts, as though the connection between healer and patient has static in the line. Others describe a sensation of reaching for a tool that isn't where they left it. The Jedi who still practice healing do so with kolto backup and surgical teams standing by.

Kolto remains effective. The substance accelerates cellular regeneration and treats trauma with a potency that synthetic alternatives have failed to match. Demand has outstripped supply since the Great Hyperspace War. A standard kolto treatment that cost two hundred credits in 3964 BBY costs twelve hundred credits now. Military contracts consume the bulk of Manaan's output. Civilian hospitals ration. Frontier communities go without.

Scarcity breeds a secondary market. Diluted kolto, counterfeit kolto, kolto stolen from military shipments and resold at markup, and experimental variants produced by off-book laboratories circulate across the Outer Rim. Quality varies. Side effects vary more. The Windfall Protocol scenario involves one such experimental operation.

People are dying of injuries and illnesses that were treatable a decade ago. The medical crisis is not dramatic in the way that a battle or a political upheaval is dramatic. It is the slow accumulation of individual suffering: a child whose broken bone heals wrong because the local clinic has no kolto and the nearest Jedi healer's abilities failed last week. A veteran of the Ashen Campaigns whose nerve damage could be treated by Force therapy that no longer works. A kolto merchant who charges double because she can, because the alternatives are worse, because she has debts too.

### Travel

Hyperspace travel functions, but the margins of reliability have narrowed. The galaxy's hyperspace lane network was mapped over millennia by Force-sensitive navigators whose cartographic traditions predated the Republic. Those traditions assumed the Force was a constant. It is not.

Near dead zones, hyperspace routes degrade. A ship following a charted lane might drop out of hyperspace hours or days early, stranded in realspace until its navigator can recalculate. In the worst cases, ships have entered hyperspace on a standard route and never arrived. Whether they were destroyed, displaced, or lost in some unmapped region of hyperspace is unknown. Insurance premiums for hyperspace shipping have tripled since the Concordat's signing.

The major trade routes (the Hydian Way, the Perlemian Trade Route, the Corellian Run) remain functional in their core segments. Outer Rim feeder routes are less reliable. Frontier routes are speculative. A captain running cargo to a frontier system carries backup navigation charts, emergency supplies for an unplanned realspace stop, and the kind of optimism that frontier capitalism requires.

Astromech droids, which calculate hyperspace jumps through algorithmic rather than Force-based methods, have become more valuable than they've ever been. A good astromech navigator is worth its weight in cortosis. The droid market has responded accordingly.

### Labor, Class, and Displacement

The wars displaced millions. Cathar refugees scattered across the Mid and Outer Rim after the Razing. Serroco's surviving Stereb population was relocated (some voluntarily, most not) to Concordat resettlement facilities. Onderon's beast-riders, those who survived the Second Siege, have no jungle to return to. Sith Pureblood populations displaced by the Republic's post-GHW counter-invasion live in diaspora communities across the galaxy, navigating prejudice and surveillance.

Alongside the displaced, the wars produced veterans. Hundreds of thousands of soldiers, Mandalorian and Republic and Revanchist, fought for years and then found themselves in a galaxy that had moved on. Concordat veterans receive pensions and reintegration support (Mandalorian clans are better at this than Republic institutions, which is an uncomfortable fact that Concordat administrators acknowledge privately). Remnant veterans receive what the Remnant can afford, which is little. Neo-Mandalorian veterans receive honor and poverty. Former Revanchist Jedi who participated in the Ashen Campaigns receive nothing, because no institution wants to claim them.

The Concordat's economy runs on resource extraction, trade, and the military-industrial apparatus that Malak's government has built. Czerka Corporation, the galaxy's largest private resource extraction company, operates mining operations on dozens of Outer Rim worlds with a labor practices record that charitable observers call "aggressive" and honest observers call "indentured servitude with better marketing." The Exchange, a criminal syndicate with operations spanning the Mid and Outer Rim, manages smuggling, gambling, loan-sharking, and protection rackets that fill the gaps in Concordat governance. Hutt Space operates its own economy, connected to the Concordat by trade agreements that everyone knows are financing both sides of every conflict.

Working people in the Concordat Era work hard, get paid unevenly, and know that the political order above them could shift at any time. Where you live determines how you live: Core World citizens in comparative comfort, Mid Rim citizens in anxiety, Outer Rim citizens in contingency.

### What People Argue About

In cantinas, in Senate lobbies, in military barracks, in refugee shelters, people argue about the same questions:

Is Malak a necessary strongman or a tyrant in the making? The Concordat works, but the emergency powers haven't expired and the Senate's authority shrinks every year. How long is "temporary"?

Was the Concordat worth the price? The war ended. The economy stabilized. But Mandalorians patrol Republic cities and Force-sensitive citizens carry registration papers. Is stability worth the compromises it required?

Is the Force dying, and does it matter? For the Force-sensitive, this is existential. For everyone else, it is a question about infrastructure: if the Force goes away entirely, what breaks? The medical system. The hyperspace network. The governance structures built on Jedi mediation. How much of civilization was built on an assumption that might be wrong?

Are the Remnant freedom fighters or dead-enders? They claim the Republic's principles. They hold twenty-three agricultural worlds. Their leader may be losing his mind. Is loyalty to an ideal worth dying for when the ideal can't feed you?

Are the Mandalorians allies or occupiers? The Concordat says partners. The Mandalorian patrol ships in Coruscant orbit say something more complicated.

These arguments do not have correct answers. Your character's position on them is one of the most important things about them.

## Chapter 6: The Major Powers

### 6.1 The Concordat

**What they claim:** The Concordat is the legitimate successor to the Galactic Republic, enhanced by the military strength of the Mandalorian clans and the administrative reforms necessary to prevent the governance failures that allowed the Mandalorian Wars to happen. The Chancellor-General's emergency powers are temporary measures required by an ongoing security crisis. The Concordat protects its citizens, provides economic stability, and maintains order in a galaxy that nearly destroyed itself.

**What they do:** The Concordat governs approximately four hundred major systems and claims authority over six hundred more in various stages of integration. Core World governance resembles the old Republic with added security apparatus. Mid Rim governance operates through military governors with broad discretionary authority. Outer Rim Concordat territories are garrison states where Mandalorian clan forces provide security and Republic-trained administrators manage resource extraction.

The Concordat military is a hybrid force: Republic naval traditions merged with Mandalorian ground doctrine. Joint units train together. Joint academies have opened on Coruscant and Dxun. The integration works better at the operational level than at the political level, because soldiers who fight together develop professional respect faster than politicians who negotiate together develop trust.

Force-sensitive citizens within Concordat territory are required to register with the Office of Force Affairs, a security agency that tracks Force-user locations, monitors their activities, and, in contested territories, restricts their movements. The stated purpose is public safety: unreliable Force abilities in a stressed population create genuine risks. The unstated purpose is political control: Force users are the Remnant's most effective operatives, and the Concordat treats unregistered Force sensitives as potential insurgents.

**Internal factions:**

The **Integrationists**, led by Veera Ordo and supported by elements of Cassus Fett's economic bloc, want the Concordat to evolve into a permanent hybrid government. They push for Mandalorian representation in the Senate, joint cultural programs, and the legal recognition of Mandalorian customs within Republic law. They are the Concordat's optimists. They believe the marriage of traditions can produce something stronger than either tradition alone.

The **Restorationists** are former Republic senators and administrators who accepted the Concordat as wartime necessity and want full democratic governance restored as soon as conditions permit. Their leader in the Senate is Senator Lorso of Telos, a pragmatist who despises Malak personally and supports his government reluctantly because removing him would create a power vacuum. The Restorationists push for sunset clauses, judicial oversight, and the restoration of Senate authority over military operations.

The **Militarists** are Malak's core supporters. Military officers, Mandalorian chieftains, and security-state architects who believe the galaxy's overlapping crises require permanent centralized authority. They argue that democratic deliberation is a luxury the galaxy cannot afford while the Force dies, three hostile factions contest Concordat territory, and the Outer Rim remains ungovernable. The Militarists are not cynics. Many of them served in the wars, saw what indecision cost, and have decided that effective governance matters more than participatory governance.

**Mandalore the Vindicated** serves as Lord Marshal of the Concordat, a Mandalorian title reforged for Republic consumption. He attends military councils. He provides insight into Mandalorian clan politics. He is, in function, a hostage dressed as a partner: his presence legitimizes the Concordat to Mandalorian clans, and his bond of Ka'halar to Malak guarantees their compliance. Both Malak and Mandalore understand this. Neither says it aloud. The arrangement holds so long as no one examines it too closely.

**Character hooks:** A Concordat character might be a military officer navigating the tensions between Republic and Mandalorian command traditions. A bureaucrat in the Office of Force Affairs who's started to doubt the program's purpose. A Mandalorian warrior serving in a joint unit who respects her Republic counterparts but wonders what she's fighting for. A Restorationist senator's aide who's discovered something about the emergency powers that the Senator needs to hear. A Concordat citizen who believes in the project and fears what will happen if it fails.

### 6.2 The Revanchist Remnant

**What they claim:** The Remnant is the legitimate Republic. The Concordat is a military coup dressed in democratic language. Malak betrayed the principles the Revanchists fought for when he bargained with Mandalorians and consolidated power. The Remnant will restore democratic governance, end the military governor system, dissolve the Office of Force Affairs, and return the Republic to its citizens.

**What they do:** The Remnant holds twenty-three systems in the Outer Rim, connected by hidden hyperspace routes and guerrilla supply networks. Its military consists of three battle groups under Admiral Kaeranth: roughly forty capital ships, a network of hidden bases, and a corps of intelligence operatives who conduct supply-line raids, sabotage operations, and propaganda campaigns in Concordat territory.

The Remnant's civilian governance is administered by Master Dorak, a Jedi historian who left the Council to join Revan. Carth Onasi, a Republic captain who defected after the Senate ratification vote, manages diplomatic relations and intelligence coordination. The three of them (Kaeranth, Dorak, Onasi) form the Remnant's functional leadership. Revan is the Remnant's figurehead, its moral authority, and, since his return from the Unknown Regions, its most unpredictable variable.

Revan spent two years chasing Rakatan Star Maps and the trail of the Star Forge, an ancient factory of immense power built by a pre-Republic civilization called the Rakata. He has returned diminished. He speaks in long monologues about "the pattern" he perceives beneath the Force, a structure deeper than the light-side/dark-side distinction. He sketches diagrams on any available surface. He sometimes fails to recognize people he has known for years. He sometimes demonstrates insights so precise that Kaeranth wonders whether genius and madness are the same thing seen from different distances.

The Remnant's rank and file are split. Some worship Revan as a returned savior. Some fear he has become the cautionary tale the Council always warned about. Some are loyal to Kaeranth and don't particularly care what Revan thinks anymore, because the Admiral is the one who keeps them fed.

**Internal tensions:** The Remnant runs on conviction and scarcity. Its people believe in democratic principles, but their government is an emergency structure with no elections, no free press, and a chain of command that answers to a man who may be unfit to lead. The contradiction is not lost on the Remnant's leadership. Dorak has raised the question of Revan's competence in closed sessions. Kaeranth has deflected it. Onasi has started keeping his own records of Revan's behavior, in case they become evidence later.

The Jedi who serve the Remnant (approximately two hundred) maintain the Revanchist military structure: Jedi Commanders, Captains, and Lieutenants integrated into Kaeranth's battle groups. They are the most militarized Jedi in the galaxy, and the most conflicted. They left the Order to fight for principle. They stayed to fight for a republic that no longer exists. Their lightsabers work when the Force cooperates and don't when it doesn't. They carry blasters as backup. Some of them have started to prefer the blasters.

**Character hooks:** A Remnant character might be a guerrilla pilot who's flown supply runs through Concordat patrols for three years and is running out of luck. A Jedi Commander who followed Revan out of the Temple and now isn't sure what she's following. A civilian administrator on a Remnant agricultural world who's watched the idealism curdle into survivalism. A defector from the Concordat military who chose the Remnant because the alternative was complicity. An intelligence operative who's seen Revan's research notes and can't decide whether they're revelation or psychosis.

### 6.3 The Neo-Mandalorian Coalition

**What they claim:** The Concordat is dishonor. Mandalore lost in single combat and accepted servitude rather than death, violating the deepest tenets of Mandalorian tradition. The Remnant is weakness: clinging to mystical guardians and democratic idealism while the galaxy demands strength. The Neo-Mandalorians will demonstrate true Mandalorian values through combat. They will prove that warriors who maintain independence and self-reliance deserve to survive, and that those who trade freedom for comfort deserve to fall.

**What they do:** The Coalition operates as a nomadic military force. Mobile bases on uninhabited moons and asteroid installations. A raiding fleet that hits Concordat and Remnant shipping. Recruitment from Mandalorian clans dissatisfied with the Concordat and from non-Mandalorian populations that have been failed by both Republic factions.

Canderous Ordo leads through personal combat prowess. He has killed seven challengers to his authority in three years and is growing tired of killing subordinates. His leadership style is charismatic and increasingly desperate: the Coalition's resources are shrinking, its best warriors are aging, and the flow of recruits does not replace the losses.

**Internal tensions:** The Coalition contains traditionalists who fight for honor, mercenaries who fight for pay, and refugees who fight because no one offered them anything better. These groups agree on the Concordat's illegitimacy and disagree on everything else.

Daaro Ordo, Veera's cousin and Canderous's friend, has been carrying messages about reconciliation between Concordat-aligned clans and the Coalition. If those messages reach Canderous, the political calculus of the Mandalorian split could change. If Daaro dies (or if the messages are intercepted), the split hardens into permanence.

**Character hooks:** A Neo-Mandalorian character might be a warrior who's watched the Coalition's resources shrink and wonders if honor can sustain a people. A non-Mandalorian recruit who adopted the armor and the code because the Mandalorians were the only faction that treated strength as its own justification. A mechanic who keeps the raiding fleet flying with scavenged parts and stubbornness. A child of Concordat Mandalorians who defected to the Coalition because the Concordat's version of Mandalorian culture felt like a costume.

### 6.4 The Jedi Order

**What they claim:** The Force is in crisis. Using it carelessly accelerates the damage. The Jedi Order's duty is to study the crisis, preserve Jedi knowledge, and avoid actions that could make things worse. The Order is neutral in the Concordat political conflict because choosing a side would subordinate the Jedi's mission to a political agenda.

**What they do:** Approximately five hundred Jedi remain loyal to the Council on Coruscant, led by Masters Vrook, Vandar, Zez-Kai Ell, and Lonna Vash. The Council maintains enclaves on Coruscant, Dantooine (contested), Telos, and smaller sites. Its scholars research the Force crisis. Its healers staff clinics (when their abilities cooperate). Its diplomats attempt mediation between the factions (with diminishing success).

The Council authorized the Sentinel Program two years ago: an accelerated training initiative that produces Force-sensitive field operatives in months instead of years. Designed by Atris, a Jedi scholar and Council member, and run by Master Kavar, a decorated Guardian, the program has produced approximately three hundred operatives. Thirty-seven are confirmed dead. Twelve have deserted. Eight show signs of dark-side instability. Four have been killed by Concordat intelligence. The program is officially secret and practically public knowledge. Every faction has opinions about it. None of them are complimentary.

Atris herself withdrew from the program after its establishment. She maintains a private academy on Telos, where she trains Echani Handmaidens, elite warriors specifically equipped to fight and subdue Force users. She collects Sith holocrons to study the enemy. The boundary between studying the enemy and learning from the enemy has grown thin.

**Internal factions:** The Jedi Order in 3952 BBY is at least five groups wearing the same robes.

**Council Loyalists** follow the Masters' directive of restraint. **Remnant Jedi** serve Revan's government-in-exile with Revanchist military ranks. **Concordat Advisors** serve Malak's government in medical, military, and administrative roles. **Sentinel Operatives** serve in the field with accelerated training and minimal doctrine. **Independents** have renounced the Order and vanished into civilian life.

The fracture is theological as much as political. The Council believes using the Force accelerates its death. The Remnant Jedi believe the Force dies because people fail to act. The Concordat Advisors believe the Force's decline is irreversible and practical governance matters more than metaphysical hope. The Sentinels believe what their trainers taught them, which varies by trainer. The Independents believe the Order failed and see no reason to go down with it.

**Character hooks:** A Jedi character might be a Council Loyalist who's starting to suspect the Council is wrong. A Remnant Jedi Commander who carries a blaster because her lightsaber only works half the time. A Concordat Advisor who heals patients in a government clinic and files reports to a security agency. A Sentinel operative with three months of training and a mission she doesn't fully understand. An Independent who left the Order, opened a cantina on Nar Shaddaa, and is trying very hard not to get involved.

### 6.5 The Sith

**What most people know:** The Sith Empire was destroyed in the Great Hyperspace War. Its military was shattered. Its political structure collapsed. Its survivors were driven into exile.

**What is actually happening:** The empire collapsed. The Sith did not collapse with it.

Scattered across the Outer Rim, Wild Space, and the galaxy's ungoverned margins, the remnants of the Sith military operate as independent flotillas: some as pirates, some as mercenaries, some as warlords governing small territories in sectors where no one else bothers to look. Former Sith officers of ability and ambition command these fragments. A handful maintain enough ships and personnel to constitute minor military powers.

Sith Pureblood diaspora communities exist on dozens of worlds, concentrated in the Outer Rim and on Nar Shaddaa, where prejudice is less organized and survival is more valued than pedigree. These communities maintain fragments of Sith culture: language, cuisine, religious practice, caste memory. They navigate a galaxy that views their species with suspicion and their heritage as a security threat.

On Korriban, a small garrison of Sith traditionalists maintains the tomb world and its ancient academies. They claim religious authority over Sith cultural heritage and answer to no external government. The Valley of the Dark Lords still contains sealed tombs, and the Force crisis has destabilized some of those seals. Reports from the last two years include tremors in tomb complexes, the activation of security systems dormant for centuries, and, in one unverified report from a Czerka archaeological team, a voice speaking Old Sith from behind a wall that sensors read as solid stone.

The Sith Triumvirate operates in this landscape, and most people do not know it exists. The Triumvirate is not a government. It is a network, coordinated by a former Jedi Master who has drawn conclusions about the Force that would terrify anyone who heard them, served by two beings who are less people than they are consequences of the wars. Its agents operate in every major faction. Its goals are patient. Its methods are precise. Its existence is a rumor that the galaxy's intelligence services have not yet confirmed.

For the purposes of this book, the Triumvirate is a shadow on the edge of the setting. Player characters may encounter its agents, its operations, or the effects of its work. They will not understand the full architecture. That understanding comes in play.

**Character hooks:** A Sith character might be a Pureblood veteran of the GHW who's been running a mercenary outfit for twelve years and just received a recruitment offer from someone who knows things about the Sith that no one should know. A Pureblood scholar in diaspora who's been studying pre-Exile Sith traditions and stumbled onto something the Keepers of Adas have been protecting for millennia. A former Sith naval officer serving in the Concordat military under a false name, hiding red skin under a uniform that doesn't ask questions. A non-Pureblood drawn to Sith philosophy not as evil but as a framework for understanding passion, strength, and the will to survive.

### 6.6 Independent Powers

Not every faction in the galaxy answers to the Concordat, the Remnant, or the Mandalorians. The spaces between the major powers are full.

**Hutt Space** encompasses approximately three hundred systems in the Outer Rim under Hutt clan authority. The Hutts have been neutral in the Concordat conflict, which means they've sold weapons, intelligence, kolto, and logistical support to every side while profiting from the chaos. Hutt Space functions as a trading hub, a refuge for those fleeing all three factions, and a place where credits talk louder than politics.

**Czerka Corporation** is the galaxy's largest private resource extraction company. It operates mining operations, processing facilities, and security forces on dozens of worlds. Its labor practices range from aggressive to indentured. Its lobbyists maintain offices on Coruscant and in Hutt Space. Its security forces are the de facto authority on several Outer Rim worlds where no government has established a garrison. Czerka's interest in the Concordat conflict is commercial: stable markets and resource access matter more than who signs the legislation.

**The Exchange** is a criminal syndicate with operations spanning the Mid and Outer Rim. Smuggling, gambling, loan-sharking, protection rackets, and the trafficking of controlled substances (including black-market kolto). The Exchange fills governance gaps, providing services that legal authorities can't or won't, at prices that reflect the risk. Its leadership is decentralized: sector bosses operate with significant autonomy, and the organization functions more as a franchise than a hierarchy.

**Neutral systems** exist throughout the galaxy. Worlds too remote, too poor, or too stubborn to accept any faction's authority. Some maintain genuine independence through defensive capability or strategic irrelevance. Some survive by playing factions against each other. Manaan, the sole source of kolto, is the most powerful neutral system in the galaxy and maintains its independence through monopoly leverage and the threat of self-destruction.

## Chapter 7: The Force in Crisis

### What Everyone Notices

The Force crisis is not abstract. It touches daily life in ways that even people with no Force sensitivity can identify, even if they can't explain the mechanism.

Jedi are less effective than they used to be. Everyone knows this. The Jedi healers who once staffed Republic medical facilities with near-miraculous capability now fail as often as they succeed. The Jedi mediators who once resolved planetary disputes through Force-enhanced empathy and truth-sensing now arrive at negotiations with the same blind spots as everyone else. The Jedi warriors who once moved with supernatural speed and foresight now hesitate, miscalculate, and die in engagements they would have won a decade ago.

People who live near dead zones report specific, observable effects: mechanical failures in equipment that worked fine elsewhere, communication blackouts, navigation errors in ships passing through the zone, and a pervasive unease that even non-Force-sensitive individuals describe as "wrongness." Animals avoid dead zones. Plants grow strangely in their periphery. Weather patterns shift near the larger ones in ways that meteorologists cannot account for through conventional atmospheric models.

The holonet carries speculation: Republic scientists attempting to measure the zones through conventional instruments, Jedi scholars offering cautious theological interpretations, Mandalorian commentators citing the Doctrine of Necessary Strength as vindication, conspiracy theorists blaming the Concordat, the Remnant, ancient Sith curses, or galactic alignment cycles. Everyone speculates. No one has answers.

### What the Jedi Believe

The Jedi Council's official position: the Force is damaged, possibly by the massed Sith rituals of the Great Hyperspace War, possibly compounded by the Ashen Campaigns. Using the Force carelessly in its weakened state may accelerate the deterioration. The correct response is caution, study, and minimal intervention.

This position is not unanimous even within the Council. Master Vrook argues for strict non-intervention. Master Vandar favors cautious engagement. Master Zez-Kai Ell has privately suggested that the Council's understanding of the crisis may be fundamentally incomplete and that restraint based on incomplete understanding is indistinguishable from paralysis.

Among Jedi outside the Council, beliefs vary further. Remnant Jedi tend to believe the Force is damaged by inaction and suffering, that failing to use the Force in defense of others is itself a form of harm. Concordat Advisors tend to view the crisis as a practical problem requiring practical solutions, with theological debate a luxury. Sentinel operatives, trained in accelerated programs, tend not to have developed nuanced Force theology at all. They know what they were taught: the Force is unreliable, carry a blaster.

### What the Sith Believed

The Sith Empire's scholars had their own understanding of the Force, rooted in traditions that predated the Republic. Sith theology held that the Force was not a benevolent cosmic energy but an elemental power, morally neutral, shaped by the will of those who wielded it. Passion was the key to accessing the Force's full potential. Restraint was weakness. The Jedi's "light side" was a philosophy of self-limitation that prevented Force users from reaching their true capacity.

The Sith rituals of the Great Hyperspace War were not random acts of destruction. They were, in the Sith theological framework, applications of the Force at its highest intensity. That the rituals damaged the Force itself was not predicted by Sith theory, and the implications have fractured Sith thought in the decades since.

Some surviving Sith scholars argue that the Force is resilient and will heal if given time. Others argue that the Force was always finite and the rituals proved it. A handful, working from traditions older than the Sith Empire itself, argue that the light-side/dark-side framework was always an oversimplification and that the Force's deterioration is the consequence of ten thousand years of Force users on both sides treating it as a tool rather than a relationship.

### What Ordinary People Think

Most people do not think about the Force in theological terms. They think about it in practical terms.

The kolto medic on Taris thinks: "Force healing doesn't work anymore, so I need more kolto, and kolto costs more every month."

The freighter captain thinks: "The Perlemian run used to be safe. Now I drop out of hyperspace a day early and have to calculate the rest by hand. My astromech earns its maintenance."

The Mandalorian patrol sergeant thinks: "We told them the Force was a crutch. We were right. They should have listened."

The Cathar refugee thinks: "The Jedi couldn't save us because the Force was failing. Or the Jedi wouldn't save us because the Council was debating. I can't tell the difference and it doesn't matter."

The Sith Pureblood dockworker thinks: "My grandmother could feel the Force in everything. I can barely feel it at all. My daughter feels nothing. Is this the end of what we are?"

The Miraluka navigator thinks: "I see through the Force. When the Force fails, I go blind. When I go blind, my ship and everyone on it dies. I haven't told my crew how often the failures are happening."

### What It Means in Practice

Everyone who depends on the Force has recalculated.

**Combat:** A Force-sensitive combatant cannot rely on precognition, telekinesis, or enhanced reflexes. Some engagements proceed normally. Some see abilities cut out mid-fight. Smart commanders plan for both contingencies. Soldiers who serve alongside Jedi have learned to operate as though Force support might vanish at any moment. This has made non-Force-sensitive troops more self-reliant and more resentful of Force users who are praised for abilities they can no longer guarantee.

**Governance:** Jedi mediators once resolved disputes through Force-enhanced truth-sensing and empathic communication. Without reliable access to those abilities, mediation is slower, less certain, and more susceptible to deception. The Concordat has partially replaced Jedi mediation with bureaucratic arbitration. It works. Nobody trusts it the way they trusted a Jedi reading the room.

**Spirituality:** Billions of beings understood the Force as the binding energy of life. Its deterioration has cracked open every religious tradition that touches it. The Jedi, the Sith, the Order of Shasa, the Baran Do Sages, village shamans on a hundred Outer Rim worlds: all asking whether the Force is dying, being killed, evolving, or punishing them. None agreeing on the answer.

**Daily life:** The Force crisis registers as a background hum of anxiety. Things that used to work don't work as well. Institutions that used to be reliable aren't. Promises that used to hold don't. The load-bearing walls are cracked. The building still stands. Everyone on the upper floors can feel the shift.

# PART IV: PLACES

## Chapter 8: Worlds, Regions, and Frontiers

This chapter covers the worlds and regions most relevant to the campaign. It is not an encyclopedia. Each entry answers: who controls it, what happened there, why it matters now, and what kind of trouble finds you there.

### Coruscant

**Control:** Concordat capital. **Security:** High.

The galaxy's political center has not changed hands, but it has changed character. The Senate still meets in the same chamber. The Chancellor-General's office occupies the same floor. The Jedi Temple still stands, though its population has thinned to a fraction of pre-war numbers.

Coruscant under the Concordat is a city of surfaces. The upper levels function as they always have: politics, commerce, culture, the apparatus of galactic governance. Below the surface, the security apparatus operates: the Office of Force Affairs, military intelligence, the network of monitoring stations that track Force-sensitive citizens. Mandalorian patrol ships in orbit are visible from the Senate plaza, a daily reminder that the Concordat's stability rests on a partnership between traditions that have no historical precedent for trusting each other.

For a player character, Coruscant is where you make deals, trade information, and see the Concordat's contradictions up close. A senator's aide overhears something in the wrong corridor. A Mandalorian liaison officer discovers that her joint command assignment is really a surveillance post. A Force-sensitive citizen notices that the "routine registration update" involves questions that have nothing to do with registration.

### Taris

**Control:** Concordat (Mandalorian administration). **Security:** Medium.

Taris was the first Mandalorian conquest and the first test of every promise the Expansion made. Three years into the Concordat, Taris is the setting's proof of concept and its most visible potential failure.

The upper city has stabilized under Mandalorian-Concordat governance. Trade flows. Infrastructure functions. The Mandalorian customs authority manages offworld commerce with efficiency the old Republic bureaucracy never achieved. Tarisian nobles who fled to Coruscant during the siege have returned and found their influence diminished: the new power brokers are Mandalorian clan liaisons and Republic administrators, not hereditary aristocrats.

The Undercity has improved more than the upper city will admit. Mandalorian infrastructure investment reached levels the Republic never provided. Water treatment, power distribution, basic medical care. The Undercity is not comfortable, but it is no longer abandoned.

Between the levels, resentment simmers. Upper-city Tarisians resent Mandalorian authority. Undercity Tarisians resent upper-city Tarisians for caring about sovereignty more than survival. Mandalorian administrators resent the complexity of managing a world that doesn't think like a military camp.

### Onderon and Dxun

**Control:** Concordat (contested). **Security:** Medium to Low.

Onderon bears the scars of the Second Siege. Thousands of square kilometers of jungle are dead, killed when the Ashen strike ripped the Force from the ecosystem. The dead zone covers the former beast-rider territories. Nothing grows there. The beasts that survived the strike are feral, disconnected from their riders, dangerous.

The living jungle beyond the dead zone supports the remnant of Onderon's civilian population. Queen Talia maintains nominal authority, but her power depends on Concordat military support, and the Concordat's interest in Onderon is strategic rather than humanitarian. Dxun, the jungle moon in Onderon's orbit, remains culturally significant to Mandalorians as the site of Mandalore the Vindicated's original gathering and as the location of the Dhas'kara that ended the war.

For a player character, Onderon is a wound. The dead zone is physically present: you can walk to its edge and feel the Force vanish. Veterans of the Second Siege avoid it. Civilians cross it when they must and speak of headaches, nausea, and a sensation they describe as grief that isn't theirs.

### Manaan

**Control:** Independent (Selkath sovereignty). **Security:** High (Selkath-enforced).

The galaxy's most important neutral world. Manaan produces kolto, the substance that keeps wounded soldiers alive and civilian medicine functional. The Selkath government in Ahto City sells kolto to every faction at prices they set. The arrangement makes Manaan the most politically significant neutral system in the galaxy.

Ahto City is a surface city built on a world-spanning ocean. Foreign embassies compete for influence. The Concordat embassy is the largest. The Remnant maintains a smaller diplomatic mission. Corporate offices (Czerka prominent among them) line the commercial districts. Intelligence operatives from every faction circulate through the markets.

Beneath the surface, the Selkath maintain a relationship with their ocean that outsiders do not fully understand. The kolto deposits lie deep, near the Hrakert Rift, a deep-sea geological formation of immense significance to Selkath religion and science. The Order of Shasa, an indigenous Selkath Force tradition, guards sites in the deep ocean that predate Republic contact. The connection between kolto, the Rift, and the Force crisis is not a coincidence. The Selkath know more than they share.

Internal Selkath politics are fracturing under the pressure. Isolationists want to reduce exports. Commercialists want to expand. Interventionists argue that Manaan's neutrality enables atrocities. The Order of Shasa argues that the kolto crisis is a Selkath spiritual concern, not merely an economic one.

### Korriban

**Control:** Sith traditionalist garrison. **Security:** Low (dangerous).

The tomb world of the Sith. A desert planet in the Stygian Caldera, home to the Valley of the Dark Lords, where Sith rulers of extraordinary power were entombed across millennia. Korriban was the Sith Empire's spiritual center. After the Empire's destruction, a small garrison of Sith traditionalists maintained the tombs and the ancient academies.

The Force crisis has changed Korriban. Ancient seals on tomb complexes have weakened. Security systems dormant for centuries have activated. The dark-side energy saturating the Valley has not diminished with the rest of the Force; if anything, it has concentrated, as though the Force's retreat has left the dark side pooled in the places where it was always strongest.

A Rakatan Star Map, older than the Sith Empire, sits in the Valley. It has been drawing on dark-side energy for millennia. What it has become is unclear. The Czerka archaeological teams that have visited Korriban in the last two years have reported findings they refuse to discuss publicly.

Korriban is not a place for casual visitors. For a Sith Pureblood character, it is the ancestral homeland and the repository of everything their people built and lost. For anyone else, it raises a question the Republic has never answered: what did the Sith build on, and what is it doing now that no one is maintaining it?

### Dantooine

**Control:** Contested (Concordat claim, Remnant presence, Mandalorian cultural footprint). **Security:** Low.

A pastoral world on the Outer Rim, home to a Jedi Enclave that predates the Republic's colonial expansion. Dantooine fell to the Mandalorians without a shot during the Expansion: colonists walked to the Mandalorian camp because the Mandalorians offered practical instruction while the Jedi offered meditation.

The Jedi Enclave remains operational but diminished. Its Masters maintain the facility and train a handful of Padawans. The surrounding settlements are split between Concordat-aligned administrators, Mandalorian-influenced farming communities, and Remnant sympathizers who use Dantooine's remoteness as cover for intelligence operations.

Rakatan ruins near the Enclave contain a partially degraded Star Map. Its current status is uncertain, which makes Dantooine a target for anyone pursuing Revan's research.

### Nar Shaddaa

**Control:** Hutt Space (The Exchange, Hutt clans). **Security:** Low.

The Smuggler's Moon. A world-city in the orbit of Nal Hutta, the Hutt homeworld. Nar Shaddaa is where the galaxy's margins meet: refugees, criminals, deserters, merchants, bounty hunters, spies, and anyone who needs a place where questions aren't asked.

Sith Pureblood diaspora communities maintain a significant presence. The Exchange operates openly. Concordat intelligence maintains covert stations. Remnant operatives recruit. Neo-Mandalorian scouts resupply. Everyone coexists through the Hutt principle of commerce: as long as credits flow, conflict is bad for business.

For a player character, Nar Shaddaa is where you go when every other faction has a reason to arrest you. It is also where you go when you need something that doesn't exist in legal markets: black-market kolto, forged transit papers, a ship with no registry, or information that someone powerful wants to stay buried.

### The Outer Rim Frontier

**Control:** Variable (see sovereignty system). **Security:** Null to Low.

The frontier is not a single place. It is the vast, poorly charted expanse beyond the major powers' reliable reach. Thousands of inhabited systems answer to no galactic authority. Mining camps, agricultural outposts, refueling stations, hermit communities, and abandoned pre-war installations dot the frontier.

Travel here is risky. Hyperspace routes are poorly maintained and increasingly degraded by dead-zone proximity. Piracy is common. Patrols are rare. A ship that drops out of hyperspace in the wrong sector might encounter Neo-Mandalorian raiders, Sith remnant warlords, Exchange smugglers, Czerka security corvettes protecting a mining claim, or nothing at all for parsecs in every direction.

The frontier is where the campaign's starting scenarios take place. The Freighter Incident occurs in contested frontier space. The Windfall Protocol occurs on a hidden station in frontier ocean territory. The frontier is where the Concordat Era's rules break down and the player characters' choices start to matter more than any government's policies.

# PART V: PEOPLES

## Chapter 9: Peoples and Species of the Concordat Era

This chapter describes the species and cultures most relevant to the campaign. Each entry covers what most people assume, what is actually true, how the Concordat Era has changed things, and why it matters for character creation.

### Humans

**What people assume:** Humans are the default. The Republic was built by humans. The Senate is dominated by humans. Humans are everywhere, unremarkable, and culturally invisible.

**What is true:** Humans are the galaxy's most politically powerful species, which is not the same as being the default. Human cultural dominance in the Republic was maintained through institutional structures that favored Core World human populations: Senate representation weighted toward human-majority systems, commercial law written in Basic, military academies on human-majority worlds.

The Concordat Era has complicated human identity. Mandalorian integration brought a warrior culture into the Republic's administrative framework, and that warrior culture defines membership by behavior rather than species. A human raised Mandalorian has more in common with a Twi'lek raised Mandalorian than with a human senator on Coruscant. Regional variation has always mattered more than species for humans, and the war has sharpened those regional divides: Core World humans, Outer Rim humans, Mandalorian-adopted humans, and frontier humans live in different galaxies that happen to share a species.

**Character hooks:** A human character needs a specific origin, a specific region, a specific relationship to the war, and a specific set of opinions. "I'm human" is not enough. "I'm a human freighter mechanic from Taris who grew up in the Undercity during the siege and learned to speak Mando'a before I learned to read Basic" is a character.

### Mandalorians

**What people assume:** Mandalorians are a warrior species. They wear armor. They fight. They conquer.

**What is true:** Mandalorians are a culture, not a species. Any individual of any species who swears to the Resol'nare, the six core tenets of Mandalorian identity, and is accepted by a clan, is Mandalorian. Mandalorian demographics include humans, Twi'leks, Zabraks, Cathar (rare, and politically fraught), and dozens of other species who were adopted into the clans through the tradition of gai bal manda.

Before the wars, Mandalorian society was agricultural and martial simultaneously. A Mandalorian farmer woke before dawn, worked the kkanmur grain fields (a hardy Dxun cereal crop that grows in the moon's dense humidity) in beskar-composite work armor, trained with weapons each evening, and taught her children to do the same. The Resol'nare structured daily life the way religious law structures an observant community: wear armor, speak Mando'a, defend your clan, raise your children as Mandalorians, contribute to the clan's welfare, and answer Mandalore's call.

The Concordat split this culture down a fault line that was already present. Concordat Mandalorians maintain their traditions within the framework of Republic governance: they train, they serve, they wear armor, and they answer to Mandalorian chieftains for matters of honor and Republic officers for matters of strategy. Neo-Mandalorians maintain their traditions in exile: nomadic, resource-scarce, fiercely orthodox, and increasingly aware that orthodoxy alone doesn't feed children.

**Character hooks:** A Mandalorian character must choose which Mandalorian they are. Concordat or Coalition? Traditionalist or pragmatist? Born Mandalorian or adopted? Which clan? Which campaign did they fight in? Do they see the Concordat as evolution or betrayal? These questions matter more than the armor.

### Sith Purebloods

**What people assume:** The Sith are the enemy. Red-skinned, Force-sensitive, tied to the Empire that nearly destroyed the Republic. Dangerous by nature.

**What is true:** The Sith are a people, not a philosophy. The species called Sith existed on Korriban for tens of thousands of years before the Dark Jedi Exiles arrived and conquered them. The word "Sith" was stolen from them by their conquerors and applied to an ideology that was never theirs.

The old caste system organized Sith Pureblood society into four groups. The **Kissai** were priests and scholars who practiced sorcery and maintained religious tradition. The **Massassi** were warriors who channeled the Force through combat. The **Zuguruk** were engineers and builders. The **Grotthu** were the enslaved lower caste. This system was the backbone of the Sith Empire, and it fractured when the Empire fell.

In the Concordat Era, surviving Kissai scholars maintain traditions in diaspora and on Korriban. Massassi warriors, who suffered the worst casualties during the Republic's post-GHW counter-invasion (Republic forces executed surrendering Massassi soldiers on multiple documented occasions), are scattered, traumatized, and angry. Zuguruk engineers adapted most successfully, finding work across the galaxy where technical skills are valued regardless of species. The old caste system is dead in practice, but its ghost shapes how Purebloods read each other.

Among the oldest Pureblood traditions is the memory of King Adas, who united the Sith species and repelled the Rakata twenty-seven thousand years before the present. The Keepers of Adas maintain pre-Exile cultural practices through oral tradition and a handful of texts in Old Sith. They hold that the "true Sith" are the people, not the ideology, and that the Force traditions of the original Sith predated and were corrupted by the Dark Jedi Exiles' light-side/dark-side framework.

Blood ideology persists: the belief that Sith Pureblood heritage carries inherent spiritual significance. The Triumvirate uses blood ideology to recruit. The Concordat uses it to justify surveillance. Bigots use it to justify discrimination. Sith Purebloods who've integrated into Republic society navigate all three daily.

**Character hooks:** A Sith Pureblood character is one of the most complex options in the setting. Imperial loyalist or diaspora survivor? Kissai-descended scholar or Massassi-descended fighter? Keeper-tradition practitioner who challenges both Jedi and Sith assumptions about the Force? A Pureblood hiding their heritage in Concordat space? A Pureblood who's never seen Korriban and knows their culture only through their grandmother's stories? The choice defines the character.

### Cathar

**What people assume:** Victims. The species that the Mandalorians tried to destroy.

**What is true:** The Cathar are a people defined by survival, not by victimhood. Before the Razing, Cathar were known for their physical capability, their clan-based social structure, and their ferocity in combat. The Razing of Cathar killed fourteen thousand civilians at Ves'kaan and displaced the majority of the planet's population. The survivors scattered across the galaxy as refugees.

Cathar diaspora communities face the particular burden of being a political symbol. Every faction uses Cathar suffering to make its case. The Remnant cites Cathar as evidence that the Mandalorians cannot be trusted. The Concordat cites Cathar as evidence that the old Republic failed to protect its citizens. The Neo-Mandalorians treat Cathar as a difficult subject that most prefer not to discuss.

The Cathar themselves are tired of being someone else's argument. Young Cathar, raised in refugee communities, have opinions about the war that don't always align with their parents' trauma. Some want justice. Some want revenge. Some want to be left alone. Some have joined the Mandalorians, the culture that destroyed their homeworld, because the Mandalorians respect strength and the refugee camps do not.

**Character hooks:** A Cathar character carries the Razing whether they want to or not. How they carry it defines them. Anger, determination, pragmatism, grief, or the refusal to be defined by what was done to their people.

### Miraluka

**What people assume:** Blind Force-seers. Mysterious. Spiritual.

**What is true:** The Miraluka are a near-human species that perceives the world through the Force rather than through biological sight. They do not have functional eyes. Their connection to the Force is not a talent or a gift; it is their primary sense. A Miraluka who loses access to the Force does not merely lose a spiritual connection. They lose the ability to see.

The Force crisis is an existential threat to the Miraluka as a species. As dead zones proliferate and Force reliability degrades, Miraluka individuals experience episodes of blindness: minutes, hours, sometimes days where the Force withdraws and the world vanishes. The Miraluka homeworld of Alpheridies has not yet been affected by dead-zone proximity, but the degradation is measurable and trending.

Miraluka responses to the crisis vary. Some have retreated to Alpheridies. Some have joined the Jedi Council's research efforts. Some have sought solutions outside orthodox Jedi thought, exploring Force traditions that the Order considers heterodox. Some have left Force-dependent professions entirely and learned to navigate the world through technology: sonic mapping, droid assistance, sensory augmentation.

**Character hooks:** A Miraluka character lives the Force crisis in their body. The setting's central problem is not abstract for them. It is the difference between seeing and not seeing.

### Selkath

**The reputation:** The kolto people. Aquatic. Neutral. Wealthy.

**The reality:** The Selkath are an amphibious species native to Manaan, and their relationship with kolto is more complex than an economic arrangement. Selkath religious traditions describe kolto as a gift from the ocean, connected to the life of the planet itself. The Order of Shasa, the Selkath Force tradition, maintains a spiritual relationship with the deep-sea ecology that produces kolto, including sites of Rakatan origin that predate Republic contact by millennia.

Selkath neutrality is a political strategy, not a cultural disposition. The Selkath are neutral because neutrality is the best way to maintain sovereignty when every faction in the galaxy wants what your planet produces. Beneath the diplomatic surface, Selkath society is fracturing over questions of isolation, intervention, and the moral implications of selling healing substances to governments that use them to wage war.

**Character hooks:** A Selkath character navigates the tension between their homeworld's neutrality and the galaxy's demands. A kolto merchant who knows exactly where her product goes. A member of the Order of Shasa who's discovered something about the deep-ocean Rakatan ruins that the Order's elders have been hiding. A Selkath diplomat who's concluded that neutrality is complicity.

### Other Peoples

The galaxy contains thousands of species. A few deserve brief mention for their relevance to the campaign:

**Twi'leks** face ongoing exploitation across the galaxy. Twi'lek trafficking remains a persistent crime that the Concordat has reduced in Core territories and ignored in the Outer Rim. Twi'lek communities on Ryloth and in diaspora maintain cultural traditions of resilience.

**Echani** martial culture treats combat as communication. Echani warriors serve in multiple factions, and the Echani combat tradition is the foundation of Atris's Handmaiden program on Telos.

**Zabraks** and **Iridonians** maintain clan-based frontier cultures that have adapted to the Concordat Era through mercenary service, independent trade, and a pragmatic refusal to commit to any single faction.

**Wookiees** on Kashyyyk face Czerka exploitation that the Concordat has done little to address. The dead zone left by Revan's destruction of the Kashyyyk Star Map has complicated Wookiee relations with all Force-using factions.

**Duros** spacefaring tradition gives them particular relevance in an era of degrading hyperspace routes. Duros navigators are in high demand across every faction.

# PART VI: BELIEF AND MEANING

---

## Chapter 10: Belief, Religion, and Philosophy

### The Jedi Way (and Its Fractures)

Jedi philosophy, in its pre-war form, held that the Force was a living energy connecting all things, that Jedi served as its guardians, and that peace, knowledge, serenity, and harmony were the guiding principles of that service. The Jedi Code was not merely a set of rules; it was a description of the Force's nature and the Jedi's place within it.

The Force crisis has broken this framework open. If the Force is dying, what does it mean to be its guardian? If the Force is unreliable, what does serenity accomplish? If the Jedi who went to war did more damage to the Force than the Jedi who stayed in the Temple, was the Council right all along? And if the Council was right, why does being right feel indistinguishable from doing nothing?

At least three Jedi theological positions have emerged from the wreckage:

**Orthodox restraint** (the Council's position): the Force is damaged and must be allowed to heal. Intervention accelerates the damage. Patience and study are the Jedi's duty.

**Active service** (the Remnant Jedi's position): the Force is damaged because people suffer. Inaction is itself a form of harm. The Jedi's duty is to protect, even at cost.

**Pragmatic instrumentalism** (common among Concordat Advisors and Sentinels): the Force is a tool that works intermittently. Theology is irrelevant. Use what works, carry a backup, and get the job done.

### The Sith Tradition

Sith philosophy, as developed by the hybrid culture that arose after the Dark Jedi Exiles conquered the Sith species, holds that passion is the path to the Force's full power. The Sith Code ("Peace is a lie, there is only passion...") is not a celebration of cruelty. It is a statement about the nature of power: that emotional intensity, ambition, and the willingness to struggle are more honest foundations for strength than the Jedi's cultivated detachment.

In the Concordat Era, Sith philosophy exists in fragments. Some practitioners maintain the Code as a personal discipline, pursuing strength through passion without the institutional structure of the Empire. Others have adapted Sith thought for a post-imperial context: a philosophy of individual sovereignty and self-reliance that resonates with Mandalorian doctrine and with civilians who've decided that no institution will protect them.

The pre-Exile traditions maintained by the Keepers of Adas offer a different reading: the Force as an elemental relationship between the living and the natural world, shaped by passion but not owned by it, older and stranger than either the Jedi or the post-Exile Sith understood.

### The Doctrine of Necessary Strength

The Mandalorian philosophical tradition has become the Concordat Era's most influential secular creed. The Doctrine holds that strength must be self-generated, that reliance on external gifts (including the Force) produces fragility, and that the galaxy's crisis is proof that civilizations dependent on supernatural power will collapse when that power fails.

The Doctrine has adherents outside Mandalorian culture. Former Jedi who've lost their Force connection. Secular intellectuals on Core Worlds. Frontier communities that have survived without institutional support. Anti-Force political movements that argue Force users have been a destabilizing influence throughout galactic history and that the Force's death might be the best thing that ever happened to the galaxy.

The Doctrine is intellectually coherent and emotionally satisfying to people who feel betrayed by institutions that relied on the Force. It is also, in the hands of Concordat Militarists, a justification for Force-user surveillance and restriction.

### Local and Syncretic Traditions

The galaxy contains thousands of Force traditions beyond the Jedi and the Sith. A few that matter for the campaign:

The **Order of Shasa** on Manaan practices a Force tradition rooted in the Selkath relationship with their ocean. Their understanding of the Force emphasizes connection, balance, and the deep ecology that produces kolto. They may understand something about the Force crisis that larger traditions have missed.

The **Baran Do Sages** of the Kel Dor practice a contemplative tradition focused on perceiving the future. The Force crisis has made their visions unreliable, and the Baran Do are debating whether to retreat from public life or engage with the galaxy's crisis directly.

**Anti-Force secularism** has grown in the Concordat Era. On Core Worlds and Mid Rim systems, political movements argue that Force users, whether Jedi, Sith, or otherwise, have caused the galaxy's problems and that governance should be entirely secular. These movements overlap with the Concordat's Force-monitoring programs and with the Doctrine of Necessary Strength, though their motivations range from principled humanism to crude speciesism directed at Force-sensitive peoples like the Miraluka and the Sith Purebloods.

# PART VII: THE MATERIAL GALAXY

## Chapter 11: War, Trade, Technology, and Survival

### How War Is Fought

Every military in the galaxy learned from the Mandalorian Wars and the Force's declining reliability. They learned different lessons.

**Concordat military doctrine** combines Republic naval tradition with Mandalorian ground tactics. Fleet engagements follow Republic patterns: capital ships supported by fighter screens, with combined-arms assault on contested systems. Ground operations follow Mandalorian patterns: rapid deployment, decentralized command, and an emphasis on small-unit initiative that Republic military culture historically lacked. Joint training has produced a competent hybrid force, though command-culture friction remains.

**Remnant military doctrine** is guerrilla warfare adapted for space. Kaeranth's three battle groups avoid direct engagement with Concordat fleets. They raid supply lines, conduct intelligence operations, and strike targets where the Concordat's garrison is thin. The Remnant Jedi serve as special operations units when their abilities cooperate and as conventional officers when they don't.

**Neo-Mandalorian doctrine** is raiding. Fast ships, hard targets, and the discipline to disengage before the enemy's reinforcements arrive. The Coalition's warriors are individually superb and collectively under-resourced. They fight with what they have and scavenge what they need.

Force users in all three forces operate as unreliable force multipliers. Smart commanders plan operations that succeed without Force support and benefit from it when it's available. Foolish commanders rely on Force support and lose soldiers when it fails.

### Supply Chains

Five resources keep the galaxy running:

**Kolto** for medicine. Produced on Manaan, sold to all factions, never in sufficient supply.

**Cortosis** for military-grade hull plating and personal armor. Mined on Serroco, Apatros, and several Outer Rim worlds. Cortosis has the additional property of disrupting lightsaber blades on contact, which makes it strategically significant in any conflict involving Force users.

**Tibanna gas** for blaster weaponry. Processed on Bespin and several gas giants in the Mid Rim. Every soldier who's watched a Jedi's precognition fail mid-battle wants a better blaster.

**Hyperfuel** for starship drives. Refined from various sources across the galaxy. Dead-zone disruption and piracy have fractured the supply chains.

**Food.** The most overlooked strategic resource. The Remnant's twenty-three agricultural worlds are its lifeline. The Concordat's Outer Rim territories produce surplus grain that feeds the Core. The Neo-Mandalorians raid food convoys because they have no agricultural base.

### Technology

Technology has not regressed. But the galaxy's dependence on specific technologies has shifted as the Force becomes unreliable.

Droids have become more important as organic labor is scarce and Force-dependent functions fail. Astromech droids navigate hyperspace routes that Force-sensitive cartographers can no longer maintain. Medical droids supplement kolto treatment in clinics where Force healing is unavailable. Protocol droids handle diplomatic functions that Jedi mediators once performed.

Weapons technology reflects the Force's decline. Cortosis-weave armor, once a specialist material for fighting Force users, has become standard military equipment. Sonic weapons, which bypass Force barriers, have found buyers in every military that fights Jedi or expects to. Personal shield generators, once a luxury, are standard issue for Concordat special operations troops.

The holonet still connects Core and Mid Rim territories. On the Outer Rim, communications depend on relay stations that nobody has funded to maintain since the wars.

# PART VIII: YOUR CHARACTER

---

## Chapter 12: Making a Character for the Concordat Era

### The Questions That Matter

Character creation in the Concordat Era starts with questions, not statistics.

**Where are you from?** The answer determines your character's baseline assumptions about the galaxy. A Core World citizen grew up with functioning institutions and considers the Concordat normal. An Outer Rim frontier resident grew up with none of the above and considers self-reliance normal. A Mandalorian from Dxun grew up in a warrior culture that trained her to fight since she could walk. A Sith Pureblood from Nar Shaddaa grew up hiding her heritage in a galaxy that considers her species a threat.

**What did you do during the wars?** If your character is old enough to have served, which war? The Great Hyperspace War? The Mandalorian Wars? The Ashen Campaigns? Which side? What did they see? What did they do? If your character is too young to have fought, what happened to the people who raised them?

**Who do you work for?** The Concordat? The Remnant? The Coalition? Nobody? Someone specific within those factions, or the faction as an abstraction? Is your employment ideological or economic? Would you quit if you discovered your employer had done something unforgivable, or have you already made that discovery and stayed?

**What do you believe about the Force?** Is it dying? Should it be saved? Is its death a catastrophe or a liberation? If you're Force-sensitive, what does the unreliability of your abilities mean to you? If you're not Force-sensitive, how has the Force crisis affected your life anyway?

**What do you owe?** Debts, obligations, promises, oaths, grudges. Who saved your life? Who destroyed something you loved? Who is waiting for you to come home? Who are you avoiding? The galaxy's political factions are abstractions. Debts are personal.

**What are you afraid of?** Not what threatens your character's life. What threatens their sense of who they are? A Jedi who fears losing the Force. A Mandalorian who fears losing her honor. A Concordat officer who fears discovering that the government he serves is doing something indefensible. A Remnant operative who fears that Revan is never going to be the leader they need.

### Types of Characters That Fit

The Concordat Era supports a wide range of character types. A few that emerge naturally from the setting:

**The Veteran.** Fought in the wars. Carries the experience in their body and their choices. Whether they served with the Revanchists, the Mandalorians, the Republic regulars, or somebody else, the war shaped them. What are they now?

**The Operative.** Works for a faction's intelligence apparatus. Remnant spy, Concordat agent, Sentinel operative, Exchange fixer, or freelance information broker. The Concordat Era runs on information, and someone has to gather it.

**The Exile.** Left something behind. A Jedi who left the Order. A Mandalorian who left the clan. A Concordat citizen who left their home. A Sith Pureblood who left their heritage. What drove them out, and what are they looking for?

**The Believer.** Holds convictions strongly enough to act on them. A true-believing Concordat loyalist. A Remnant idealist. A Doctrine of Necessary Strength convert. A Jedi orthodox. A Sith philosopher. A secular humanist. The Concordat Era tests every belief system. What does yours look like after the test?

**The Survivor.** Did not choose a side. Survived the wars by luck, skill, or pragmatic flexibility. Works the frontier, runs a business, fixes droids, hauls cargo, tends bar, or does whatever pays. The galaxy's political drama is someone else's problem, until it shows up in their cargo hold.

**The Inheritor.** Too young to have fought. Old enough to live with the consequences. Raised in a refugee camp, a military barracks, a Jedi enclave, a Mandalorian clan, or a Sith diaspora community. The war made the world they inherited. What will they do with it?

### Why the Party Stays Together

The Concordat Era's political fractures mean party members may come from opposing factions. A Concordat officer, a Remnant sympathizer, a Neo-Mandalorian warrior, and a Jedi Sentinel have no institutional reason to cooperate.

Parties stay together for the same reason people in crisis form crews: necessity, shared survival, and the bonds that form when you depend on each other under pressure. The Freighter Incident is designed to establish these bonds. By the time the party escapes their first crisis, they've saved each other's lives, shared information that makes them liabilities to their respective factions, and discovered that the person across the table is more complicated than the uniform suggested.

The campaign will test party cohesion. Let it. Characters who disagree about politics but trust each other in combat are more interesting than characters who agree about everything. Argue at the cantina table. Fight back-to-back when the shooting starts.

### A Note on Fate

This campaign uses Fate. Your character's high concept, trouble, and other aspects should reflect the setting's tensions. A high concept like "Disillusioned Concordat Intelligence Officer" carries more weight than "Skilled Spy." A trouble like "My Unit Committed War Crimes at Onderon" generates more play than "Has Enemies."

Your skills and stunts should reflect what your character can actually do, not what the Force might let them do. In a galaxy where Force abilities are unreliable, a character whose competence rests on conventional skills (shooting, piloting, negotiation, medicine, engineering) is never helpless. A character who relies on the Force is sometimes extraordinary and sometimes nothing. Build accordingly.

## Chapter 13: Starting Scenarios

### 13.1 The Freighter Incident

**Premise:** You are aboard a freighter traveling a major hyperlane. The ship drops out of hyperspace six days early, stranded at the edge of a dead zone. The Force doesn't work here. Hyperspace navigation fails. Three other ships are stuck: a Concordat military transport, a Remnant courier vessel, and a Neo-Mandalorian raiding ship.

Nobody can leave. The dead zone is expanding. In three days, it will engulf all four ships. You need to find a way out, which means working with people who are nominally your enemies.

**Tone:** Tense, political, collaborative under pressure. A bottle episode where survival requires cooperation and cooperation requires trust that nobody has earned.

**What kind of characters fit:** Crew, passengers, hired security, or stowaways. Mixed-faction parties are ideal: the scenario forces characters from different backgrounds to negotiate, argue, and depend on each other.

**What you know going in:** Dead zones are real and dangerous. Hyperspace navigation fails near them. Force-sensitive characters will feel the dead zone's effects. The people on the other ships are not your friends, but they're stuck in the same problem.

**Themes:** The Force crisis as a practical threat. Faction loyalty tested by shared survival. Trust earned under pressure. The question of what you do when the rules that define your life stop working.

### 13.2 The Windfall Protocol

**Premise:** You are Sith-aligned operatives. You wake in kolto immersion tanks inside a blacked-out refining station on the seafloor of an ocean moon. The station is in lockdown. Emergency lighting only. The kolto in your tanks is wrong: cloudy, faintly luminescent, and it tastes like copper and ozone.

None of you know each other. None of you remember arriving. Each of you was sent here by a different Sith authority, and your missions contradict. The last station log is three days old. One survivor remains: a dying Selkath foreman named Vossk. Six canisters of refined kolto sit in the docking bay, loaded onto a shuttle that seats one fewer than your party. A Republic patrol answered an automated distress beacon. It arrives in two hours.

**Tone:** Claustrophobic, paranoid, and morally complex. A pressure cooker where secrets collide.

**What kind of characters fit:** Sith-aligned characters with specific, secret agendas. This scenario works as a one-shot with pre-generated character hooks or as an alternate campaign entry for a party of Sith-faction characters.

**What you know going in:** You work for Sith interests. You were sent to this station for a reason. The others were sent for different reasons. The kolto is valuable. The station is compromised. Escape is limited. Trust no one, and remember that the person you suspect the most may be the one you need the most.

**Themes:** Dark-side characters forced into unwanted empathy. Competing loyalties within a single faction. The cost of secrecy. The question of whether people who've been trained to use each other can choose not to.

## Appendix A: Glossary and Proper Noun Guide

**Ahto City** — The surface capital of Manaan, built on platforms above the planet-spanning ocean. Home to the Selkath government and foreign embassies from every major faction.

**Aliit'alor** — Mandalorian term for "honoured integration": the status granted to a conquered world that earned Mandalorian respect through combat.

**Ashen Campaigns** — A series of Revanchist military operations (3957-3956 BBY) using coordinated Force-channeling strikes to break Mandalorian defenses. Effective and devastating; responsible for twelve planets' permanent Force damage and an estimated six hundred thousand civilian deaths.

**Atris** — Jedi scholar and Council member. Designer of the Sentinel Program. Maintains a private academy on Telos with her Echani Handmaidens.

**Beskad** — Traditional Mandalorian saber. The weapon used by Mandalore the Vindicated in the Dhas'kara against Malak.

**Beskar** — Mandalorian iron. A rare metal used in Mandalorian armor, prized for its durability and resistance to lightsaber strikes.

**Canderous Ordo** — Leader of the Neo-Mandalorian Coalition. Traditionalist who rejected the Concordat as dishonor.

**Cassus Fett** — Mandalorian strategist and logistician. Commanded the Cathar operation. Now aligned with the Concordat's Integrationist faction.

**Cathar** — A feline species native to the world of the same name. Victims of the Razing of Cathar (3959 BBY). Scattered in diaspora across the galaxy.

**Concordat** — The political agreement (3955 BBY) merging Republic governance with Mandalorian military strength. Also refers to the government that emerged from it, led by Supreme Chancellor-General Malak.

**Concordat Era (CE)** — Calendar system used by the Concordat government, measured from the signing of the Concordat. The campaign begins in 4 CE (3952 BBY).

**Cortosis** — A mineral that disrupts lightsaber blades and energy shields on contact. Strategically valuable for military applications.

**Czerka Corporation** — The galaxy's largest private resource extraction company. Operates mining operations across the Outer Rim with aggressive labor practices.

**Dhas'kara** — Mandalorian single-combat challenge. Can resolve disputes between clans or challenge Mandalore's authority.

**Doctrine of Necessary Strength** — Mandalorian philosophical framework holding that strength must be self-generated, and reliance on the Force or other external gifts produces civilizational fragility.

**Dead Zone** — A region where the Force is absent or severely distorted. Caused by Sith rituals, Ashen Campaign strikes, or unknown factors.

**Dxun** — Jungle moon of Onderon. Culturally significant to Mandalorians.

**The Exchange** — Criminal syndicate operating across the Mid and Outer Rim.

**Gai bal manda** — Mandalorian adoption tradition. Taking a being of any species into a clan as Mandalorian.

**Great Hyperspace War (GHW)** — War between the Republic and the Sith Empire, ending 3964 BBY. Caused catastrophic Force damage through massed Sith ritual warfare.

**Hrakert Rift** — Deep-sea geological formation on Manaan, near the kolto deposits. Spiritually significant to the Selkath.

**Ka'halar** — Mandalorian life-debt owed by a defeated warrior to the victor who spares them. Binds Mandalore the Vindicated to Malak.

**Kaeranth, Admiral** — Commander of the Revanchist Remnant's military forces. Extracted Revan from the Unknown Regions. The Remnant's operational leader.

**Keepers of Adas** — Sith Pureblood cultural tradition maintaining pre-Exile Sith practices and the memory of King Adas.

**Kissai** — The priestly-scholarly caste of Sith Pureblood society.

**Kolto** — The galaxy's primary healing substance. Produced on Manaan. Accelerates cellular regeneration.

**Korriban** — Tomb world of the Sith. Home to the Valley of the Dark Lords and the ancient Sith academies.

**Malak** — Supreme Chancellor-General of the Concordat. Former Jedi general. Defeated Mandalore the Vindicated in single combat. Authorized the Ashen Campaigns.

**Mandalore the Vindicated** — Leader of the Mandalorian clans. Defeated by Malak in the Dhas'kara. Now serves as Lord Marshal of the Concordat, bound by Ka'halar.

**Manaan** — Ocean world and sole source of kolto. Neutral under Selkath sovereignty.

**Massassi** — The warrior caste of Sith Pureblood society.

**Miraluka** — Near-human species that perceives the world through the Force. The Force crisis threatens their primary sense.

**Neo-Mandalorian Coalition** — Faction led by Canderous Ordo, rejecting the Concordat as dishonor.

**Order of Shasa** — Indigenous Selkath Force tradition on Manaan.

**Rakata** — Ancient species that built the Infinite Empire, powered by Force-based technology. Their civilization collapsed when their connection to the Force deteriorated.

**Resol'nare** — The six core tenets of Mandalorian identity: wear armor, speak Mando'a, defend your clan, raise your children as Mandalorians, contribute to clan welfare, answer Mandalore's call.

**Revan** — Former Jedi, founder of the Revanchists, figurehead of the Remnant. Recently returned from the Unknown Regions. His mental state is uncertain.

**Revanchist Remnant** — Government-in-exile claiming to be the legitimate Republic. Holds twenty-three Outer Rim systems.

**Sentinel Program** — Jedi Council-authorized accelerated training program for Force-sensitive operatives. Designed by Atris, run by Master Kavar.

**Sith Triumvirate** — A shadowy network consolidating Sith remnants toward an unknown purpose. Most people do not know it exists.

**Star Forge** — A Rakatan factory of immense scale, reportedly capable of producing entire fleets by drawing on the Force. Its current status is unknown.

**Star Maps** — Rakatan navigational monuments that record the location of the Star Forge. Five were scattered across the galaxy; two have been destroyed, three remain in various states.

**Veera Ordo** — Canderous Ordo's sister. Concordat Integrationist leader and advocate for Mandalorian-Republic administrative partnership.

**Zuguruk** — The engineer caste of Sith Pureblood society.

## Appendix B: Suggested Reading and Playing

This setting draws on material from the Star Wars Expanded Universe, adapted and altered for the Concordat Era. None of it is required reading. All of it deepens your understanding of the traditions this campaign builds on.

**Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic** (video game, 2003). The baseline. The game that established the era's characters, worlds, and tone. Revan, Malak, Korriban, Dantooine, Manaan, Taris, and the Star Forge all originate here.

**Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic II — The Sith Lords** (video game, 2004). The philosophical spine. Kreia's arguments about the Force, the Sith Triumvirate, the Jedi's fracture, and the concept of wounds in the Force all inform this setting. Play it with the restored content mod.

**Star Wars: The Old Republic** (MMO, 2011-present). Expands the era's species, worlds, and political factions. Sith Pureblood culture, Mandalorian clan politics, and the broader galactic landscape draw from this source.

**The Kotor Comics** (2006-2010). Expand the Mandalorian Wars era with characters and events that inform this AU's history.

For historical parallels: the Napoleonic Wars, the French Revolution's aftermath, the Congress of Vienna, and the Peninsular War all shaped this setting's political dynamics. Any good history of the period will reward a player looking for character inspiration.
