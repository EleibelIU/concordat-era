# THE CONCORDAT ERA — SUPPLEMENT: FACTIONS, HIERARCHIES, AND PEOPLES

## A Companion Document to the Concordat Wars Setting Book

This supplement expands the setting book's treatment of Jedi and Sith internal structures, hierarchies, and factions, and provides deeper profiles of the galaxy's alien species. It is player-facing material, written at the same knowledge level as the main book: what a reasonably informed person in 3952 BBY would know, with annotations where public knowledge is incomplete.

---

# SECTION I: THE JEDI ORDER — RANKS, FACTIONS, AND FRACTURES

---

## 1.1 The Old Order (Pre-War Structure)

Before the Mandalorian Wars, the Jedi Order governed itself as a monastic institution with peacekeeping authority. The Order governed itself as a monastic institution with peacekeeping authority. When the Mandalorian Wars demanded an army, the Order discovered it had never built one.

### The Three Paths

The Order organized its members along three traditional disciplines:

**Jedi Guardians** trained in lightsaber combat, physical combat techniques, and direct intervention. Guardians served as the Order's martial presence: bodyguards, crisis responders, frontline defenders. They were the Jedi most visible to the public and the least influential in the Order's internal politics. The Council preferred Consulars for leadership. Guardians were respected the way a republic respects its soldiers: with gratitude and a firm hand on the purse strings.

**Jedi Consulars** trained in Force study, diplomacy, mediation, and governance. They staffed the Order's academic programs, maintained relationships with the Senate and planetary governments, and filled most Council seats. The Consular path produced the Order's thinkers, negotiators, and institutional leaders. A Consular who spent thirty years resolving trade disputes between Outer Rim systems had never ignited a lightsaber in combat and considered that a point of pride.

**Jedi Sentinels** blended Force training with practical, non-Force skills: intelligence work, technical expertise, field medicine, languages, survival training. Sentinels operated in the spaces between Guardian muscle and Consular statecraft. They worked alone or in small teams, often in frontier regions where the Order's institutional presence was thin. The Order's other paths viewed Sentinels as useful eccentrics. The Sentinels viewed the other paths as sheltered.

The paths were not rigid castes. A Guardian could study diplomacy. A Consular could carry a lightsaber and use it well. But the paths shaped promotion, assignment, and the Order's internal culture. A Jedi's path determined which Masters would train them, which missions they received, and which colleagues took them seriously.

### The Council

The Jedi Council in the years before the Mandalorian Wars consisted of twelve Masters. The exact composition shifted with appointments and retirements, but the Council at the time of Revan's departure included:

**Master Vrook Lamar** — Senior Council member. Human. Guardian-trained, but had served primarily as an administrator and disciplinarian for three decades. Vrook's reputation among younger Jedi was "the voice that says no." His critics considered him a reactionary. His defenders pointed out that he'd been right about every reckless Jedi initiative for thirty years, including the ones that seemed like good ideas at the time.

**Master Vandar Tokare** — The Council's oldest member. Species debated (appears diminutive, green-skinned, of a species that produces Force-sensitive individuals with unusual frequency). Vandar's authority rested on experience and temperament rather than political maneuvering. He listened more than he spoke. When he spoke, the room went quiet. His position on the Mandalorian Wars was cautious engagement rather than strict non-intervention, which placed him in a minority on the pre-war Council.

**Master Zez-Kai Ell** — Human. Consular. One of the Order's finest negotiators. Zez-Kai Ell had resolved three planetary civil wars through mediation and was responsible for the Corellia Trade Accords. He understood politics the way a surgeon understands anatomy. His private assessment of the Council's response to the Mandalorian Wars, recorded in a personal journal that later surfaced in Remnant intelligence archives: "We are not wrong to be cautious. We are wrong to treat caution as a policy rather than a starting position."

**Master Lonna Vash** — Human. Sentinel-trained, one of the few Sentinels to reach Council rank. Vash maintained field contacts across the Outer Rim and understood the frontier perspective better than most of her colleagues. She argued for sending Jedi observers to the Mandalorian expansion zones. The Council voted her down three times before the Cathar footage made the debate irrelevant.

**Master Kavar** — Human. Guardian. The Council's most decorated combat veteran. Kavar had served in Republic military operations before the Mandalorian Wars and understood military command structure from the inside. When the Council authorized the Sentinel Program in 3 CE, Kavar was the natural choice to run it. He accepted with reservations he kept private and a competence that made the reservations academic.

**Atris** — Human. Consular. Historian and archivist. Atris sat on the Council and simultaneously maintained a private research program into Sith artifacts that the other Council members approved in principle and monitored with varying degrees of unease. She designed the Sentinel Program, handed operational control to Kavar, and then withdrew to Telos to pursue her own work with the Echani Handmaidens. Her current relationship with the Council is formally collegial and practically nonexistent.

The remaining six Council seats were held by Masters of various species and disciplines whose names are less relevant to the current campaign. What matters is the pattern: a deliberative body dominated by Consulars, cautious by institutional temperament, and unprepared for a crisis that required action faster than deliberation could produce.

## 1.2 The Revanchist Military Structure (3959-3955 BBY)

When Revan left the Temple with four hundred Jedi, he built an army. Force users served in it. The army's needs came first, and that priority reshaped every Jedi who followed him.

### Ranks

**Field Marshal Revan** — Supreme commander. Revan held no Republic military commission. His authority derived from the Jedi who followed him (the largest Jedi faction outside the Council) and from the Republic military volunteers who joined because their own chain of command had failed them. Revan set strategic direction. He identified which planets mattered, how to frame the conflict for public support, where to apply pressure. He did not manage logistics, troop rotations, or supply chains. He thought at the level of the war. The details belonged to Malak.

**General Malak** — Second in command. Malak held a Republic military commission (granted retroactively by sympathetic officers who recognized the Revanchists as de facto Republic forces). He managed everything Revan did not: operational planning, fleet coordination, ground force deployment, supply logistics, intelligence analysis, and the daily work of keeping an army alive in contested space. Malak's staff included Jedi and non-Jedi officers. He treated both the same, which earned him the loyalty of Republic regulars and the wariness of Jedi who expected deference.

**Jedi Commander** — The most consequential rank in the Revanchist structure. Roughly forty Jedi held Commander rank at the peak of the Revanchist war effort. A Jedi Commander led mixed units of Jedi and Republic regulars, typically at the battalion or regiment level. The rank placed a Force user in direct authority over hundreds of non-Force-sensitive soldiers.

Some Commanders adapted. They learned conventional military leadership, respected their troops' autonomy, and used the Force as one tool among many. These Commanders produced units with high morale and low casualty rates.

Others treated their soldiers as instruments. A Commander with battle meditation could coordinate three hundred troops through the Force, overriding individual combat decisions with a unified tactical awareness. The soldiers survived at higher rates. They also stopped making choices. Veterans of units led by heavy-handed Jedi Commanders describe the experience as "being piloted" — your body moves, your weapons fire, your position shifts, but the decisions are not yours. The soldiers who served under these Commanders have complicated feelings about Force users.

**Jedi Captain** — Mid-rank officers commanding company-level units (sixty to two hundred personnel) or serving as attachés to Republic fleet captains. Jedi Captains were the workhorses of the Revanchist Jedi military. They translated Revan's strategic vision and Malak's operational orders into tactical execution. Many of the Concordat Era's surviving militarized Jedi held Captain rank during the wars.

**Jedi Lieutenant** — Junior officers. Recent Padawans, newly Knighted Jedi with minimal field experience, or Force-sensitive volunteers with accelerated training. Lieutenants commanded platoon-level units or served as second-in-command to Captains. The casualty rate among Jedi Lieutenants was the highest of any Revanchist rank. The burnout rate was higher. A Lieutenant who survived eighteen months in the field was either a future Captain or a future civilian, and often both in sequence.

**Jedi Specialist** — Non-command roles for Jedi who served in support functions: healers, intelligence analysts, battle meditation practitioners, communications coordinators, logistics officers. Many Jedi Consulars who joined the Revanchists ended up as Specialists. They were uncomfortable with command but unwilling to sit in the Temple while the war continued. Specialists kept the army functioning. They were promoted less and thanked less than Commanders. They also slept better.

**Republic Line Officers** — Non-Jedi military officers who served alongside and under Jedi Commanders. Their experience of the war was different from the Jedi experience. They did not have precognition to warn them. They did not have the Force to heal their wounds. When a Jedi Commander's abilities failed in a dead zone, the Republic regulars paid the price. Line officers developed a professional relationship with the Jedi that was respectful, resentful, and essential. A good Jedi Commander and a good Line Officer formed a partnership that was greater than either alone. A bad pairing produced friendly fire, insubordination, or worse.

## 1.3 Post-Concordat Jedi Factions (3952 BBY)

The Jedi Order in the fourth year of the Concordat Era contains at least five distinct groups. They share robes, a tradition, and little else.

### Council Loyalists (~200 Jedi)

**Base:** Coruscant Temple, Dantooine Enclave, Telos Academy, smaller satellite sites.

**Leadership:** Masters Vrook, Vandar, Zez-Kai Ell, Lonna Vash. The surviving Council governs by consensus, which means Vrook's conservatism sets the ceiling and Vandar's patience sets the floor.

**Doctrine:** The Force is damaged. Using it accelerates the damage. The Jedi's duty is restraint, study, and preservation of knowledge until the crisis passes or is understood well enough to address. The Council does not take sides in the Concordat political conflict. Neutrality is policy.

**Internal divisions:** Vrook and his supporters argue for near-total non-intervention. Vandar favors cautious engagement with all factions. Zez-Kai Ell privately believes the Council's understanding of the crisis is incomplete and that restraint based on incomplete understanding is paralysis wearing a different name. Lonna Vash maintains field contacts that the other Council members know about and additional contacts they do not.

**What Loyalists believe about themselves:** They are the Order's conscience. They held the line when everyone else charged into the dark.

**What others say about them:** Cathar burned while the Council debated the proper response to burning.

### Remnant Jedi (~200 Jedi)

**Base:** Scattered across the Remnant's twenty-three Outer Rim systems. No central enclave. Operate from military installations, hidden bases, and Kaeranth's capital ships.

**Leadership:** Master Dorak (spiritual authority), senior Jedi Knights who coordinate with Kaeranth's battle groups. Revan is the figurehead. His actual influence on Remnant Jedi operations is unpredictable.

**Doctrine:** The Force dies because people suffer. Inaction is harm. The Jedi's duty is to defend the innocent, challenge tyranny, and restore the Republic. Using the Force in service of justice does not damage it; failing to act does.

**Military structure:** The Remnant Jedi continue to use the Revanchist rank system: Commanders, Captains, Lieutenants, Specialists. Integration with Kaeranth's naval command is tight. Remnant Jedi are the most militarized Force users in the galaxy and the most conflicted about it. Their lightsabers work when the Force cooperates. They carry blasters for when it does not. Some of them reach for the blaster first now.

**What Remnant Jedi believe about themselves:** They are the Jedi who chose to act. They carry the cost of that choice, but at least they chose.

**What others say about them:** They followed Revan into a war and then into exile. Loyalty to a person is not the same as loyalty to a principle, and Revan is not the person he was.

### Concordat Advisors (~80 Jedi)

**Base:** Coruscant and Concordat administrative centers across the Core and Mid Rim.

**Leadership:** No formal Jedi hierarchy. Concordat Advisors report to their Concordat government supervisors. Informal coordination happens through a network of senior Advisors, the most prominent of whom is Master Doris Fennel, a former Consular who serves as Malak's primary Force advisor and detests the position.

**Doctrine:** The Force's decline is a practical problem requiring practical solutions. Theological debate is a luxury the galaxy cannot afford. Work within the system that exists. Heal who you can. Advise where you're asked. Accept that imperfect governance is better than no governance.

**The contradiction:** Concordat Advisors serve a government that monitors, tracks, and in some territories restricts Force-sensitive citizens. They advise a leader who authorized the Ashen Campaigns and built the Office of Force Affairs. Some have rationalized: Malak provides order, and order is a precondition for healing. Some are quietly miserable. A few feed information to the Remnant or the Council and convince themselves this is conscience rather than treason.

**What Advisors believe about themselves:** They are the Jedi who stayed useful. While the Council debates and the Remnant fights, the Advisors keep the clinics running and the mediators working.

**What others say about them:** They traded their independence for a desk and a government pension.

### Sentinel Program Operatives (~300 active, ~240 after losses)

**Base:** No fixed base. Operatives deploy across all territories. Training facilities on Telos (under Atris's indirect oversight) and at classified locations in the Mid Rim.

**Leadership:** Master Kavar (operational command). Atris (program architect, no longer directly involved). Individual trainers vary in doctrine and method.

**Doctrine:** Variable. The Sentinel Program produces operatives, not philosophers. Individual Sentinels absorb whatever their trainers taught, which ranges from condensed Jedi orthodoxy to stripped-down combat pragmatism to "the Force is unreliable, your blaster isn't, carry both."

**Who they are:** The Sentinel Program recruits Force-sensitive individuals from outside the Order's traditional channels. Accelerated training turns raw recruits into field-capable operatives in months instead of the decade-plus traditional Jedi training requires. The result is a force that is diverse (multiple species, including some Sith Purebloods), young, undertrained by traditional standards, and overdeployed.

**The numbers:** Approximately three hundred operatives trained to date. Thirty-seven confirmed dead. Twelve deserted. Eight show dark-side instability that Kavar's reports describe with clinical precision and personal anguish. Four killed by Concordat intelligence operatives who identified them as threats. The remaining two hundred and thirty-nine are in the field, doing work that ranges from humanitarian assistance to espionage to missions that Kavar will not describe to the Council.

**What Sentinels believe about themselves:** They're doing the work. They weren't asked to debate. They were asked to act.

**What others say about them:** Cannon fodder with lightsabers. The Council's compromise between doing nothing and admitting the Revanchists were right.

### The Independents (~several dozen confirmed, true number unknown)

**Base:** None. Scattered across the galaxy. Frontier worlds, Nar Shaddaa, Hutt Space, anywhere the Order's reach is thin.

**Leadership:** None. That's the point.

**Who they are:** Jedi who left. Some renounced their vows publicly and walked out of the Temple. Some stopped answering communications and disappeared. Some deserted from the Revanchist military after the Ashen Campaigns and never reported to anyone again. A few have been recruited by intelligence networks (Concordat, Remnant, or Kreia's) without knowing whose agenda they serve.

The Independents share no organization and no common purpose. Each one left for personal reasons: disillusionment, trauma, moral injury, exhaustion, the simple realization that the Order they'd sworn to serve no longer existed in any meaningful form. A former Jedi Guardian tends bar on Nar Shaddaa. A former Consular teaches history at a university on Taris under a false name. A former Sentinel runs a freight company on the Outer Rim and carries a lightsaber in a locked box she hasn't opened in two years.

**What Independents believe about themselves:** Variable. Some believe they failed the Order. Some believe the Order failed them. Some have stopped thinking about it.

**What others say about them:** Depends who's talking. The Council calls them lost. The Remnant calls them cowards. The Concordat calls them unregistered Force-sensitive individuals. The Independents call themselves done.

## 1.4 Atris and the Handmaiden Program

Atris occupies a position no other Jedi holds: technically a Council member (never formally expelled), practically independent, and philosophically drifting toward conclusions that would alarm her colleagues if they understood them.

**Her project:** Atris collects Sith holocrons. She maintains a vault on Telos containing artifacts that the Council knows about (three holocrons, seized during the GHW) and artifacts they do not (at least seven additional holocrons acquired through private channels since the Concordat's signing). She studies the enemy. The boundary between studying the enemy and learning from the enemy requires constant maintenance, and Atris has not been sleeping well.

**The Handmaidens:** Approximately thirty Echani warriors trained in a discipline Atris designed. Echani combat tradition treats physical movement as communication. A fight between two Echani is a conversation conducted through strikes, blocks, footwork, and body language. Atris built on this foundation and added techniques for fighting and subduing Force users: Force-resistant combat forms, cortosis-weave equipment, coordination tactics that exploit the gaps in Force precognition, and methods for operating in dead zones where Force users are weakened and the Handmaidens are not.

The Handmaidens are an anti-Jedi strike team created by a Jedi. The Council tolerates this because Atris frames it as a contingency against rogue Force users. The Council tolerates it also because challenging Atris on her own ground requires a kind of intellectual courage that the current Council prefers to deploy elsewhere.

The Handmaidens are loyal to Atris with an intensity that concerns outside observers. They are not enslaved or manipulated. Their loyalty is earned through training, shared purpose, and the force of Atris's personality. Whether that loyalty is healthy depends on whether Atris's judgment remains sound. Current evidence is mixed.

# SECTION II: THE SITH — HIERARCHY, FACTIONS, AND FRACTURES

## 2.1 The Old Sith Empire's Structure

The Sith Empire that fought the Great Hyperspace War was a rigidly hierarchical state organized around three principles: Force sensitivity as the basis of authority, species heritage as the basis of identity, and the dark side as the basis of philosophy.

### The Dark Lord

At the apex: the Dark Lord of the Sith. A single individual who held supreme military, political, and religious authority. The Dark Lord's power was not merely institutional. It was, in the Sith framework, cosmological. The Dark Lord embodied the Sith people's relationship with the Force. When the Dark Lord was strong, the Empire was strong. When the Dark Lord fell, the Empire convulsed.

Naga Sadow held the title during the Great Hyperspace War. His decision to invade the Republic and his mastery of ritual warfare shaped the war's conduct. His death during the Republic counter-invasion shattered the Empire's command structure. The lesson the surviving Sith drew: building a civilization around a single individual's Force connection is a vulnerability, not a strength.

### The Dark Council

Below the Dark Lord: a council of twelve Sith Lords who governed the Empire's major functions. Military operations, intelligence, economic management, colonial administration, diplomatic relations, religious authority, archaeological research, and Force training each fell under a Council member's portfolio. The Dark Council was advisory in theory and factional in practice. Council members competed for the Dark Lord's favor, built personal power bases, and occasionally killed each other.

### Sith Lords and Darths

**Sith Lord** was the generic title for a Force user of significant power and political standing within the Empire. A Sith Lord commanded resources, apprentices, military forces, and territory. The title "Darth" designated a Sith Lord of exceptional power or accomplishment who had earned (or seized) recognition from the Dark Council.

Below the Lords: **Sith Apprentices** (students bound to a specific master), **Sith Acolytes** (students in the Academy system who had not yet been claimed by a master), and **Sith Adepts** (Force users with training but without the political connections or raw power to achieve Lord rank).

### The Caste System

The Empire's non-Force-sensitive population was organized by the ancient Sith Pureblood caste system, which the Dark Jedi Exiles adopted and reinforced:

**Kissai** (Priests/Scholars): Maintained religious tradition, practiced sorcery, oversaw the tombs and temples. The Kissai were the keepers of Sith spiritual identity. Many were Force-sensitive; those who were not maintained administrative and scholarly roles. Kissai families held religious authority that predated the Exiles and survived the Exiles' conquest by adapting their traditions to the new hybrid culture.

**Massassi** (Warriors): The Empire's soldiers. Massassi channeled the Force through combat in a tradition that predated lightsaber use. They fought with alchemically enhanced weapons, Force-augmented strength, and a battle discipline that Mandalorian observers later described as "terrifyingly coordinated." Massassi units in the GHW fought with suicidal bravery and tactical sophistication. The Republic's post-war treatment of captured Massassi soldiers (summary execution on multiple confirmed occasions) is one of the Republic's unacknowledged atrocities.

**Zuguruk** (Engineers/Builders): The caste responsible for the Empire's infrastructure, technology, and material culture. Zuguruk built the temples, designed the warships, maintained the communication networks, and managed the industrial base that made the Empire function. They were the least politically powerful caste and the most practically essential. After the Empire's fall, Zuguruk skills translated well to civilian life. Zuguruk-descended Sith Purebloods in the Concordat Era are disproportionately represented in engineering, technical, and manufacturing professions.

**Grotthu** (Enslaved): The lowest caste. Comprised conquered peoples, prisoners, and Sith Purebloods who had been stripped of caste standing through punishment or loss of status. The Grotthu performed manual labor, served as experimental subjects for Sith alchemy, and held no legal rights within the Empire. The caste was formally abolished when the Empire collapsed (there was no institution left to enforce it), but the social stigma attached to Grotthu lineage persists in some Sith Pureblood communities.

## 2.2 The Sith After the Empire (3964-3952 BBY)

The Great Hyperspace War destroyed the Sith Empire as a political entity. It did not destroy the Sith as a people, a military force, or a philosophical tradition. Understanding what survived and how it organized requires distinguishing between several different groups.

### Military Remnants

Sith fleet elements that survived the GHW and the Republic counter-invasion scattered across the Outer Rim, Wild Space, and the Unknown Regions. These are not organized under a single command. They are independent flotillas, each commanded by a surviving officer of ability and ambition, operating as pirates, mercenaries, or warlords governing small territories in sectors where no one else patrols.

The largest known military remnant is commanded by **Admiral Saul Karath's successor** (Karath himself defected to the Republic during the GHW and currently serves in the Concordat navy; his name is poison among Sith loyalists). The identity of this successor is unknown to Concordat intelligence. The flotilla consists of approximately twelve capital ships and operates in the Sith Worlds border region.

Other notable remnants include:

**The Leviathan Group** — A cluster of five warships operating as mercenaries in Hutt Space. They take contracts from anyone who pays and maintain no political loyalty. Their commander, a Pureblood named Yuthura Ban, is a former Kissai scholar who retrained as a military officer after the GHW destroyed the academy she'd spent twenty years building.

**The Dreshdae Compact** — A loose alliance of three remnant commanders who maintain a garrison on Korriban and claim custodial authority over the tomb world. They are not the Sith traditionalists referenced in the main setting book; they are military officers who seized Korriban as a base of operations and wrapped themselves in religious legitimacy to justify holding it.

**Free Sith Captains** — Independent ship commanders, typically controlling one to three vessels, who operate as raiders, smugglers, or hired guns across the Outer Rim. Some maintain Sith military discipline. Some have abandoned everything except the ships and the skills. A few have quietly integrated into Concordat, Remnant, or even Mandalorian service under assumed identities.

### Diaspora Communities

The Sith civilian population scattered during and after the GHW. The Republic's counter-invasion drove millions of Sith Purebloods from their homeworlds. Some fled to the Unknown Regions. Some were captured and resettled (often forcibly) in Republic-administered territories. Some found their own way to the galaxy's margins.

Major diaspora concentrations exist on:

**Nar Shaddaa** — The largest Sith Pureblood community outside Korriban. Approximately fifteen thousand Purebloods live in the lower districts, maintaining cultural traditions (Sith-language schools, caste-memory ceremonies, religious observances) alongside the economic survival that Nar Shaddaa demands. The community is politically fragmented: some families maintain imperial loyalties; some have assimilated into Hutt Space's multicultural chaos; some young Purebloods have never seen Korriban and define "Sith" by what their grandparents told them.

**Concordat Resettlement Zones** — The Republic (now the Concordat) administers resettlement facilities for Sith Pureblood populations captured during the post-GHW counter-invasion. Conditions vary. Some facilities are functional, providing housing, education, and a path to Concordat citizenship. Others are detention camps in all but name. The Office of Force Affairs monitors resettlement zones with particular attention, since Sith Pureblood populations have higher-than-average Force sensitivity rates.

**Frontier Settlements** — Small communities on Outer Rim and Wild Space worlds where Sith Pureblood families settled independently. These communities are often isolated, self-reliant, and distrustful of all galactic governments. They maintain varying degrees of cultural preservation, from communities that practice daily Sith religious observance to families where the red skin is the only visible connection to a heritage three generations removed.

### The Keepers of Adas

The oldest surviving Sith tradition, predating the Dark Jedi Exiles by twenty thousand years. The Keepers maintain the memory of King Adas, who united the Sith species and repelled the Rakata. Their tradition holds that the "true Sith" are the people, not the ideology that the Exiles imposed.

In the Concordat Era, the Keepers operate as a cultural preservation network, connecting elder Kissai scholars across diaspora communities through oral tradition, a handful of Old Sith texts, and ritual practices that have been adapted for exile conditions. The Keepers maintain no military force and pursue no political agenda. They preserve the Sith people's memory of who they were before the Exiles arrived and redefined them.

The Keepers' Force tradition predates the light-side/dark-side framework. They understand the Force as an elemental relationship between the living and the natural world, shaped by passion but not owned by any single philosophy. This places them in tension with the Jedi (who see Sith Force traditions as inherently dark), with the post-Exile Sith philosophy (which the Keepers regard as a colonial corruption of their original practices), and with the Concordat (which monitors all Sith Force traditions under the same security framework).

For a Sith Pureblood character, the Keepers offer an identity that challenges every faction's assumptions about what "Sith" means.

### The Triumvirate

The Sith Triumvirate is not a government, not an army, not a religion. It is a network, coordinated by three beings who represent different answers to the question: what happens after the Force?

**Kreia** built it. She is a former Jedi Master who spent decades studying the Force's fundamental nature and concluded that the Force is not a gift but a dependency. Her project is the managed severance of the Force from the galaxy. She operates through proxies, agents embedded in every major faction, and a patience that spans decades. Her Triumvirate agents do not know the full scope of her plan. Most do not know each other.

**Darth Nihilus** is the weapon. Born a civilian on Duro, his Force sensitivity awoke during the Burning of Duro (an Ashen Campaign strike that created a permanent dead zone). The experience consumed his identity. Nihilus is a void: a being whose connection to the Force has inverted, turning him into something that feeds on Force energy rather than channeling it. The mask Kreia gave him contains what is left of his identity. Without it, the void expands. With it, Nihilus can drain the Force from a region, a world, a person. Kreia uses him as a precision instrument. She does not deploy him often. Each use risks losing control.

**Darth Sion** is the shield. A former Revanchist Jedi, shattered during the Second Siege of Onderon. Sion's body is held together by the dark side: broken bones, torn muscle, a body that should be dead and refuses to die because Sion's rage and pain sustain it through the Force. He guards Kreia's operations, eliminates threats to her network, and serves as a deterrent. He is also, in Kreia's analysis, a test case. If Sion can survive the Force's death, other dark-side practitioners can too. If he cannot, she needs to know before she proceeds.

The Triumvirate's agents work across factions: intelligence operatives in the Concordat, sympathizers in the Remnant, contacts in the diaspora communities, assets on Korriban. Their operations are subtle. A policy shifted here. A supply line disrupted there. A researcher pointed toward data that advances Kreia's understanding. A rival eliminated before they become a threat. The Triumvirate does not fight wars. It shapes the conditions that make wars serve its purpose.

## 3.1 Cathar (Expanded)

### Before the Razing

The Cathar were not victims before they were victims. They were a people.

Cathar civilization on their homeworld organized around clan compounds in the planet's dense forest and canyon systems. Cathar social structure was matrilineal: clan mothers governed domestic and economic life, while hunt-leaders (male or female, selected by demonstrated competence) organized defense and resource acquisition. Cathar children learned to climb before they learned to walk on flat ground. The forests were home. The canyons were sacred.

Cathar Force sensitivity ran in family lines, though the Cathar understanding of the Force differed from the Jedi model. Cathar Force traditions emphasized predatory awareness: enhanced senses, tracking through Force-augmented smell and hearing, combat reflexes sharpened beyond the limits of Cathar physiology (which was already formidable). The Jedi recruited Cathar children for training. Some families considered this an honor. Others considered it theft.

### The Guerrilla War

When the Mandalorians arrived on Cathar, the clan mothers refused the Mandalorian offer. Not through a single negotiating authority; the Cathar had no planetary government. Each clan decided independently, and every clan decided the same way: no. The forests were theirs. The canyons were theirs. The Mandalorians could demonstrate their strength somewhere else.

The guerrilla campaign that followed was the Mandalorians' worst experience of the Expansion. Cathar fighters used their homeworld's terrain with a fluency that Mandalorian scouts could not match. Canyon ambushes. Night raids using Cathar night vision against Mandalorian helmet sensors. Poisoned water supplies targeting Mandalorian camps while leaving forest aquifers untouched. The campaign cost the Mandalorians more per week than any three other conquests combined.

Cassus Fett was assigned to resolve the problem. He did.

### The Diaspora

After Ves'kaan, the surviving Cathar scattered. Major diaspora communities exist on:

**Taris** — The largest urban Cathar community. Approximately twelve thousand Cathar live in Taris's mid-levels, concentrated in a district locals call the Scratch. The community maintains clan structures adapted for urban life: clan mothers govern apartment blocks rather than forest compounds. Young Cathar train in rooftop gymnastics that echo the climbing culture of their homeworld, adapted for durasteel instead of bark.

**Nar Shaddaa** — A smaller community, more integrated into the moon's multicultural chaos. Cathar on Nar Shaddaa work as bodyguards, enforcers, hunters, and (more than a few) Exchange operatives. Their physical capability commands premium rates in the bodyguard market. Some Cathar on Nar Shaddaa have adopted Mandalorian armor and customs, a choice that older community members regard with feelings that range from complex to volcanic.

**Remnant Worlds** — The Revanchist Remnant shelters approximately two thousand Cathar refugees across its twenty-three systems. These communities have the strongest political identity: anti-Concordat, anti-Mandalorian, committed to the principle that the Mandalorians must answer for Ves'kaan. Young Cathar in Remnant communities train for military service at higher rates than any other demographic in the Remnant.

**Scattered Frontier** — Individual Cathar and small family groups on dozens of Outer Rim worlds. Some fled the homeworld during the guerrilla war. Some were born in transit and have never seen forests. A Cathar teenager on a frontier mining station knows her heritage through her grandmother's stories and a claw-sharpening ritual performed every morning that she does not fully understand but will not skip.

### What the Razing Did to Cathar Identity

Every Cathar alive carries Ves'kaan. The question is how.

Some carry it as rage. These Cathar want justice, want revenge, want the Mandalorians to pay. They join the Remnant, or the Exchange, or whoever will give them the means to fight.

Some carry it as purpose. These Cathar have decided that survival is the best revenge. They build communities. They raise children. They preserve the language and the climbing songs and the clan structures because existing is an act of defiance.

Some carry it as exhaustion. They are tired of being the galaxy's symbol of Mandalorian brutality. They are tired of every politician citing Ves'kaan to make their case. They want to be Cathar, not "Cathar survivors."

And some, the ones who disturb their elders most, carry it as a question. A Cathar teenager who joins the Mandalorians has chosen a culture that respects the strength her people have always possessed. That the Mandalorians who respect her are the same culture that destroyed her homeworld is a contradiction she lives with, because the galaxy is full of contradictions and she is too young to pretend otherwise.

## 3.2 Miraluka (Expanded)

### How They See

The Miraluka have no functional eyes. Their species perceives the world through the Force the way humans perceive it through photons. Light, shape, depth, color, movement: all of these are Force-mediated perceptions for a Miraluka. Their brains process Force information through neural pathways that in other near-human species process visual input.

A Miraluka in a functioning Force environment sees the world with a richness that human vision cannot match. They perceive living beings as patterns of Force energy: emotional states, physical health, intent, and the indefinable quality that Miraluka call *viith* — the way a person's Force presence reflects who they are. A Miraluka can look at a stranger across a cantina and perceive grief, exhaustion, deceit, or kindness without a word being spoken. Miraluka call this perception, and they get annoyed when outsiders call it telepathy. The distinction matters to them even when it confuses everyone else.

### The Crisis

When the Force stutters, a Miraluka goes blind.

Not gradually. Not like a dimming light. The perception vanishes. One moment, the world exists in full Force-mediated detail. The next, nothing. A Miraluka in a dead zone experiences total sensory deprivation: no sight, no Force-mediated hearing (which supplements their biological hearing), no spatial awareness. They can still hear through their ears and feel through their skin, but these senses are supplementary for Miraluka the way hearing is supplementary for sighted humans. Losing Force-sight is losing the primary sense. It is losing the world.

The episodes are worsening. Five years ago, a Miraluka might experience a flicker once a month: a half-second of darkness, disorienting but survivable. Now, episodes last minutes. Some last hours. Miraluka navigators on freight ships have stopped reporting the frequency of their episodes because the honest numbers would ground them, and being grounded means losing their livelihood.

The Miraluka homeworld of Alpheridies has not yet experienced dead-zone proximity. Miraluka elders monitor the Force crisis with instruments and rituals that predate Republic contact. The degradation is measurable. The trend is clear. If the Force continues to deteriorate at its current rate, Alpheridies's Miraluka population will begin experiencing permanent vision loss within a generation.

### Miraluka Responses

**Retreat:** Some Miraluka have returned to Alpheridies, reasoning that the homeworld's distance from the major conflict zones and dead-zone concentrations provides the safest environment. The returnee population has tripled in three years.

**Research:** Miraluka scholars work alongside the Jedi Council's research efforts, contributing a species-level understanding of Force perception that the Jedi have never matched. Miraluka researchers have identified patterns in the Force's deterioration that suggest the damage is not random but structural, as though the Force's connective architecture is failing in a sequence rather than collapsing uniformly. This finding has not been widely published because its implications are alarming.

**Adaptation:** Some Miraluka have invested in technological alternatives. Sonic mapping devices, droid-assisted navigation, sensory augmentation implants that translate sonar and infrared data into neural inputs compatible with Miraluka perception. These technologies work. They are also, for a Miraluka, the equivalent of a sighted person learning to navigate by sound after losing their eyes. Functional but diminished. Survivable but grieving.

**Radicalization:** A small number of Miraluka have concluded that passive research and patient adaptation are insufficient. Some have joined the Remnant, believing that active resistance to the Concordat (which they blame for continuing the Force-damaging policies of the Ashen Campaigns era) is the best way to protect their species. Others have sought unorthodox Force traditions, including Sith techniques, reasoning that any knowledge that might arrest the Force's decline is worth pursuing regardless of its origin. A few have made contact with Kreia's network, though they do not know whose agenda they serve.

## 3.3 Selkath (Expanded)

### The Ocean Relationship

Outsiders understand the Selkath as the species that produces kolto. The Selkath understand themselves as the species that maintains a relationship with something ancient in their ocean.

Manaan's ocean is not ordinary water. The deep trenches, particularly the Hrakert Rift, produce kolto through a process that Selkath scientists have studied for millennia without fully explaining. Kolto's healing properties derive from a combination of organic compounds and residual Force energy that permeates the deep-sea ecology. The Selkath did not discover kolto. They grew up alongside it, the way a forest species grows up alongside the trees.

The Order of Shasa is the Selkath Force tradition that maintains this relationship. Shasa practitioners descend into the deep ocean on pilgrimages that last weeks, communing with the marine ecology through the Force, monitoring the health of the kolto-producing systems, and maintaining Rakatan ruins that predate any other civilization's presence on Manaan. The Order's knowledge of the Hrakert Rift's true nature is the Selkath government's most closely held secret. What they know, and what they are not telling outsiders, is that the kolto production is connected to something the Rakata built. Something that is still functioning. Something that the Force crisis is affecting.

### Internal Politics

Selkath politics in the Concordat Era divide along four positions:

**Isolationists** argue that Manaan should reduce kolto exports, increase prices, and use the leverage to force the galactic powers to leave Selkath space entirely. They are led by **Speaker Shelkar**, an elder statesperson whose argument is simple: every liter of kolto sold to a galactic government is a liter used to keep soldiers alive so they can fight wars that produce more wounded who need more kolto. The cycle benefits everyone except the Selkath, who bear the ecological cost of extraction.

**Commercialists** argue that kolto revenue funds Selkath civilization and that restricting exports would impoverish Manaan without changing the galaxy's behavior. They are led by **Trade Minister Jhosa**, whose office manages the kolto contracts that keep every faction's medical infrastructure functional. Jhosa views neutrality as a product: the Selkath sell healing to everyone, and the price of healing includes the price of leaving Manaan alone.

**Interventionists** argue that Selkath neutrality enables atrocities. Kolto sold to the Concordat heals soldiers who enforce the military governor system. Kolto sold to the Remnant sustains a guerrilla war. Kolto sold to the Neo-Mandalorians keeps raiders alive. The interventionists do not agree on which faction Manaan should support; they agree only that selling healing indiscriminately is not the moral high ground it pretends to be.

**The Order of Shasa** occupies a position outside conventional politics. The Order's concern is the deep ocean, the Rakatan ruins, and the kolto-producing ecology. Their elders have observed changes in the deep-sea ecosystem that correlate with the Force crisis: shifts in kolto composition, behavioral changes in deep-ocean fauna, and energy readings from the Rakatan installations that suggest something is responding to the Force's deterioration. The Order's position is that the galaxy's arguments about kolto distribution are irrelevant compared to the possibility that the source itself is changing.

### Selkath Physiology and Culture

Selkath are amphibious, comfortable in both air and water but more at ease in ocean environments. Their speech in atmosphere has a liquid quality that non-Selkath find distinctive. Underwater, Selkath communicate through a combination of vocalization, bioluminescent signaling, and Force-mediated empathy that is impossible to replicate on land.

Selkath lifespan averages 80-90 standard years. Their society is matriarchal in structure, with elder females holding governance and religious authority. Gender roles are less rigid than this suggests in practice: Selkath culture values competence over convention, and individuals who demonstrate ability in any domain are respected regardless of gender.

The Selkath relationship with water shapes their architecture, their art, and their psychology. Ahto City, the surface capital, is a concession to the galaxy: a place built for air-breathers to conduct their business. The true Selkath cities are underwater. Visitors who have been permitted to visit the deep cities describe structures that integrate with the ocean rather than displacing it, buildings that flow rather than stand, and a quality of light filtered through living water that makes the surface world feel crude by comparison.

## 3.4 Echani (Expanded)

### Combat as Language

The Echani are a near-human species from the Inner Rim world of Eshan. Their defining cultural characteristic is the treatment of physical combat as a form of communication. An Echani fight is not aggression. It is conversation.

Two Echani who meet for the first time will spar. Not to establish dominance. To learn each other. The way a person moves reveals their personality, their emotional state, their training, their history. An Echani can read a sparring partner the way a Consular reads a diplomatic communique: the feint that reveals hesitation, the guard that reveals fear, the strike pattern that reveals whether someone was trained by a parent who loved them or an instructor who did not.

This cultural framework makes Echani formidable fighters and difficult diplomats. Non-Echani find the constant physicality unsettling. Echani find non-Echani communication frustratingly imprecise. Words lie. Bodies, in the Echani view, do not.

### Echani in the Concordat Era

The Echani have not aligned with any major faction as a species. Individual Echani serve in all three governments, in mercenary companies, and as independent operators. Their combat skills command premium rates, and their cultural tendency toward political neutrality (Echani culture values personal truth over institutional loyalty) makes them attractive hires for factions that need skilled fighters without ideological complications.

The most significant Echani presence in the setting is **Atris's Handmaiden program** on Telos. Atris recruited Echani warriors for a specific purpose: to create a force capable of fighting Force users. The Echani combat-as-communication tradition makes them unusually effective against Jedi and Sith, because they can read a Force user's intent through physical movement rather than relying on the Force to predict attacks. When a Jedi's precognition fails in a dead zone, the Jedi fights blind. An Echani fights the same way she always does.

The Handmaidens are Echani warriors reshaped by Atris's training and purpose, which makes them a specific expression of Echani tradition rather than a representative one. Other Echani view the Handmaiden program with reactions ranging from respect (they are excellent fighters serving a clear purpose) to discomfort (they have subordinated Echani individual truth to a single master's agenda).

## 3.5 Wookiees and Kashyyyk (Expanded)

### The Czerka Problem

Kashyyyk in the Concordat Era is a world under corporate occupation that no government has addressed because no government benefits from addressing it.

Czerka Corporation established logging and mining operations on Kashyyyk before the Mandalorian Wars. The Republic permitted it. The Concordat inherited it. Czerka's operations on Kashyyyk employ Wookiee labor at rates that the Corporation's own internal audits describe as "below market standard" and that independent observers describe as slavery with a payroll.

The Wookiees have resisted Czerka's presence for decades. Guerrilla campaigns in the Shadowlands (Kashyyyk's lowest forest levels, where the canopy blocks sunlight and the ecosystem becomes hostile to non-native species) have cost Czerka security forces significant casualties. Czerka's response has been to restrict Wookiee movement between forest levels, a policy that disrupts Wookiee cultural practices tied to vertical migration through the wroshyr trees.

### The Dead Zone

Revan's destruction of the Kashyyyk Star Map created a dead zone in the Shadowlands that has not healed. The zone is small by galactic standards (approximately forty square kilometers), but its effect on Kashyyyk's ecosystem is disproportionate. The wroshyr trees in the zone's boundary have stopped growing. Fauna that relied on Force-connected symbiotic relationships have died or migrated. The Wookiees who maintained spiritual traditions tied to the Shadowlands report that the zone feels "empty in the wrong direction," a description that Miraluka researchers have confirmed correlates with Force-void conditions.

Wookiee relations with Force-using factions are complicated by this. The Jedi did not destroy the Star Map. Revan did, and Revan was a Jedi (or had been). The distinction between "the Jedi Order" and "a former Jedi who acted without the Council's authority" is clear to Jedi scholars and irrelevant to Wookiees living next to a dead zone that a lightsaber-carrying Force user created.

### Wookiee Navigators

Wookiee life-debt culture and mechanical aptitude have made Wookiee co-pilots and navigators valued crew members across the galaxy. In an era of degrading hyperspace routes, Wookiee navigators who learned from pre-war Wookiee spacefaring traditions (which incorporated intuitive route-finding that some researchers believe is a latent Force sensitivity expressed through spatial awareness) are in high demand. A freighter captain with a Wookiee navigator pays a premium and sleeps better for it.

## 3.6 Twi'leks and the Trafficking Crisis (Expanded)

### The Problem That Won't Stop

Twi'lek trafficking predates the Concordat Era by centuries. The cultural fetishization of Twi'lek physical characteristics by other species (particularly humans and Hutts) created a slave market that the Republic restricted in law and failed to eliminate in practice. The wars made it worse. Displaced Twi'lek populations, weakened enforcement infrastructure, and the expansion of ungoverned space all contributed to a trafficking increase that the Concordat has reduced in Core territories (where enforcement is visible and politically rewarding) and ignored on the frontier (where enforcement is expensive and politically irrelevant).

The Twi'lek homeworld of Ryloth maintains its own government, which the Concordat recognizes. Ryloth's leadership has demanded galactic enforcement against trafficking for decades. The demands are received, noted, and filed.

### Twi'lek Culture Beyond the Stereotype

Twi'leks are among the galaxy's most culturally diverse species. Ryloth's clan-based society produces politicians, scholars, artists, engineers, merchants, and soldiers alongside the trafficking victims that dominate outsiders' perception. Twi'lek lekku (head-tails) are sensory organs that facilitate a secondary communication channel: lekku movement conveys emotional states and social signals that non-Twi'leks cannot read without training. A Twi'lek negotiator in a room full of non-Twi'leks is conducting two conversations simultaneously.

In the Concordat Era, Twi'lek communities on Ryloth and in diaspora maintain resistance traditions against both trafficking and cultural erasure. Twi'lek mutual aid networks operate across the Outer Rim, providing safe houses, transit assistance, and legal advocacy for trafficking survivors. These networks are underfunded, overextended, and essential.

## 3.7 Zabraks and Iridonians (Expanded)

### Two Names, One People, Many Arguments

Zabraks are a near-human species distinguished by their cranial horns, facial tattoo traditions, and a cultural emphasis on individual self-reliance that predates and parallels the Mandalorian Doctrine of Necessary Strength.

Iridonians are Zabraks from the planet Iridonia. The distinction matters to Zabraks and confuses everyone else. Iridonian Zabraks maintain that their cultural traditions (the rite-of-passage tattoos, the combat training, the philosophical framework that treats pain as a teacher rather than an obstacle) are specifically Iridonian, not generically Zabrak. Zabraks from colony worlds who practice the same traditions disagree. The argument has been going on for centuries and shows no sign of resolution.

In the Concordat Era, Zabraks maintain a pragmatic relationship with all major factions. Zabrak mercenaries serve in Concordat, Remnant, and Neo-Mandalorian forces. Zabrak merchants operate independent trade routes in the Outer Rim. Zabrak communities on frontier worlds govern themselves with a stubborn self-sufficiency that makes them resistant to recruitment by any faction and valuable allies to any faction that earns their respect.

Zabrak cultural traditions regarding pain and endurance make them effective soldiers and difficult prisoners. A Zabrak prisoner of war is a security problem that most jailers prefer to avoid.

## 3.8 Duros Navigators (Expanded)

### The Species Built for Space

The Duros are among the galaxy's oldest spacefaring species. Their homeworld of Duro (now bearing the scars of the Ashen Campaign's Burning of Duro) produced the galaxy's first non-Rakatan hyperspace-capable ships. Duros culture treats space travel the way agrarian cultures treat farming: it is the foundation of identity, the subject of their oldest stories, and the skill that every Duros child learns before they learn their own history.

In the Concordat Era, Duros navigators are the galaxy's most valuable civilian specialists. Their spacefaring tradition includes route-finding techniques that predate the Republic's standardized hyperspace charts. Where Republic navigation relies on Force-sensitive cartographic data (now degrading), Duros navigation incorporates gravitational mapping, stellar drift calculations, and an intuitive spatial sense that some researchers theorize is a species-level adaptation to millennia of spacefaring natural selection.

A Duros navigator can find a safe route through degraded hyperspace territory that would strand a standard nav-computer. The premium for Duros navigators has quadrupled since the Force crisis began affecting hyperspace reliability. The best Duros navigators name their own price and work for whoever interests them.

The Burning of Duro is a wound the Duros carry. Their homeworld's upper atmosphere is a dead zone. Duro itself is habitable but damaged in ways that go beyond environmental destruction: the planet's ecosystems are failing in patterns that biologists cannot explain through conventional models because the mechanisms are tied to the Force's absence. The Duros diaspora (always significant, given their spacefaring culture) has accelerated. Duro may become uninhabitable within decades if the dead zone expands.
