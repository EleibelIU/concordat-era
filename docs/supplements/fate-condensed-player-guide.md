# THE CONCORDAT ERA — FATE CONDENSED PLAYER GUIDE

## Building Characters, Running Scenes, and Playing for an Audience

This document replaces the system-agnostic Chapter 12 of the main setting book with a fully mechanical guide for Fate Condensed. It also provides party composition guidelines for the Concordat Wars actual play podcast.

You need a copy of Fate Condensed to play. This guide assumes you have one. It does not reprint the full rules. It tells you how to apply them to this setting and this campaign.

---

# PART I: CHARACTER CREATION

---

## 1. The Skill List

The Concordat Era uses a modified version of Fate Condensed's default skill list. Nineteen skills, ten rated above Mediocre (+0), arranged in a pyramid:

- 1 skill at Great (+4)
- 2 skills at Good (+3)
- 3 skills at Fair (+2)
- 4 skills at Average (+1)
- Everything else at Mediocre (+0)

### The Skills

**Academics** — Formal education, institutional knowledge. A character with high Academics studied somewhere: a Republic university, a Jedi enclave, a Mandalorian war college, a Sith academy. What you studied and where you studied it tells the table who you were before the campaign started. Use Academics to recall historical facts, understand legal systems, read ancient texts, or identify the political implications of a Concordat policy.

**Athletics** — Running, climbing, dodging, zero-gravity maneuvering, and every other physical feat that isn't hitting someone. In the Concordat Era, Athletics keeps you alive in collapsing structures, decompressing ships, and dead zones where the Force won't catch you if you fall.

**Burglary** — Breaking into places, cracking security systems, bypassing locks (physical and electronic), slicing restricted databases. The Concordat's security apparatus, the Remnant's hidden bases, and Czerka's corporate installations all present targets. Burglary is the skill of getting where you're not supposed to be.

**Contacts** — Knowing people. A character with high Contacts has a network: informants, old friends, former colleagues, people who owe favors. The network's composition depends on your backstory. A former Concordat intelligence officer has government contacts. A Nar Shaddaa fixer has underworld contacts. A Mandalorian clan member has warrior-culture contacts. When you need information, supplies, or a favor, Contacts is how you find the person who can provide it.

**Crafts** — Building, repairing, modifying, and jury-rigging physical objects. Starship repair, droid maintenance, weapon modification, armor fabrication, medical equipment calibration, and the thousand small mechanical tasks that keep a crew operational. On the frontier, where replacement parts are scarce and repair facilities are distant, Crafts is survival.

**Deceive** — Lying, disguise, misdirection, forged documents, false identities. The Concordat Era runs on intelligence operations and political maneuvering. Every faction has agents operating under cover. A Sith Pureblood passing as a human in Concordat space uses Deceive. A Remnant operative posing as a merchant uses Deceive. A politician telling the Senate what they want to hear uses Deceive.

**Drive** — Piloting. Starships, speeders, walkers, submersibles, anything with a throttle. The skill covers both combat piloting (dogfights, evasive maneuvers, attack runs) and navigation (plotting hyperspace jumps, navigating degraded routes, flying through dead-zone-adjacent space where instruments behave strangely). In an era of unreliable hyperspace, a good pilot is worth more than a good gunner.

**Empathy** — Reading people. Detecting lies, sensing emotional states, understanding what someone wants even when they won't say it. Empathy is the social equivalent of Notice: it tells you what's happening beneath the surface. A Jedi who's lost Force-enhanced empathy and learned to read people the conventional way has earned their Empathy skill the hard way.

**Fight** — Melee combat. Lightsabers, vibroblades, beskad sabers, fists, improvised weapons. Fight covers attack and defense in close quarters. In the Concordat Era, melee combat happens more often than pre-war doctrine assumed, because dead zones neutralize ranged Force abilities and push engagements to physical distance.

**Investigate** — Deliberate, methodical examination. Searching a crime scene, analyzing a data file, tracing a supply chain, piecing together a conspiracy from fragmentary evidence. Investigate is the slow, careful counterpart to Notice's fast, reactive perception. Intelligence operatives, journalists, forensic analysts, and anyone trying to understand what happened (rather than what's happening right now) uses Investigate.

**Lore** — Knowledge that isn't institutional. Obscure history, Force traditions, Sith archaeology, Mandalorian customs, Rakatan ruins, dead-zone phenomena, kolto biochemistry, frontier survival wisdom, and the thousand things you learn outside a classroom. A character with high Lore has spent time in unusual places, read unusual texts, or listened to unusual people. The distinction between Academics and Lore: Academics is what the university teaches. Lore is what the university doesn't know.

**Notice** — Immediate perception. Spotting danger, hearing the wrong sound, noticing the weapon under the jacket, reading the room when you walk in. Notice is reactive and fast. In combat, Notice determines initiative and detects ambushes. In social scenes, Notice catches the detail that Empathy interprets.

**Physique** — Raw physical power, endurance, and toughness. Physique determines your physical stress boxes. A character with high Physique can take hits, endure harsh conditions, and perform feats of brute strength. Mandalorian warriors, dockworkers, beast-riders, and anyone who's spent years doing physical labor have Physique.

**Provoke** — Intimidation, goading, emotional manipulation through aggression. Provoking fear, anger, or reckless action in an opponent. A Mandalorian warrior who wants a fight uses Provoke. A Concordat interrogator uses Provoke. A Sith philosopher who wants you to feel the weight of your own contradictions uses Provoke. Provoke is Rapport's angry sibling: both change how someone feels, but Provoke does it through pressure rather than connection.

**Rapport** — Building trust, establishing connection, persuading through sincerity (or the appearance of sincerity). Negotiation, diplomacy, calming a frightened crowd, convincing someone to give you information because they want to help. The Concordat Era is a setting where trust is scarce and valuable. A character with high Rapport can create it.

**Resources** — Material wealth and the ability to acquire things. Credits, trade goods, corporate accounts, military requisition authority, clan treasury access. Resources determines what you can buy, what you can requisition, and how quickly you can acquire equipment, supplies, or services. A Concordat officer has military Resources. A Hutt Space merchant has commercial Resources. A Neo-Mandalorian raider has whatever they took last week.

**Shoot** — Ranged combat. Blasters, slugthrowers, ship-mounted weapons, thrown weapons. Shoot covers attack at distance. In the Concordat Era, Shoot has become the primary combat skill for practical-minded fighters. When your Jedi Commander's precognition might fail mid-engagement, a soldier who can put blaster bolts on target through marksmanship rather than Force guidance is more reliable.

**Stealth** — Moving unseen, hiding, tailing someone, setting up an ambush. The Remnant's guerrilla operations, Concordat intelligence work, Neo-Mandalorian raids, and Kreia's network of agents all depend on people who can be where they aren't expected. Stealth also covers electronic countermeasures: jamming sensors, spoofing transponders, running silent in a ship.

**Will** — Mental fortitude, emotional resilience, resistance to psychological pressure. Will determines your mental stress boxes. In the Concordat Era, Will also represents your ability to function in dead zones without panicking, to resist Provoke-based interrogation, and to maintain your composure when the Force behaves in ways that undermine your understanding of reality.

## 2. Your Five Aspects

Fate Condensed gives every character five aspects. In the Concordat Era, each one carries specific weight.

### High Concept

Your high concept describes what your character is at their core. For this setting, a good high concept names three things: what you do, who you're connected to, and what complicates it.

Weak: "Jedi Knight"
Functional: "Remnant Jedi Commander"
Strong: **"Remnant Jedi Commander Who Stopped Trusting the Force"**

The strong version tells the table what you do (command troops), who you're connected to (the Remnant), and what makes you interesting (your primary tool is something you no longer rely on). Every word in the aspect should generate play.

More examples:

- **"Concordat Intelligence Analyst With a Conscience"** — You work for the Office of Force Affairs. You're good at it. You hate what you find.
- **"Neo-Mandalorian Mechanic Keeping the Fleet Alive"** — Your job is the Coalition's survival. Your tools are scavenged. Your repairs are miracles held together with spit and stubbornness.
- **"Sith Pureblood Professor Hiding in Plain Sight"** — You teach ancient history on Coruscant. Your students don't know your species. Your long sleeves cover red skin.
- **"Cathar Bounty Hunter Who Took the Armor"** — You hunt for the Mandalorians. The Mandalorians destroyed your homeworld. You wear their armor. You are not confused about this. Everyone else is.

### Trouble

Your trouble is the thing that makes your life harder. The best troubles for this setting are political, moral, or relational. They should generate compels that push you into the campaign's central tensions.

- **"My Unit Burned Onderon and I Signed the Orders"** — The Ashen Campaigns follow you. The Concordat wants your expertise. The Remnant wants your testimony. You want to sleep through the night.
- **"Ka'halar Binds Me to a Man I Despise"** — You owe Malak a life-debt. Mandalorian honor demands you serve. Your conscience demands you resist. Both demands are real.
- **"The Force Is Killing Me Slowly"** — Force sensitivity in a dying Force. Your abilities degrade. Your perception fractures. The thing that defined you is leaving, and you can feel every centimeter of the departure.
- **"Concordat Intelligence Has My Family's File"** — The Office of Force Affairs knows about your sister's Force sensitivity. Your cooperation keeps her off the registry. For now.
- **"Revan Trusts Me With Secrets I Can't Carry"** — You're close to Revan. He tells you things. Some of those things are brilliant. Some are alarming. You can't tell which is which anymore.

### Relationship Aspect

Your relationship aspect connects you to another player character. In Fate Condensed, this should introduce or hint at conflict, an imbalance, or tension that gives the relationship momentum.

Build these at the table during Session Zero. The best relationship aspects create two-way obligations:

- **"She Pulled Me Out of the Wreck at Onderon"** — You owe someone your life. What they ask of you now may cost more than the life they saved.
- **"We Served in the Same Unit Before I Defected"** — You were comrades. Then you chose different factions. The trust is still there. The politics aren't.
- **"I'm the Only One Who Knows What He Did on Serroco"** — Shared knowledge. Shared guilt. Shared leverage.
- **"Her Clan Destroyed My Homeworld and She Knows It"** — The Mandalorian and the Cathar. The cultural weight of that history sits between you at every meal.

### Free Aspects (Two)

Your two free aspects round out the character. Use them for: a skill or reputation, a possession that matters, a belief that drives action, or a connection outside the party.

- **"Fastest Hands on the Outer Rim"** — Reputation. Someone will test it.
- **"The Datacron Revan Brought Back From Lehon"** — An object with secrets. Everyone wants it. You have it. Why?
- **"Strength Is Forged, Not Given"** — The Doctrine of Necessary Strength as personal creed. Compels when faith in self-reliance meets a situation where you need help.
- **"Vossk's Dying Words Won't Leave Me Alone"** — Something the Selkath foreman said on that station. You don't understand it yet. It matters.
- **"I Still Wear the Robes but I Stopped Praying"** — A Jedi identity in crisis. The uniform stays. The faith is gone.

## 3. Stunts

You start with three free stunts. You may take additional stunts by spending Refresh (1 Refresh per extra stunt, minimum Refresh of 1).

Stunts in Fate Condensed follow two basic templates:

**Template A:** Because I [describe how you're exceptional], I get a +2 when I use [skill] to [overcome/create advantage/attack/defend] when [specific circumstance].

**Template B:** Because I [describe how you're exceptional], once per session I can [describe a special effect] instead of [what you'd normally have to do].

### Setting-Specific Stunts

The following stunts are built for the Concordat Era. They are examples, not an exhaustive list. Build your own using these as models.

#### Combat Stunts

**Dead Zone Fighter.** Because I trained for the worst conditions, I get +2 when I use Fight to attack in dead zones or areas of Force disruption. (For non-Force users who've learned to exploit the gaps in Force-dependent opponents.)

**Beskad Duelist.** Because I trained in traditional Mandalorian saber combat, I get +2 when I use Fight to defend against lightsaber-wielding opponents. (The beskad forms were developed to counter Jedi. They still work.)

**Suppressive Fire.** Because I understand fire discipline, once per session I can use Shoot to create the advantage **Pinned Down** on every enemy in a zone, with a single roll against the highest opposition.

**Cortosis Armor Training.** Because I wear cortosis-weave armor and know how to use it, I get +2 when I use Physique to defend against lightsaber attacks or Force-enhanced strikes.

#### Piloting Stunts

**Dead Reckoning.** Because I navigate by instinct when instruments fail, I get +2 when I use Drive to overcome hyperspace navigation challenges in degraded or dead-zone-adjacent space.

**Guerrilla Pilot.** Because I've run Remnant supply lines for three years, once per session I can automatically succeed on a Drive roll to escape pursuit when I'm in a system where I've previously established a hidden route.

**Astromech Partnership.** Because my astromech and I work as one unit, I get +2 when I use Drive to create advantages related to navigation, sensor evasion, or emergency course correction.

#### Social Stunts

**Concordat Credentials.** Because I carry legitimate Concordat authorization, I get +2 when I use Rapport to overcome bureaucratic obstacles in Concordat-administered territory.

**Clan Reputation.** Because my clan name carries weight, I get +2 when I use Contacts to create advantages when dealing with Mandalorians who recognize my clan affiliation.

**Read the Room.** Because I've survived enough political meetings to know when the mood shifts, once per session I can use Notice instead of Empathy to determine the emotional state and hidden agenda of everyone present in a social scene.

**Exchange Fixer.** Because I know the underworld's channels, I get +2 when I use Contacts to overcome obstacles related to acquiring black-market goods, information, or services in Exchange-controlled territory.

#### Knowledge and Investigation Stunts

**Sith Archaeologist.** Because I've studied pre-Exile Sith traditions, I get +2 when I use Lore to overcome challenges related to Sith ruins, artifacts, Old Sith language, or Korriban tomb complexes.

**Kolto Specialist.** Because I trained in kolto medicine, I get +2 when I use Academics to create advantages related to medical treatment, kolto quality assessment, or diagnosing injuries.

**Intelligence Analyst.** Because I've spent years reading signals intelligence, I get +2 when I use Investigate to create advantages by analyzing intercepted communications, shipping manifests, or military deployments.

**War Historian.** Because I've studied the Mandalorian Wars in detail, once per session I can declare a story detail about a past military engagement, veteran, or strategic location as though I'd spent a fate point to invoke an aspect.

#### Survival and Technical Stunts

**Jury-Rigger.** Because I fix things with whatever's available, I get +2 when I use Crafts to overcome equipment failure when working with improvised materials or incomplete parts.

**Frontier Survivor.** Because I've lived on worlds no faction bothers to govern, I get +2 when I use Will to defend against mental stress caused by isolation, deprivation, or hostile environmental conditions.

**Droid Whisperer.** Because I understand droid systems at an intuitive level, I get +2 when I use Crafts to create advantages by modifying, reprogramming, or repairing droids.

## 4. The Force as a Mechanical Extra

The Force in the Concordat Era is not a skill. It is an **extra**: a bundle of permissions and costs that modifies how your character interacts with the world.

### The Force-Sensitive Extra

**Permission:** Your high concept or one of your aspects must reference Force sensitivity.

**Cost:** One of your stunt slots (you get two free stunts instead of three) AND your trouble must involve the Force crisis in some way.

**What it gives you:**

**Force Techniques** — You choose two Force techniques from the list below. Each technique lets you use a specific skill in an expanded way. You may purchase additional techniques as stunts.

**Force Instability** — The GM has a standing compel against your Force-related aspects whenever you use a Force technique. The GM does not need a specific justification beyond "the Force is unreliable." You get a fate point when this compel triggers, as normal. This means that Force-sensitive characters feed the fate point economy: the Force fails on them, they earn fate points, and they spend those fate points on non-Force solutions. The mechanic reinforces the setting's themes without punishing players for choosing Force-sensitive characters.

### Force Techniques

Each technique pairs with a skill and expands what that skill can do. A Force technique does not replace the skill. It adds capability.

**Telekinesis (Physique)** — You can use Physique to move objects at a distance, create barriers of force, and perform feats of physical manipulation without touching the target. When the Force cooperates, this is as effective as physical strength applied at range.

**Precognition (Notice)** — You can use Notice to sense danger before it arrives, perceive the immediate intentions of beings in your vicinity, and react to threats before they materialize. When the Force cooperates, this is supernatural awareness. When it doesn't, you're just observant.

**Force Healing (Academics)** — You can use Academics to accelerate physical recovery, reduce the severity of consequences, and stabilize dying characters. This supplements kolto treatment rather than replacing it. When the Force cooperates, you can start clearing a mild consequence in the same scene it was taken.

**Battle Meditation (Rapport)** — You can use Rapport to coordinate allied actions in combat, creating advantages that apply to multiple allies simultaneously. When the Force cooperates, you can grant a free invoke to every ally in a zone with a single create advantage action. This technique is what Revanchist Jedi Commanders used to coordinate their units. It is also what made the Ashen Campaigns possible.

**Force Empathy (Empathy)** — You can use Empathy to perceive emotional states, detect deception, and read intentions at a depth beyond normal observation. When the Force cooperates, you perceive *viith* the way a Miraluka does: the Force-signature of a person's character, grief, intent, and history.

**Force Concealment (Stealth)** — You can use Stealth to suppress your Force presence, become invisible to Force-enhanced perception, and move undetected through areas monitored by Force-sensitive beings. When the Force cooperates, you are a gap in the Force's fabric. The Sentinel Program teaches this technique to every operative. Kreia's agents have mastered it.

**Lightsaber Mastery (Fight)** — You can use Fight with a lightsaber to deflect blaster bolts (defend against Shoot attacks), cut through materials that would stop conventional weapons, and perform combat maneuvers that depend on Force-enhanced reflexes. When the Force cooperates, a lightsaber master is the most dangerous combatant in any room. When it doesn't, you're holding a plasma torch with no precognition and hoping your footwork is enough.

**Force Persuasion (Provoke or Rapport)** — You can use Provoke or Rapport to influence the thoughts and decisions of weak-willed targets, plant suggestions, and project emotional states onto others. This technique is ethically contested within every Force tradition and mechanically resisted by Will. When the Force cooperates, a strong practitioner can end a negotiation with a sentence. When it doesn't, you just said something strange to a person who is now suspicious of you.

### How Force Instability Works at the Table

When a Force-sensitive character attempts a Force technique, the GM may compel their Force-related trouble aspect (or any other relevant aspect) to represent the Force failing. The player has the standard options:

**Accept the compel:** Take the fate point. The Force fails. You must find a non-Force solution. Your telekinetic grab drops the crate. Your precognition goes silent mid-fight. Your healing sputters and the patient doesn't stabilize.

**Buy off the compel:** Spend a fate point. The Force works this time. You succeed as normal.

**The GM's guideline:** Compel Force instability roughly one-third of the time in normal space. Compel it two-thirds of the time near dead zones. Compel it automatically inside dead zones (the player must spend a fate point for any Force technique to function at all, and even then results are degraded).

This creates a fate point economy where Force-sensitive characters are rich in fate points (they get compelled often) but must spend those points to guarantee their abilities work. Non-Force-sensitive characters are more reliable but earn fate points through other compels. The party's economy balances naturally.

### Miraluka Characters and Force Instability

Miraluka characters perceive the world through the Force. For Miraluka, Force instability doesn't just mean a technique fails. It means they lose their primary sense. A compel against a Miraluka's Force-related aspect can mean temporary blindness: the character cannot use Notice or Investigate through Force perception until the scene ends or they spend a fate point to reassert their connection.

This is harsh. It is also accurate to the setting. Miraluka players should expect their characters to experience moments of terrifying vulnerability. The trade-off: Miraluka characters receive the Force Empathy technique for free (in addition to their two chosen techniques), because Force perception is their baseline existence, not a special ability.

## 5. Stress and Consequences

Fate Condensed's default stress and consequence system works for the Concordat Era without modification.

**Physical stress:** Three 1-point boxes (more with higher Physique).
**Mental stress:** Three 1-point boxes (more with higher Will).

**Consequences:**
- Mild (absorbs 2 shifts) — *Bruised Ribs*, *Shaken Confidence*, *Force Headache*
- Moderate (absorbs 4 shifts) — *Blaster Wound to the Shoulder*, *Broken Trust*, *Force Sensitivity Flickering*
- Severe (absorbs 6 shifts) — *Crushed Leg*, *Deep Psychological Trauma*, *Force Connection Severed*

**Setting-specific note on consequences:** In the Concordat Era, Force-related consequences are slow to heal. A mild consequence like *Force Headache* clears normally. A moderate consequence like *Force Sensitivity Flickering* requires time in a region with stable Force presence (not near any dead zone). A severe consequence like *Force Connection Severed* is a campaign-altering event that may require a dedicated story arc to address. The Force does not heal on the galaxy's schedule anymore.

**Kolto and consequence recovery:** Kolto treatment (represented by an Academics overcome roll by a character with medical training) can begin clearing physical consequences one step faster than normal. Kolto is the setting's healing accelerant. Its availability depends on the party's Resources and location. On the frontier, kolto is currency. In the Core Worlds, it's expensive but obtainable. On Manaan, it's everywhere and its use is politically charged.

## 6. Refresh and Fate Points

**Starting Refresh:** 3 (reduced by 1 for each stunt beyond your three free stunts, minimum 1).

**Force-sensitive characters** spend one stunt slot on the Force-Sensitive extra, leaving two free stunts. A Force-sensitive character who takes no additional stunts has Refresh 3. A Force-sensitive character who takes one additional stunt has Refresh 2.

**Fate point economy in the Concordat Era:** This setting generates compels constantly. The political tensions, the Force crisis, the overlapping loyalties, and the troubled histories built into character aspects mean the GM has compel material in every scene. Players should expect to earn 2-4 fate points per session through compels and spend them freely. The economy is meant to flow, not to be hoarded.

## 7. Party Composition for the Podcast

The Concordat Wars actual play podcast will feature a party of player characters whose composition should satisfy three requirements: factional diversity, mechanical coverage, and dramatic friction.

### Factional Diversity

The party should include characters connected to at least three different factions. The Freighter Incident (the campaign's opening scenario) strands ships from multiple factions in the same dead zone. The party forms from this crisis. The characters who emerge from it carry factional loyalties that will generate conflict for the rest of the campaign.

A strong party might include:

**One character connected to the Concordat** — A military officer, an intelligence operative, a bureaucrat, or a Mandalorian serving in the joint forces. This character represents the setting's dominant power and brings access to institutional resources, military contacts, and the moral burden of serving a government that does questionable things effectively.

**One character connected to the Remnant** — A guerrilla fighter, a Jedi Commander, an idealist civilian, or a defector from the Concordat who chose principle over stability. This character represents the opposition and brings democratic conviction, military resourcefulness, and the question of what loyalty to an ideal costs when the ideal can't feed you.

**One character from outside the major factions** — A Mandalorian who rejected both the Concordat and the Coalition. A Sith Pureblood navigating diaspora life. A frontier independent who answers to no government. An Exchange operative. A Selkath with kolto connections. This character represents the galaxy's margins and brings perspective that faction loyalists miss.

**One wild card** — A character whose factional allegiance is ambiguous, divided, or in transition. A Sentinel operative with unclear orders. A former Jedi Independent dragged back into the conflict. A Sith-aligned agent whose true employer is unknown (possibly to the player as well, if the GM and player agree to that arrangement). This character creates the party's most unpredictable dynamic.

### Mechanical Coverage

The party needs characters who can handle the campaign's four primary activity types. This does not mean one character per type. Overlap is fine. Gaps are dangerous.

**Combat capability.** At least two characters should be competent fighters (Fight or Shoot at Good (+3) or better). The campaign includes action scenes. A party that can't fight will die. Force-sensitive combatants count, but remember: their abilities are unreliable. At least one party member should be a reliable non-Force combatant.

**Social capability.** At least one character should be a strong social operator (Rapport, Deceive, Provoke, or Empathy at Good (+3) or better). The campaign's political intrigue and intelligence operations require characters who can negotiate, manipulate, and read people. A party of pure fighters will win battles and lose the war.

**Technical capability.** At least one character should handle ships, droids, and equipment (Drive, Crafts, or Burglary at Good (+3) or better). The party will fly ships, repair equipment in the field, slice security systems, and maintain the technology that keeps them alive. A party without a mechanic or a pilot is a party that gets stranded.

**Knowledge and investigation.** At least one character should be the one who figures things out (Investigate, Lore, or Academics at Good (+3) or better). The campaign's mysteries, from the Force crisis to Kreia's network to the political conspiracies running through every faction, require someone who can gather information, analyze it, and connect the pieces.

### Dramatic Friction

The most important party composition principle for actual play: **the characters should disagree about things that matter.**

A party that agrees on politics is boring to listen to. A party where the Concordat officer and the Remnant guerrilla argue about whether Malak's government is legitimate, where the Mandalorian and the Cathar sit with the weight of history between them, where the Jedi and the Sith Pureblood discover they have more in common than their traditions admit, that party produces scenes worth recording.

Build friction into the party at Session Zero. The relationship aspects are your tool. Every relationship aspect should contain a seed of conflict. Not hostility. Not incompatibility. Conflict: a difference in values, priorities, loyalties, or understanding that produces interesting scenes when it surfaces.

**What friction is not:** Friction is not a player character working against the party, stealing from other characters, or prioritizing individual goals over the group's survival. That's not friction. That's sabotage, and it kills actual play campaigns.

**What friction is:** Two characters who care about different things having to make a shared decision. A Concordat officer who believes in the law and a Remnant operative who believes the law is unjust, both looking at the same evidence and reaching different conclusions about what to do. That's a scene. That's a conversation the audience leans into.

## 8. Character Types That Work for Actual Play

Some character concepts produce better actual play than others. The difference is not quality. It's audibility. A character who works in prose might not work on a microphone.

### Characters That Play Well on a Podcast

**Characters with opinions they express out loud.** A brooding loner who processes everything internally produces dead air. A character who argues, jokes, questions, challenges, and tells stories produces content. Build characters who talk.

**Characters who react to other characters.** The party dynamic is the podcast's engine. A character who engages with the other player characters, who has opinions about what they do and say, who pushes back and gets pushed back, drives scenes forward. Build characters who care about the people at the table.

**Characters with visible internal conflicts.** A character torn between loyalty and conscience, between duty and desire, between two versions of who they could be, gives the audience something to invest in. The audience roots for characters who struggle with decisions, not characters who always know the right answer.

**Characters who do things.** Fate Condensed rewards proactive characters. A character who takes action (even wrong action) produces more play than a character who waits for the GM to present options. Build characters who walk through doors, start conversations, pick fights, and make plans.

### Characters That Struggle on a Podcast

**The silent observer.** A character built around watching and waiting. Effective in fiction. Deadly on audio. If your character concept is "the quiet one," give them a reason to talk when it matters.

**The lone wolf with no reason to stay.** The party stays together because the characters choose to stay together. If your character has no bonds, no debts, and no reason to care about the other characters, you will spend every session explaining why you're still here instead of playing.

**The character who already knows everything.** A know-it-all flattens mystery and investigation scenes. Build a character who's smart about some things and curious about others. The audience wants to discover alongside you.

**The character whose concept is a secret.** If your character's most interesting quality is something the other characters (and the audience) can't know, that quality doesn't exist for the podcast until it's revealed. Secrets are fine as one element. They shouldn't be the whole character.

## 9. Session Zero: Building the Party Together

Session Zero is where the party becomes a party. For the Concordat Wars actual play, Session Zero has a specific agenda:

### Step 1: Faction Selection

Each player states which faction their character is connected to. The GM confirms that the party has the factional diversity described in Section 7. If everyone wants to play Remnant characters, the GM redirects until the mix works. The campaign needs friction.

### Step 2: Shared History

Before individual character creation, the table establishes shared history. Answer these as a group:

- **Which war touched all of you?** The Great Hyperspace War? The Mandalorian Wars? The Ashen Campaigns? The answer determines the party's shared frame of reference.
- **What event connects you before the Freighter Incident?** You don't need to have met, but your histories should rhyme. Maybe two of you fought on opposite sides of the same battle. Maybe one of you arrested the other's sibling. Maybe all of you were on Onderon when it burned. The connection doesn't need to be friendly. It needs to exist.
- **What is the party's first shared problem?** The Freighter Incident provides this: survival in a dead zone. But your characters' personal problems should overlap in at least one place. If the Concordat officer's intelligence file and the Remnant operative's mission target the same person, the campaign has a hook that both characters care about for different reasons.

### Step 3: Individual Character Creation

Build characters using the rules in Sections 1-6. Do this at the table, out loud, with the other players listening. In Fate Condensed, character creation is play. Every aspect, every skill choice, and every stunt tells the table something about who you are.

**Build relationship aspects last.** After everyone has their high concept and trouble, look around the table and build your relationship aspect based on what the other players have created. The best relationship aspects emerge from the collision between two characters' concepts.

### Step 4: The Opening Question

Each player answers one question in character, out loud, before the campaign begins:

**"What do you believe about the Concordat, and what would change your mind?"**

The answer establishes your character's political position for the audience. It also establishes that your character has a position that could change, which is essential for a campaign built on political intrigue.

## 10. Dead Zones as Mechanical Environments

Dead zones are regions where the Force has failed. They are the campaign's most distinctive environmental hazard and require specific mechanical treatment.

### In a Dead Zone:

**All Force techniques require a fate point to attempt.** The Force does not function here by default. A Force-sensitive character who wants to use any Force technique must spend a fate point, and even then the result is degraded (the GM may set the difficulty one or two steps higher than it would be outside the zone).

**Miraluka characters are blind.** Unless they spend a fate point per scene to maintain Force perception. This is expensive and represents the character's desperate effort to hold onto their primary sense in an environment that actively denies it.

**Force-related aspects cannot be invoked for free.** If a character has a Force-related aspect (e.g., "Remnant Jedi Commander Who Stopped Trusting the Force"), the non-Force elements of the aspect can still be invoked (Commander, Remnant), but Force-specific invocations (Jedi combat prowess, Force intuition) require a fate point as though they were a compel buy-off.

**Lightsabers still work.** They are plasma weapons powered by kyber crystals, not by the Force. A lightsaber in a dead zone cuts, blocks, and burns. The Force-enhanced reflexes, precognition, and combat awareness that make a lightsaber master deadly? Those don't.

**Dead zone compels are free for the GM.** The GM does not need to offer a fate point for dead-zone-related compels against Force-sensitive characters. The dead zone itself is the compel. (Players still receive fate points for these compels, as normal.) This creates a fate point surplus for Force-sensitive characters in dead zones, which represents the narrative reality: when the Force fails, you fall back on grit, skill, and fate's favor.

### Dead Zone Scale

Dead zones vary in severity. The GM determines the zone's rating:

- **Partial (rating 1):** Force techniques work intermittently. Compel frequency: roughly half the time. The Force stutters.
- **Significant (rating 2):** Force techniques require effort. All Force technique difficulties increase by +2. Compel frequency: most of the time.
- **Total (rating 3):** The Force is absent. Force techniques require a fate point and operate at +4 difficulty. Miraluka are fully blind. Force-related consequences cannot begin healing.

## 11. The Sovereignty Gradient

The campaign uses a five-tier sovereignty system to describe who controls a region and how dangerous it is. This is not a mechanical system with dice interactions. It is a narrative framework that tells the GM (and the players) what kind of trouble to expect.

**Tier 1 — Core Sovereignty.** Concordat-controlled Core Worlds. Coruscant, Alderaan, Corellia. Full government services, law enforcement, medical infrastructure, holonet access. Trouble here is political and subtle: intelligence operations, Senate intrigue, Force-monitoring programs. Combat is rare and carries severe consequences.

**Tier 2 — Administered Territory.** Concordat-controlled Mid Rim. Taris, Telos. Military governors, joint Mandalorian-Republic patrols, functional but stressed infrastructure. Trouble is political with an edge: smuggling operations, faction espionage, occasional armed resistance. Combat happens but attracts official attention.

**Tier 3 — Contested Space.** Regions where multiple factions claim authority. Onderon, Dantooine, the Perlemian corridor. Overlapping jurisdictions, unreliable services, active intelligence operations by all factions. Trouble is constant and varied: guerrilla raids, diplomatic crises, criminal enterprises, dead-zone encounters. Combat is common and consequences are negotiable.

**Tier 4 — Frontier.** Outer Rim regions with minimal governance. The Remnant's twenty-three systems, Neo-Mandalorian operating territory, Hutt Space border regions. Limited infrastructure, unreliable communications, danger from multiple sources (pirates, warlords, wildlife, dead zones). Trouble is survival. Combat is expected.

**Tier 5 — Ungoverned / Wild Space.** Beyond the frontier. No government services, no reliable hyperspace routes, no medical infrastructure, no law. Sith remnant territories, unexplored systems, deep frontier. Trouble is everything. Combat is just another Thursday.

## 12. Kolto as a Campaign Resource

Kolto is the setting's most important consumable resource. Mechanically, kolto works as follows:

**Kolto treatment** allows a character with Academics (medical training) to begin clearing a physical consequence one recovery step faster than normal. A mild consequence that would normally clear after one full scene can begin clearing immediately. A moderate consequence that would normally require a session can begin clearing after one full scene. A severe consequence still requires significant time but can begin the recovery process sooner.

**Kolto availability** depends on the sovereignty tier:

- Tier 1-2: Available at standard prices. An Overcome roll with Resources at Fair (+2) difficulty.
- Tier 3: Expensive. Resources at Good (+3) difficulty, or Contacts at Fair (+2) to find a black-market source.
- Tier 4: Scarce. Resources at Great (+4) difficulty, or a side mission to acquire it.
- Tier 5: Absent unless you brought it. If you didn't bring kolto to Wild Space, you don't have kolto in Wild Space.

**Experimental kolto** (the modified substance from the Windfall Protocol scenario) is a plot device, not a standard resource. Its effects are GM-determined and always include complications.

## 13. Advancement

Fate Condensed handles advancement through milestones. The Concordat Era uses the standard milestone system with setting-specific guidance:

### Minor Milestones (end of a session)

Switch the ratings of two skills, change a stunt, purchase a new stunt (reducing Refresh), or change an aspect that isn't your high concept.

**Setting guidance:** Minor milestones in the Concordat Era often reflect political shifts. A character who started as a Concordat loyalist and has been exposed to the Remnant's perspective might change a free aspect from **"The Concordat Keeps the Lights On"** to **"The Lights Are On But Who Pays the Bill?"** The aspect change reflects character growth without rebuilding the character.

### Significant Milestones (end of a story arc)

Everything from a minor milestone, plus: gain one skill point (raise a skill by one step, maintaining the pyramid).

**Setting guidance:** Significant milestones correspond to major campaign events. The resolution of the Freighter Incident. The completion of an intelligence operation. A diplomatic breakthrough or collapse. The skill point gained should reflect what the character learned during the arc. A character who spent the arc navigating dead zones might raise Drive. A character who spent it in political negotiations might raise Rapport.

### Major Milestones (end of a campaign phase)

Everything from a significant milestone, plus: gain an additional point of Refresh, rename your high concept.

**Setting guidance:** Major milestones in the Concordat Era are moments where the galaxy's political order shifts and the characters' relationship to it changes. The fall of a government. The revelation of the Triumvirate's existence. A fundamental shift in the Force crisis. Renaming your high concept at a major milestone represents who your character has become after what they've been through. **"Remnant Jedi Commander Who Stopped Trusting the Force"** might become **"The Commander Who Found Something Beyond the Force."** The character is still the same person. The person has changed.

## 14. What the GM Is Doing

This section is player-facing because understanding your GM's priorities makes you a better player.

**The GM compels your aspects constantly.** This is not adversarial. It is the engine that keeps the fate point economy flowing and the story moving. When the GM compels your trouble, they are paying you to have a harder, more interesting time. Accept compels generously. The fate points you earn are the fuel for the moments when you need to be extraordinary.

**The GM builds scenes around faction tension.** Every scene in the Concordat Era should involve at least two factions' interests. A supply run isn't just logistics. It's logistics through a region where the Concordat, the Remnant, and the Neo-Mandalorians all have reasons to care about what's in your cargo hold. When the GM frames a scene, think about which faction's interests are at stake and how your character's loyalties intersect with those interests.

**The GM uses the Force crisis as a pressure valve.** When a session needs tension, the Force stutters. When a character is too comfortable, the Force reminds them it's dying. The Force crisis is not a background detail. It is the GM's tool for keeping the campaign's central anxiety present in every scene, and it generates fate points for the characters who feel it most.

**The GM rewards proactive play.** In Fate Condensed, the most powerful thing a player can do is create advantages before a conflict starts. Scouting the location, researching the opposition, calling in favors, setting up a position. The GM will build scenes with enough lead time for proactive players to prepare. Use that time. The character who spends three rounds of an intelligence-gathering scene creating advantages before the shooting starts will dominate the conflict when it arrives.

## 15. What Good Play Sounds Like

For the actual play podcast, good play has specific qualities:

**Describe your actions.** "I attack with my lightsaber" is a dice roll. "I ignite the blade low and come up under his guard, targeting the join between chest plate and pauldron" is a scene. Describe what your character does. The audience can't see the table. They need your words.

**React out loud.** When another player does something cool, dangerous, or unexpected, react in character. "Did you just jump?!" tells the audience that the moment mattered. Silence after a dramatic moment is wasted audio.

**Name your emotions.** "I'm angry" is a statement. "I look at the Mandalorian patrol ship in orbit over the world where my family died and I grip the console until my knuckles go white" is a performance. Give the audience access to what your character feels.

**Ask questions in character.** "What do you mean, the shuttle only seats four?" is exposition delivered through character voice. It's more engaging than waiting for the GM to explain. When information matters, make your character the one who asks for it.

**Lose gracefully.** Failed rolls, accepted compels, and consequences taken are not punishments. They are the moments that make the successes mean something. A character who succeeds at everything is boring to listen to. A character who fails, adapts, and tries again is a character the audience remembers.
